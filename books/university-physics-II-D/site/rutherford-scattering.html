D8.4 Rutherford Scattering: Revealing the Structure of the Atom#

D8.4.1 Motivation: What Is an Atom Made Of?#

At the dawn of the 20th century, the atom was thought to be a simple, diffuse object — a kind of soft blob with positive and negative charge evenly distributed. But was that really true?

To find out, scientists began probing atoms with fast-moving particles. What they discovered would revolutionize atomic physics and lay the groundwork for quantum theory.

The answer came from shooting particles at gold — and what they found changed physics forever.

D8.4.2 Historical Context: The Plum Pudding Model#

After discovering the electron in 1897, J.J. Thomson proposed a new model of the atom to account for both negative and positive charge. Known as the Plum Pudding Model, it was the leading atomic model in the early 1900s.

What the Model Proposed:#

  • Atoms are spheres of diffuse positive charge.

  • Electrons are embedded within this sphere like raisins in a pudding (or plums in a cake).

  • The positive charge was thought to neutralize the negative charge of the electrons.

  • The mass of the atom was assumed to be spread out uniformly.

Why It Made Sense (At the Time):#

  • There was no experimental evidence yet for a concentrated nucleus.

  • Electron scattering experiments lacked the resolution to probe atomic structure.

  • The model predicted that particles like alpha rays would pass through atoms with only slight deflections due to the spread-out positive charge.

The Critical Assumption:#

If an alpha particle (massive and positively charged, essentially a helium nuclei) passed through such an atom:

  • It would experience tiny deflections from the weak electric field of the diffuse positive charge.

  • Large-angle scattering should be extremely rare — or impossible.

This is exactly what Rutherford set out to test — and what the gold foil experiment would ultimately disprove.

D8.4.3 The Gold Foil Experiment (1909)#

Ernest Rutherford, working with Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, devised an experiment to test atomic structure:

Setup:#

  • A source of alpha particles directed at a thin gold foil.

  • A fluorescent screen surrounded the foil to detect scattered particles.

Expectations (if Thomson’s model were correct):#

  • Alpha particles should pass through with minimal deflection, as the positive charge was thought to be spread out.

Shocking Observations#

  1. Most alpha particles did pass straight through, as expected.

  2. Some were deflected at large angles.

  3. A few were even scattered backward — almost straight back!

“It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you.” — Rutherford

D8.4.4 Rutherford’s Interpretation: A Nuclear Atom#

To explain the data, Rutherford proposed a radically different model:

  • The atom contains a small, dense, positively charged nucleus.

  • The nucleus holds nearly all the atom’s mass.

  • Electrons orbit this nucleus at relatively large distances.

  • Most of the atom is empty space, allowing alpha particles to pass through.

This was the birth of the nuclear model of the atom — a direct precursor to the Bohr model and quantum atomic theory.

D8.4.5 Conceptual View of Scattering#

When an alpha particle approaches the gold nucleus:

  • It is repelled by the strong positive charge via the Coulomb force.

  • If it passes close enough, it can be deflected — sometimes at large angles.

  • The closer the encounter, the stronger the deflection.

Far from the nucleus, there’s little to no effect — hence most particles pass straight through.

D8.4.6 Rutherford Scattering Formula#

Rutherford also derived a formula for the number of alpha particles scattered at different angles:

\[ \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{2Ze^2}{4E} \right)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sin^4(\theta/2)} \]

Where:

  • \(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\): differential cross section (probability of scattering into angle \(\theta\))

  • \(Z\): atomic number of target nucleus (e.g., \(Z = 79\) for gold)

  • \(e\): elementary charge

  • \(E\): kinetic energy of the alpha particle

  • \(\theta\): scattering angle

  • \(\varepsilon_0\): vacuum permittivity

Key Features:#

  • Strong angular dependence: scattering probability falls off as \(\sin^{-4}(\theta/2)\)

  • Most particles scatter at small angles

  • Rare but possible large-angle scattering → direct evidence of a compact nucleus

D8.4.7 Significance of the Rutherford Experiment#

✅ Disproved the plum pudding model
✅ Demonstrated that atoms are mostly empty space
✅ Revealed the existence of the nucleus
✅ Laid the groundwork for quantum atomic models

D8.4.8 Modern Applications of Rutherford Scattering#

Although originally done with gold foil and alpha particles, Rutherford’s method evolved into techniques used in:

Nuclear and Particle Physics#

  • Probing the internal structure of protons, neutrons, and nuclei.

  • Using electron or muon scattering instead of alpha particles.

Materials Science#

  • Rutherford Backscattering Spectrometry (RBS) is used to analyze thin films and surface layers with extreme precision.

Astrophysics#

  • High-energy scattering techniques are used in cosmic ray and dark matter detection.

D8.4.9 Summary#

  • The gold foil experiment revealed that atoms have a dense, charged nucleus.

  • Most of the atom is empty space, allowing alpha particles to pass through.

  • The nuclear model replaced the outdated plum pudding model.

  • The mathematical treatment of scattering matched experimental data — supporting the Coulomb force as the underlying interaction.

  • Rutherford’s work directly led to the Bohr model, quantum mechanics, and modern nuclear physics.
