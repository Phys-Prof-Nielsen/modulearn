D8.5 Atomic Spectra: Light from Atoms#

D8.5.1 Motivation: What Does an Atom’s Light Reveal?#

When gases are heated or excited by electricity, they emit light — but not a continuous rainbow. Instead, we see discrete, sharp lines of color. These spectral lines became one of the most precise tools in physics and chemistry.

But why do atoms emit only specific colors of light? What do these lines tell us about the atom?

Studying atomic spectra not only revealed the quantized nature of atomic behavior, but also helped define the structure of the periodic table and identify elements across the universe.

D8.5.2 Historical Background#

In the 1800s, physicists and chemists observed that:

  • Each element emits its own unique pattern of spectral lines when heated.

  • These patterns can be observed using a spectroscope, which separates light by wavelength.

This led to the development of spectroscopy, a powerful tool for probing atomic structure and composition.

Major Discoveries:#

  • Fraunhofer lines (1814): Dark absorption lines in the solar spectrum — first evidence of atomic fingerprints.

  • Bunsen and Kirchhoff (1850s): Showed that elements emit specific lines and that these could be used to identify elements.

  • Balmer (1885): Found a simple mathematical pattern for visible lines in hydrogen — known as the Balmer series.

D8.5.3 What Are Atomic Spectra?#

There are three main types of spectra:

  1. Continuous Spectrum

    • Produced by incandescent solids or dense gases.

    • Contains all wavelengths without interruption (e.g. a heated filament).

  2. Emission Line Spectrum

    • Produced by hot, low-density gases.

    • Contains bright lines at specific wavelengths.

  3. Absorption Line Spectrum

    • Produced when continuous light passes through a cooler gas.

    • Contains dark lines where specific wavelengths are absorbed.

These patterns are like fingerprints for each element.

D8.5.4 The Hydrogen Emission Spectrum#

Hydrogen, the simplest atom, has a surprisingly rich and structured spectrum. When excited, hydrogen emits light only at certain discrete wavelengths — these form what are known as the hydrogen spectral series.

Each series corresponds to electron transitions ending in a particular energy level (\(n_f\)), with the electron falling from a higher energy level (\(n_i > n_f\)).

The Spectral Series of Hydrogen#

The general formula for all hydrogen spectral lines is:

\[ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right), \quad n_i > n_f \]

Where:

  • \(\lambda\) is the emitted wavelength,

  • \(R_H \approx 1.097 \times 10^7 \ \text{m}^{-1}\) is the Rydberg constant,

  • \(n_i\) is the initial (higher) energy level,

  • \(n_f\) is the final (lower) energy level.

The Balmer Series (Visible Light)#

The Balmer series is historically important because it includes lines in the visible spectrum. Johann Balmer (1885) found an empirical formula to fit these lines:

\[ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{n^2} \right), \quad n = 3, 4, 5, \ldots \]

This formula predicted visible hydrogen lines such as:

  • H-alpha (red) — \(n=3 \to 2\) (~656 nm)

  • H-beta (blue-green) — \(n=4 \to 2\) (~486 nm)

  • H-gamma (violet) — \(n=5 \to 2\) (~434 nm)

  • H-delta (deep violet) — \(n=6 \to 2\) (~410 nm)

Balmer’s formula matches the observed spectral lines of hydrogen with astonishing precision — but had no classical explanation at the time.

What These Lines Represent#

Each line in a hydrogen spectrum corresponds to a quantized transition between energy levels. The photon emitted has an energy:

\[ E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]

These transitions:

  • Occur between discrete levels, not continuously

  • Release photons in specific spectral regions (UV, visible, IR)

  • Provide evidence of quantized atomic energy states

Visualization Idea#

If you’d like to visualize this:

  • The Lyman series lies entirely in the ultraviolet

  • The Balmer series is the only one in the visible

  • All other series (Paschen, Brackett, Pfund) are in the infrared

These patterns were known before any theoretical model explained them — and were one of the key clues that led to the Bohr model of the atom.

D8.5.5 Spectral Lines: Key Features#

  • Discrete: Only specific wavelengths are emitted or absorbed.

  • Sharp: The lines are extremely narrow, indicating what we now know as precise energy transitions.

  • Element-Specific: Each element has a unique set of spectral lines.

  • Energy-Wavelength Relationship:

    \[ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]

This means that different lines correspond to specific energy differences within the atom — a hint at quantized energy levels (to be explored later).

D8.5.6 Why Classical Physics Couldn’t Explain It#

Under classical physics:

  • Electrons should spiral into the nucleus, emitting a continuous range of energy.

  • Atoms should radiate at all frequencies, not in discrete lines.

  • There was no mechanism to explain fixed emission wavelengths.

These spectral patterns were among the clearest signs that classical physics was incomplete — and they laid the groundwork for quantum theory.

D8.5.7 Applications of Atomic Spectra#

Element Identification (Spectroscopy)#

  • Used in chemistry, physics, and materials science to determine elemental composition.

Astronomy and Cosmology#

  • Determine the composition of stars and galaxies by analyzing starlight.

  • Identify elements across interstellar space (hydrogen, helium, etc.).

Redshift and Motion#

  • Measure the Doppler shift of spectral lines to determine motion of stars/galaxies.

Lighting Technologies#

  • Neon lights, sodium lamps, and fluorescent tubes work based on gas discharge and spectral emission.

D8.5.8 Summary#

  • Atomic spectra consist of discrete lines, unique to each element.

  • The Balmer formula for hydrogen’s visible lines was a major clue toward quantized energy.

  • Classical physics could not explain why atoms emitted specific wavelengths.

  • These spectra are central to fields from quantum theory to astrophysics.
