D8.2 Blackbody Radiation#

D8.2.1 Motivation: A Crisis at the Edge of the Visible#

At the end of the 19th century, physics was seen as nearly complete. Newtonian mechanics explained motion, Maxwell’s equations governed electromagnetism, and thermodynamics reigned over heat and energy.

But something simple and seemingly innocent—how hot objects glow—turned out to expose a deep inconsistency in classical physics.

Understanding blackbody radiation not only forced a rethinking of fundamental assumptions but also launched the quantum revolution. What began as a problem about the color of heat ultimately revealed that energy is not continuous, but comes in discrete packets.

D8.2.2 What Is a Blackbody?#

A blackbody is an idealized physical object that:

  • Absorbs all incident radiation (no reflection or transmission)

  • Emits radiation only due to its temperature

  • Has a characteristic emission spectrum that depends solely on its temperature

Although no perfect blackbody exists, things like cavities with small holes (used in laboratory experiments) and stars (like our Sun) approximate blackbodies closely.

Everyday Examples#

  • A red-hot piece of metal

  • The filament of an incandescent bulb

  • Cosmic Microwave Background radiation (a nearly perfect blackbody!)

D8.2.3 Historical Context: The Ultraviolet Catastrophe and Wien’s Law#

Physicists attempted to model the spectrum of blackbody radiation using classical electromagnetism and statistical mechanics.

The result?

  • At long wavelengths (low frequencies), predictions matched experiment.

  • At short wavelengths (high frequencies), the predicted intensity diverged to infinity!

This divergence, known as the ultraviolet catastrophe, implied that a blackbody should emit infinite energy, which was clearly unphysical.

Key Figures#

  • Wilhelm Wien (1896): Proposed an empirical formula that fit high-frequency radiation well.

  • Rayleigh and Jeans (1900): Derived a classical formula that worked at low frequencies but diverged at high ones.

  • Max Planck (1900): Introduced the radical idea that energy is quantized, laying the foundation for quantum theory.

Classical Attempt 1: Rayleigh–Jeans Law#

Using classical physics and the equipartition theorem, Rayleigh and Jeans derived the spectral energy density:

\[ u(\nu, T) = \frac{8\pi \nu^2}{c^3} k_B T \]

Where:

  • \(u(\nu, T)\): energy per unit volume per unit frequency

  • \(\nu\): frequency

  • \(T\): temperature

  • \(k_B\): Boltzmann constant

  • \(c\): speed of light

This matches experiment at low frequencies but leads to an unphysical prediction:

As \(\nu \to \infty\), \(u(\nu, T) \to \infty\)
→ Ultraviolet Catastrophe

nu = np.linspace(1e11, 2e15, 1000)

u_nu = (8 * np.pi * nu**2 * k_B * T) / c**3

# Plotting
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
plt.plot(nu, u_nu, label='Rayleigh–Jeans Law', color='red')
plt.title('Ultraviolet Catastrophe: Rayleigh–Jeans Law')
plt.xlabel('Frequency (Hz)')
plt.ylabel(r'Spectral Energy Density $u(\nu, T)$ (J·m⁻³·Hz⁻¹)')
plt.ylim(0, 1e-13)  # adjust as needed to avoid overflow in display
plt.grid(True)
plt.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Classical Attempt 2: Wien’s Law#

Wien proposed an empirical formula (not derived from first principles) that fit experimental data at high frequencies:

\[ u(\nu, T) \propto \nu^3 e^{-\frac{h \nu}{k_B T}} \]

While this matched the short-wavelength tail of the blackbody spectrum, it failed at low frequencies, underestimating the observed radiation.

Both classical attempts captured part of the behavior, but neither could explain the full spectrum.

Wien’s Displacement Law#

From experimental observation, Wien also proposed a relationship between the peak wavelength of radiation and temperature:

\[ \lambda_{\text{peak}} T = b \]

Where:

  • \(\lambda_{\text{peak}}\): wavelength at maximum intensity

  • \(b\): Wien’s constant (\(\approx 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \ \text{m·K}\))

This means: Hotter objects glow bluer.
(The Sun is white; a light bulb filament is yellow; cooler objects are red.)

D8.2.4 Planck’s Radiation Law#

In 1900, Planck introduced a new theoretical model that perfectly matched the blackbody spectrum at all frequencies:

\[ u(\nu, T) = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{\frac{h\nu}{k_B T}} - 1} \]

Where \(h\) is a new fundamental constant, now known as Planck’s constant.

This formula:

  • Approaches the Rayleigh–Jeans law at low frequencies

  • Approaches Wien’s law at high frequencies

  • Fits all experimental data

It was a stunning success — but where did it come from?

D8.2.5 Planck’s Leap: Why Discrete Packets?#

The Longer Story#

By 1900, Planck was determined to find a theoretical derivation of the blackbody spectrum, not just an empirical fit.

He imagined the walls of the cavity as containing tiny oscillators (resonators) that exchange energy with the radiation field.

Using classical statistical mechanics (which he accepted reluctantly), he still could not derive a formula that matched experiment. After much frustration, he tried something radical:

What if energy isn’t continuous, but comes in chunks?

He proposed that the energy of each oscillator could only take on discrete values:

\[ E_n = n h \nu \quad \text{where } n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \]

This meant the oscillators could only emit or absorb energy in packets (quanta) of size \(h\nu\).

Using this assumption, he applied Boltzmann’s statistical methods and arrived at the correct spectrum — what we now call Planck’s law.

Why It Was Radical#

  • Up to that point, energy was assumed to be continuous.

  • Planck’s idea implied a minimum unit of energy for each frequency — a huge conceptual leap.

  • He introduced an entirely new fundamental constant, \(h\).

  • He didn’t fully understand the implications — he saw it as a mathematical trick, not a physical truth.

“An act of desperation.”
— Max Planck, describing his quantization assumption

It was Einstein, in 1905, who later interpreted these energy packets as real particles of light — photons — bringing physical meaning to Planck’s mathematical fix and launching quantum mechanics in earnest.

Make a plot showing all three radiation laws.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Physical constants
h = 6.62607015e-34      # Planck constant (J·s)
k_B = 1.380649e-23      # Boltzmann constant (J/K)
c = 3.0e8               # Speed of light (m/s)
T = 3000                # Temperature in Kelvin

# Frequency range (Hz)
nu = np.linspace(1e11, 2e15, 1000)

# Rayleigh–Jeans Law
u_rj = (8 * np.pi * nu**2 * k_B * T) / c**3

# Wien's Law
u_wien = (8 * np.pi * h * nu**3) / (c**3) * np.exp(-h * nu / (k_B * T))

# Planck's Law
u_planck = (8 * np.pi * h * nu**3) / (c**3 * (np.exp(h * nu / (k_B * T)) - 1))

# Plotting
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(nu, u_rj, label='Rayleigh–Jeans Law', color='red', linestyle='--')
plt.plot(nu, u_wien, label="Wien's Law", color='green', linestyle='-.')
plt.plot(nu, u_planck, label="Planck's Law", color='blue')
plt.title('Blackbody Radiation: Comparing Rayleigh–Jeans, Wien, and Planck')
plt.xlabel('Frequency (Hz)')
plt.ylabel(r'Spectral Energy Density $u(\nu, T)$ (J·m⁻³·Hz⁻¹)')
plt.ylim(0, np.max(u_planck) * 1.1)
plt.grid(True)
plt.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

D8.2.6 Stefan–Boltzmann Law#

Planck’s law, when integrated over all frequencies, leads to the total power radiated per unit area:

\[ P = \sigma T^4 \]

Where:

  • \(\sigma\): Stefan–Boltzmann constant

  • \(T\): absolute temperature

This describes how the total emitted power increases dramatically with temperature — a result that had already been found experimentally but now had a theoretical foundation.

D8.2.7 Why Blackbody Radiation Matters#

Blackbody radiation is not just about glowing objects. It’s about the limits of classical physics and the birth of quantum mechanics.

Key implications:

  • Introduced the idea of energy quantization

  • Explained why atoms don’t radiate energy continuously

  • Set the stage for Einstein’s work on the photoelectric effect

  • Showed how thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and quantum theory intersect

Even today, the blackbody spectrum shows up in:

  • Astrophysics (star spectra, cosmic background radiation)

  • Thermal imaging

  • Climate science

  • Engineering of radiative heat transfer

D8.2.8 Summary#

  • Classical physics failed to explain blackbody radiation at high frequencies.

  • Rayleigh–Jeans and Wien’s laws captured parts of the spectrum, but not the whole.

  • Planck introduced quantized energy levels to derive a formula that matched all data.

  • This quantization was the first step toward quantum mechanics — a turning point in physics history.
