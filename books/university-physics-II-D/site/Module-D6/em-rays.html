D6.2 Electromagnetic Waves as Rays#

D6.2.1 Learning Goals#

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  • Understand that light is an electromagnetic wave.

  • Explain when and why we can approximate light as a ray.

  • Describe key concepts like wavefronts, rays, and the high-frequency limit.

  • Recognize real-world systems where this approximation is valid.

D6.2.2 What Is Light?#

Light is an electromagnetic (EM) wave — a self-sustaining oscillation of electric and magnetic fields traveling through space.

  • It propagates at the speed of light:

    \[ c = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s} \]

  • The electric and magnetic fields are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of motion — a transverse wave.

  • All visible colors, infrared, ultraviolet, X-rays, and radio waves are part of the electromagnetic spectrum.

D6.2.3 Wavefronts and Rays#

  • A wavefront is a surface of constant phase (like the crest of a wave).
    → Think of it as the “leading edge” of the wave.

  • A ray is a line perpendicular to the wavefront, indicating the direction of energy flow.

  • In uniform media, rays travel in straight lines. In non-uniform media, rays bend — this is the basis for refraction.

Key idea: Rays are not physical objects — they are mathematical abstractions that show how energy moves.

D6.2.4 When Can We Treat Light as a Ray?#

We can simplify light as rays when:

  • The wavelength is much smaller than the size of objects it interacts with:

    \[ \lambda \ll L \]

  • There is no significant interference or diffraction.

This is known as the geometrical optics limit or eikonal approximation — a high-frequency limit of wave optics.

Valid scenarios:

  • Lenses and mirrors in imaging systems

  • Laser beams in air

  • Reflection/refraction at flat surfaces

  • Even some interference effects, if we only track the ray paths and consider their phase shifts

Thin film interference will be a ray-based application of wave principles — tracking the paths, angles, and phase changes of reflected rays.

D6.2.5 Rays from Maxwell’s Equations (Without Derivation)#

While we won’t dive into the full Maxwell equations yet, it’s helpful to know this:

  • Maxwell’s equations predict wave solutions for electric and magnetic fields.

  • These fields satisfy the wave equation:

    \[ \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} - \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0 \]

  • In the short-wavelength limit, these wavefronts behave like geometrical surfaces and rays emerge perpendicular to them.

So ray optics is not a separate theory — it’s an approximation of full electromagnetic theory.

D6.2.6 Fermat’s Principle of Least Time#

A foundational idea in ray optics is Fermat’s Principle:

Light takes the path that requires the least time.

This leads to:

  • Straight-line paths in uniform media

  • Snell’s Law in layered materials

  • Reflection and refraction rules that we’ll explore in detail later

This principle is the ray-optics analog of how nature “optimizes” energy and time — and it’s a stepping stone toward variational principles in physics.

D6.2.7 Where Does This Take Us?#

From this foundation, we’ll explore:

  • How light reflects from surfaces

  • How it refracts (bends) when moving between media

  • How to construct optical paths using ray diagrams

  • How lenses, mirrors, prisms, and fiber optics are designed using ray models

Later, in Phase C and A, we’ll return to the full wave nature of light to understand:

  • Diffraction

  • Interference

  • Polarization

  • Waveguides and wavefront engineering

D6.2.8 Summary#

  • Light is an electromagnetic wave, but in many situations, we can treat it as traveling in straight rays.

  • This approximation works when the wavelength is small compared to the system.

  • Rays are perpendicular to wavefronts and represent the direction of energy propagation.

  • This approach allows us to predict and understand reflection, refraction, image formation, and optical design — which are the focus of ray optics.
