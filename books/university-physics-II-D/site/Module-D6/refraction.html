D6.4 Refraction of Light#

D6.4.1 Learning Goals#

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  • Define refraction and explain why it occurs.

  • State and apply Snellâ€™s Law.

  • Construct accurate ray diagrams for light passing through interfaces.

  • Identify everyday examples and consequences of refraction.

D6.4.2 What Is Refraction?#

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium into another with a different optical density (refractive index).

  • Caused by a change in light speed across the boundary.

  • Light bends toward the normal when entering a more optically dense medium.

  • Light bends away from the normal when entering a less dense medium.

The direction of the light ray changes, but its frequency remains constant.

D6.4.3 Snellâ€™s Law#

Snellâ€™s Law quantitatively describes how light bends at an interface:

\[ n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2 \]

Where:

  • \(n_1\): refractive index of medium 1

  • \(n_2\): refractive index of medium 2

  • \(\theta_1\): angle of incidence (from the normal)

  • \(\theta_2\): angle of refraction (from the normal)

The refractive index \(n\) is defined as:

\[ n = \frac{c}{v} \]

where \(c\) is the speed of light in vacuum and \(v\) is the speed in the medium.

D6.4.4 Drawing Ray Diagrams for Refraction#

Steps:

  1. Draw the boundary between two media.

  2. Draw the normal line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence.

  3. Draw the incident ray approaching the boundary.

  4. Use Snellâ€™s Law to estimate the angle of refraction.

  5. Draw the refracted ray, bending appropriately:

    • Toward the normal if \(n_2 > n_1\)

    • Away from the normal if \(n_2 < n_1\)

D6.4.5 Real-World Examples of Refraction#

  • A straw appears bent in a glass of water.

  • Lenses focus light by refraction through curved surfaces.

  • Mirage formation in deserts and on roads.

  • Corrective eyeglasses and contact lenses.

  • Optical fibers guiding light via total internal reflection (related topic).

D6.4.6 Special Case: No Refraction#

If \(\theta_1 = 0^\circ\) (normal incidence), then \(\theta_2 = 0^\circ\) â€” the light ray passes straight through with no change in direction.

Also, if \(n_1 = n_2\), there is no bending regardless of angle.

D6.4.7 Critical Angle and Total Internal Reflection (Preview)#

When light travels from a more dense to a less dense medium (\(n_1 > n_2\)):

  • There exists a critical angle \(\theta_c\) beyond which no refraction occurs.

  • Light is completely reflected back into the medium â€” this is total internal reflection.

D6.4.8 Summary#

  • Refraction is the bending of light due to a change in speed across a boundary.

  • Described by Snellâ€™s Law: \(n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2\).

  • The direction of bending depends on the relative refractive indices.

  • Refraction is foundational in understanding lenses, vision correction, and many optical technologies.

Box 1: Bending Light Through Water

A ray of light passes from air (\(n = 1.00\)) into water (\(n = 1.33\)). The angle of incidence in air is \(45^\circ\).

Tasks:

  1. Use Snellâ€™s Law to calculate the angle of refraction in the water.

  2. Sketch the situation clearly, labeling the incident ray, the normal, and the refracted ray.

  3. Does the light bend toward or away from the normal? Why?

Box 2: Ray Through a Glass Block

A light ray enters a flat, rectangular glass slab (\(n = 1.50\)) from air (\(n = 1.00\)) at an angle of \(30^\circ\) to the normal. It then exits the glass slab back into air through the opposite face.

Tasks:

  1. Calculate the angle of refraction inside the glass.

  2. Determine the angle at which the ray exits into air again.

  3. Sketch the full path of the ray, showing incident, refracted, and emerging rays with correct angles.

  4. Why does the ray emerge parallel to the original incident ray? What causes the lateral displacement?
