D6.7 Thin Lenses#

D6.7.1 Learning Goals#

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

  • Distinguish between converging and diverging lenses.

  • Construct accurate ray diagrams for image formation.

  • Apply the thin lens equation and magnification formula.

  • Understand the role of focal length, object distance, and image distance.

D6.7.2 What Are Thin Lenses?#

A lens is a transparent optical element that bends (refracts) light to form images.

A thin lens is one where the lens thickness is much smaller than the radii of curvature of its surfaces — so we can treat all refraction as occurring at a single plane.

There are two main types:

  • Converging lens (convex): brings parallel rays to a point (focus)

  • Diverging lens (concave): spreads parallel rays away from a point

The focal point is the point at which rays initially parallel to the optical axis converge (for a converging lens) or appear to diverge from (for a diverging lens) after passing through the lens. It represents where the lens focuses light and is located a distance equal to the focal length from the lens.

D6.7.3 Principal Rays and Ray Diagrams#

To construct image diagrams, we use three principal rays:

For a converging lens:#

  1. Parallel ray: enters parallel to the axis, exits through the far focal point.

  2. Central ray: goes through the center of the lens, continues straight.

  3. Focal ray: enters through the near focal point, exits parallel to the axis.

For a diverging lens:#

  1. Parallel ray: enters parallel, exits as if it came from the near focal point.

  2. Central ray: goes through the center, continues straight.

  3. Focal ray: heads toward the far focal point, exits parallel to the axis.

Where the rays intersect (or appear to intersect), the image is formed.

D6.7.4 The Thin Lens Equation#

The relationship between focal length, object distance, and image distance is:

\[ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} \]

Where:

  • \(f\): focal length (positive for converging, negative for diverging)

  • \(d_o\): object distance (positive if object is on the incoming side)

  • \(d_i\): image distance (positive if image is on the outgoing side)

Use sign conventions carefully:

  • Converging lens: \(f > 0\)

  • Diverging lens: \(f < 0\)

  • Real image: \(d_i > 0\)

  • Virtual image: \(d_i < 0\)

D6.7.5 Magnification#

The size and orientation of the image is given by:

\[ M = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} \]

Where:

  • \(M\): magnification

  • \(h_i\), \(h_o\): image and object heights

  • \(M > 0\): image is upright

  • \(M < 0\): image is inverted

If \(|M| > 1\), the image is larger than the object.
If \(|M| < 1\), the image is smaller.

D6.7.6 Real vs. Virtual Images#

  • Real image: formed when rays actually converge; can be projected on a screen.

  • Virtual image: formed when rays appear to diverge from a point; cannot be projected.

Examples:

  • Converging lens: real image when object is outside focal length; virtual if inside.

  • Diverging lens: always forms virtual, upright, smaller image.

D6.7.7 Applications of Thin Lenses#

  • Cameras

  • Microscopes

  • Telescopes

  • Magnifying glasses

  • Eyeglasses and contact lenses

These devices use combinations of converging and diverging lenses to control image formation, size, and position.

D6.7.8 Summary#

  • Converging lenses focus light to a point; diverging lenses spread it out.

  • The thin lens equation relates object distance, image distance, and focal length.

  • Use sign conventions and ray diagrams to determine image type.

  • Magnification tells us how big and in what orientation the image is.
