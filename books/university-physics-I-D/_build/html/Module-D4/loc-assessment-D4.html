Problem 1: Kinetic Energy as a Property of Motion#

LOC addressed: A1, A2, A3, A4

A point particle has mass

\[ m = 1.50\ \text{kg} \]

and velocity

\[ v_x = -12.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. Compute the particle’s kinetic energy.

  2. Explain why the kinetic energy is positive, even though the velocity is negative.

  3. Explain what a change in kinetic energy would indicate physically for this particle.

Problem 1 solution

Kinetic energy in one dimension is defined as

\[ k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2. \]

Substitute the given values:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}(1.50)(12.0^2) = 108\ \text{J}. \]

Interpretation

  • Kinetic energy depends on the square of the speed, not the direction of motion.

  • The velocity is negative because the particle moves in the negative \(x\)-direction, but squaring the speed makes the kinetic energy positive.

  • A change in kinetic energy would indicate that energy has been transferred to or from the particle through work done by an interaction.

\[ \boxed{k = 1.08\times10^{2}\ \text{J}} \]

Problem 2: Work as Energy Transfer in One Dimension#

LOC addressed: B1, B2, B3, B4, C1

A crate of mass

\[ m = 6.00\ \text{kg} \]

slides along a horizontal floor in one dimension.
A constant net force

\[ f_x = -18.0\ \text{N} \]

acts on the crate over a directed displacement

\[ \Delta x = +4.0\ \text{m}. \]

  1. Compute the net work done on the crate.

  2. Determine the change in kinetic energy of the crate.

  3. State whether energy is transferred to or from the crate, and explain why based on the sign of the work.

Problem 2 solution

In one dimension, work done by a constant force is

\[ w = f_x\,\Delta x. \]

Substitute the given values:

\[ w = (-18.0)(+4.0) = -72.0\ \text{J}. \]

By the work–energy theorem,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k. \]

Thus,

\[ \Delta k = -72.0\ \text{J}. \]

Interpretation

  • The work is negative, meaning the force acts opposite the displacement.

  • Energy is therefore transferred from the crate.

  • The crate’s kinetic energy decreases by 72.0 J during this interaction.

\[ \boxed{w = \Delta k = -72.0\ \text{J}} \]

Problem 3: Gravitational Potential Energy and Energy Transfer#

LOC addressed: D1, D2, D3, C1, C3

A ball of mass

\[ m = 2.00\ \text{kg} \]

is lifted vertically upward near Earth’s surface.
The ball is raised from an initial height

\[ y_i = 1.5\ \text{m} \]

to a final height

\[ y_f = 6.0\ \text{m}. \]

Take the gravitational field strength to be

\[ g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

  1. Compute the change in gravitational potential energy of the ball.

  2. Determine the work done by gravity during this process.

  3. Explain the sign relationship between the work done by gravity and the change in gravitational potential energy.

Problem 3 solution

Near Earth’s surface, gravitational potential energy is defined as

\[ u_g = m g y. \]

The change in gravitational potential energy is

\[ \Delta u_g = m g (y_f - y_i). \]

Substitute values:

\[ \Delta u_g = (2.00)(9.8)(6.0 - 1.5) = 88.2\ \text{J}. \]

The work done by gravity is related by

\[ w_g = -\Delta u_g. \]

Therefore,

\[ w_g = -88.2\ \text{J}. \]

Interpretation

  • Gravitational potential energy increases as the ball is lifted.

  • Gravity does negative work, since the gravitational force acts opposite the displacement.

  • Energy is transferred into stored gravitational potential energy.

\[ \boxed{\Delta u_g = +88.2\ \text{J}, \quad w_g = -88.2\ \text{J}} \]

Problem 4: Speed at a Given Height (Straight Up, No Air Resistance)#

LOC addressed: C1, C3, D1, D2, E1, E4

A ball of mass

\[ m = 0.400\ \text{kg} \]

is tossed straight upward from height

\[ y_i = 1.20\ \text{m} \]

with initial speed

\[ v_i = 16.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Ignore air resistance and use

\[ g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Before the ball reaches its peak, it passes the height

\[ y = 6.00\ \text{m}. \]

  1. Assuming mechanical energy is conserved, find the speed of the ball at \(y=6.00\) m.

  2. Briefly explain why the speed found corresponds to the upward part of the motion (before the peak).

Problem 4 solution

With no non-conservative forces, mechanical energy is conserved:

\[ k_i + u_i = k + u. \]

Using

\[ k = \frac12 m v^2, \qquad u_g = mgy, \]

we write

\[ \frac12 m v_i^2 + mgy_i = \frac12 m v^2 + mgy. \]

Mass cancels, and solving for \(v\) gives

\[ v = \sqrt{v_i^2 - 2g(y-y_i)}. \]

Substitute:

\[ v = \sqrt{(16.0)^2 - 2(9.80)(6.00-1.20)} = \sqrt{256 - 94.08} = \sqrt{161.92} \approx 12.7\ \text{m/s}. \]

This is the speed at \(y=6.00\) m.
That height occurs once on the way up and once on the way down, but the speed magnitude is the same at that height (in the absence of air resistance). Since the problem states “before the peak,” we interpret this as the upward pass.

\[ \boxed{v \approx 12.7\ \text{m/s}} \]

# Problem 4 — Speed at a Given Height (Symbolic + Numeric)

import sympy as sp
from IPython.display import display, Math

# Symbols
g, y_i, y, v_i, v = sp.symbols('g y_i y v_i v', positive=True)

# Mechanical energy relation (mass cancels)
# (1/2)v_i^2 + g y_i = (1/2)v^2 + g y
eq = sp.Eq(sp.Rational(1, 2)*v_i**2 + g*y_i, sp.Rational(1, 2)*v**2 + g*y)

# Solve for v (speed = positive root)
sol_v = sp.solve(eq, v)[0]

# Numerical values
vals = {
    g: 9.80,
    y_i: 1.20,
    y: 6.00,
    v_i: 16.0
}

v_val = sp.N(sol_v.subs(vals))

# Display math
display(Math(r"\frac12 v_i^2 + g y_i = \frac12 v^2 + g y"))
display(Math(r"v = " + sp.latex(sol_v)))
display(Math(r"v = " + f"{float(v_val):.2e}" + r"\ \text{m/s}"))

Problem 5: Mechanical Energy Changes Due to a Non-Conservative Force#

LOC addressed: E1, E2, E3, D1, D2, C1, C3

A 1D cart of mass

\[ m = 2.50\ \text{kg} \]

moves along a straight track. The cart starts at height

\[ y_i = 4.00\ \text{m} \]

with speed

\[ v_i = 0.00\ \text{m/s}. \]

It travels down to a lower height

\[ y_f = 1.20\ \text{m}. \]

During this motion, air drag does a known amount of non-conservative work on the cart:

\[ w_{nc} = -12.0\ \text{J}. \]

Use

\[ g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

  1. Write the mechanical-energy form of the work–energy theorem for this situation.

  2. Find the cart’s final speed at \(y_f\).

  3. Compare the result to the case with no air drag: should the final speed be larger or smaller? Explain briefly.

Problem 5 solution

Mechanical energy is

\[ e_{\text{mech}} = k + u_g. \]

The mechanical-energy form of the work–energy theorem is

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc}. \]

So,

\[ (k_f + u_f) - (k_i + u_i) = w_{nc}. \]

Substitute \(k_i=0\) and \(u_g=mgy\):

\[ \left(\frac12 m v_f^2 + mg y_f\right) - \left(0 + mg y_i\right) = w_{nc}. \]

Solve for \(v_f\):

\[ \frac12 m v_f^2 = mg(y_i-y_f) + w_{nc} \]
\[ v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2}{m}\left[m g(y_i-y_f) + w_{nc}\right]}. \]

Because \(w_{nc}\) is negative, the final speed is smaller than it would be with no air drag.

# DIY Cell

Show code cell source

Hide code cell source

 
%reset -f

# Problem 5 — Mechanical Energy with Non-Conservative Work (Symbolic + Numeric)

import sympy as sp
from IPython.display import display, Math

# Symbols
m, g, y_i, y_f, w_nc, v_f = sp.symbols('m g y_i y_f w_nc v_f', positive=True)

# Energies
k_i = 0
u_i = m * g * y_i
k_f = sp.Rational(1, 2) * m * v_f**2
u_f = m * g * y_f

# Mechanical-energy work–energy theorem
eq = sp.Eq((k_f + u_f) - (k_i + u_i), w_nc)

# Solve for v_f (positive root)
sol_vf = sp.solve(eq, v_f)[0]

# Numerical values
vals = {
    m: 2.50,
    g: 9.80,
    y_i: 4.00,
    y_f: 1.20,
    w_nc: -12.0
}

vf_val = sp.N(sol_vf.subs(vals))

# Display math
display(Math(r"\Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc}"))
display(Math(r"\left(\frac12 m v_f^2 + mgy_f\right) - \left(mgy_i\right) = w_{nc}"))
display(Math(r"v_f = " + sp.latex(sol_vf)))

# Numerical result
display(Math(r"v_f = " + f"{float(vf_val):.3e}" + r"\ \text{m/s}"))

\[\displaystyle \left(\frac12 m v_f^2 + mgy_f\right) - \left(mgy_i\right) = w_{nc}\]

Problem 6: Power from Energy Transfer#

LOC addressed: F1, F2, F3, B1, C1

A motor transfers energy to a 1D cart by doing net work on it.
The cart’s kinetic energy increases from

\[ k_i = 120\ \text{J} \]

to

\[ k_f = 680\ \text{J} \]

over a time interval

\[ \Delta t = 4.0\ \text{s}. \]

  1. Compute the net work done on the cart.

  2. Compute the average power delivered to the cart.

  3. Explain what the power value tells you physically (in words).

Problem 6 solution

From the work–energy theorem,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = k_f - k_i. \]

So,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = 680 - 120 = 560\ \text{J}. \]

Average power is the rate of energy transfer:

\[ p_{\text{avg}} = \frac{w_{\text{net}}}{\Delta t} = \frac{560}{4.0} = 140\ \text{W}. \]

Interpretation:
A power of 140 W means the motor transfers energy to the cart at an average rate of 140 joules every second.

\[ \boxed{w_{\text{net}} = 560\ \text{J}, \quad p_{\text{avg}} = 140\ \text{W}} \]

Show code cell source

Hide code cell source

 
%reset -f

# Problem 6 — Power from Energy Transfer (Symbolic + Numeric)

import sympy as sp
from IPython.display import display, Math

# Symbols
k_i, k_f, dt = sp.symbols('k_i k_f dt', positive=True)

# Work-energy theorem
w_net = k_f - k_i

# Average power
p_avg = sp.simplify(w_net / dt)

# Numerical values
vals = {
    k_i: 120,
    k_f: 680,
    dt: 4.0
}

w_val = sp.N(w_net.subs(vals))
p_val = sp.N(p_avg.subs(vals))

# Display math
display(Math(r"w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = k_f - k_i"))
display(Math(r"w_{\text{net}} = " + sp.latex(w_net)))
display(Math(r"p_{\text{avg}} = \frac{w_{\text{net}}}{\Delta t} = " + sp.latex(p_avg)))

# Numerical results
display(Math(r"w_{\text{net}} = " + f"{float(w_val):.3e}" + r"\ \text{J}"))
display(Math(r"p_{\text{avg}} = " + f"{float(p_val):.3e}" + r"\ \text{W}"))

Problem 7: Work from a Force–Position Graph and Final Speed#

LOC addressed: B1, B2, B4, C1, C3

A 1D cart of mass

\[ m = 3.00\ \text{kg} \]

moves along the +x direction on a track. A net force acts on the cart as a function of position.
The measured force data are:

Position \(x\) (m)

0.00

0.20

0.40

0.60

0.80

1.00

Net Force \(F\) (N)

0.0

10.0

18.0

16.0

8.0

0.0

Assume the cart starts from rest at \(x=0\).

  1. Plot \(F\) vs. \(x\).

  2. Use a trapezoidal-sum (area under the curve) to estimate the net work done from \(x=0\) to \(x=1.00\) m.

  3. Use the work–energy theorem to find the final speed \(v_f\) at \(x=1.00\) m.

Problem 7 solution

The net work is the area under the force–position curve:

\[ w_{\text{net}} \approx \sum \frac{F_i + F_{i+1}}{2}\,\Delta x. \]

Then apply the work–energy theorem (starting from rest):

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = \frac12 m v_f^2, \qquad v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2w_{\text{net}}}{m}}. \]

The Python script below computes the trapezoidal work and then the final speed.

# DIY Cell

Show code cell source

Hide code cell source

 
# Problem 7 — Work from F(x) graph (trapezoid) and final speed

import sympy as sp
from IPython.display import display, Math

# -----------------------------
# Data
# -----------------------------
x_data = [0.00, 0.20, 0.40, 0.60, 0.80, 1.00]   # m
F_data = [0.0, 10.0, 18.0, 16.0, 8.0, 0.0]      # N

# Symbols
m, W, v_f = sp.symbols('m w v_f', positive=True)

# Trapezoidal work (symbolic sum evaluated numerically)
W_num = 0.0
for i in range(len(x_data) - 1):
    dx = x_data[i+1] - x_data[i]
    W_num += 0.5 * (F_data[i] + F_data[i+1]) * dx

# Work-energy theorem from rest: W = (1/2) m v_f^2
eq = sp.Eq(W, sp.Rational(1, 2) * m * v_f**2)

# Solve for v_f
sol_vf = sp.solve(eq, v_f)[0]  

# Substitute numerical values
m_val = 3.00
vf_val = sp.N(sol_vf.subs({W: W_num, m: m_val}))

# -----------------------------
# Plot (matplotlib) — simple and clear
# -----------------------------
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

plt.figure()
plt.plot(x_data, F_data, marker='o')
plt.xlabel("x (m)")
plt.ylabel("F (N)")
plt.title("Net Force vs Position")
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

# -----------------------------
# Display math + results
# -----------------------------
display(Math(r"w_{\text{net}} \approx \sum \frac{F_i + F_{i+1}}{2}\,\Delta x"))
display(Math(r"w_{\text{net}} = " + f"{W_num:.1e}" + r"\ \text{J}"))

display(Math(r"w_{\text{net}} = \frac12 m v_f^2"))
display(Math(r"v_f = " + sp.latex(sol_vf)))

display(Math(r"v_f = " + f"{float(vf_val):.1e}" + r"\ \text{m/s}"))
