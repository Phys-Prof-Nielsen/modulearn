Problem 1 ‚Äî Work, Kinetic Energy, and Motion#

A 5.0 kg box is pushed across a horizontal surface by a constant net force of

\[ f_x = +235\ \text{N}. \]

The box starts from rest at

\[ x = 0\ \text{m}. \]

After being pushed a distance of

\[ \Delta x = 45\ \text{m}, \]

answer the following:

  1. How much work was done on the box?

  2. What is the kinetic energy at \(x = 45\ \text{m}\)?

  3. How fast is the box traveling at \(x = 45\ \text{m}\)?

  4. What is the velocity of the box at \(x = 45\ \text{m}\)?

Be careful with units, signs, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Work by a constant force in one dimension:

\[ w = f_x\,\Delta x \]

Work‚Äìenergy theorem:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \]

Kinetic energy:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Work done on the box

Given

  • \(f_x = +235\ \text{N}\)

  • \(\Delta x = +45\ \text{m}\)

Compute work

\[ w = (235)(45) = 1.0575\times10^4\ \text{J} \]

With proper significant figures:

\[ \boxed{w \approx 1.1\times10^4\ \text{J}} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Kinetic energy at 45 m

The box starts from rest, so

\[ k_i = 0. \]

Using the work‚Äìenergy theorem,

\[ k_f = k_i + w = w. \]

Thus,

\[ \boxed{k_f \approx 1.1\times10^4\ \text{J}} \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Speed at 45 m

Use the definition of kinetic energy:

\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. \]

Solve for speed:

\[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2k_f}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(1.0575\times10^4)}{5.0}} \]
\[ v \approx 65\ \text{m/s}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{v \approx 65\ \text{m/s}} \]

Part 4 ‚Äî Velocity at 45 m

The force acts in the positive \(x\)-direction, and the box never reverses direction.
Therefore, the velocity has the same sign as the motion.

\[ \boxed{v_x \approx +65\ \text{m/s}} \]

*Final Summary

  • Positive work increases kinetic energy.

  • Because the net force and displacement are in the same direction, the work is positive.

  • The work‚Äìenergy theorem allows us to determine the speed without using acceleration or time.

Problem 2 ‚Äî Gravitational Work and Energy#

During a pumpkin drop, a 9.5 kg pumpkin is dropped from the roof of a 35 m building.
The force exerted by gravity on the pumpkin is

\[ f_g = mg, \]

where

\[ g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Ignore air resistance.

The pumpkin is thrown downward with an initial speed of

\[ v_i = 25\ \text{m/s}. \]

How fast is the pumpkin traveling just before impact with the ground?

Be careful with signs, units, and physical interpretation.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Kinetic energy:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Gravitational potential energy (near Earth):

\[ u_g = mgy \]

Mechanical energy conservation (no non-conservative forces):

\[ k_i + u_i = k_f + u_f \]

Step 1 ‚Äî Choose a reference and write energies

Let the ground be \(y = 0\).

Initial state (roof):

  • Height: \(y_i = 35\ \text{m}\)

  • Speed: \(v_i = 25\ \text{m/s}\)

Initial kinetic energy:

\[ k_i = \frac{1}{2}(9.5)(25)^2 = 2.97\times10^3\ \text{J}. \]

Initial gravitational potential energy:

\[ u_i = (9.5)(9.8)(35) = 3.26\times10^3\ \text{J}. \]

Step 2 ‚Äî Final energy at the ground

At impact:

  • \(y_f = 0 \Rightarrow u_f = 0\)

Final mechanical energy is purely kinetic:

\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2. \]

Step 3 ‚Äî Apply mechanical energy conservation

\[ k_i + u_i = k_f. \]

Substitute values:

\[ 2.97\times10^3 + 3.26\times10^3 = \frac{1}{2}(9.5)v_f^2. \]
\[ 6.23\times10^3 = 4.75\,v_f^2. \]

Solve for \(v_f\):

\[ v_f^2 = 1.31\times10^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_f \approx 36\ \text{m/s}. \]

Answer

\[ \boxed{v_f \approx 36\ \text{m/s}} \]

Interpretation

  • Both the initial kinetic energy and the gravitational potential energy contribute to the final speed.

  • Gravity transfers energy from potential to kinetic, increasing the pumpkin‚Äôs speed.

  • Mechanical energy is conserved because no non-conservative forces act.

Problem 3 ‚Äî Mechanical Energy in One Dimension (Vertical Motion)#

A 3.0 kg object moves only in the vertical (\(y\)) direction.
The object is initially at rest at height

\[ y_i = 1.5\ \text{m}. \]

A constant upward force of magnitude

\[ f_y = +40.o\ \text{N} \]

acts on the object over a vertical displacement of

\[ \Delta y = +2.0\ \text{m}. \]

Assume:

  • gravity acts downward with \(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\),

  • air resistance is negligible.

Answer the following:

  1. How much work is done by the applied force?

  2. What is the change in gravitational potential energy of the object?

  3. What is the final kinetic energy of the object?

  4. How fast is the object moving at the end of the displacement?

Be careful with units, signs, and energy accounting.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Work by a constant force:

\[ w = f_y\,\Delta y \]

Gravitational potential energy:

\[ \Delta u_g = mg\,\Delta y \]

Mechanical energy form of the work‚Äìenergy theorem:

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc} \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Work done by the applied force

\[ w_{\text{app}} = (40)(2.0) = 80\ \text{J}. \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Change in gravitational potential energy

\[ \Delta u_g = (3.0)(9.8)(2.0) = 58.8\ \text{J}. \]

The potential energy increases because the object moves upward.

Part 3 ‚Äî Final kinetic energy

The object starts from rest, so

\[ k_i = 0. \]

Mechanical energy accounting gives

\[ k_f + \Delta u_g = w_{\text{app}}. \]

Thus,

\[ k_f = 80 - 58.8 = 21.2\ \text{J}. \]

Part 4 ‚Äî Final speed

Using

\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv^2, \]

we solve for \(v\):

\[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2k_f}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(21.2)}{3.0}} \approx 3.8\ \text{m/s}. \]

Final Answers

  • Work done by the applied force:

    \[ \boxed{w_{\text{app}} = 80\ \text{J}} \]

  • Change in gravitational potential energy:

    \[ \boxed{\Delta u_g = +58.8\ \text{J}} \]

  • Final kinetic energy:

    \[ \boxed{k_f = 21.2\ \text{J}} \]

  • Final speed:

    \[ \boxed{v \approx 3.8\ \text{m/s}} \]

Interpretation

  • The applied force is non-conservative and adds mechanical energy.

  • Gravity is conservative and stores part of that energy as gravitational potential energy.

  • The remaining energy appears as kinetic energy.

Problem 4 ‚Äî Mechanical Energy with Friction (1D Horizontal)#

A 6.0 kg crate moves only in the \(+x\) direction along a horizontal floor.
The crate has an initial speed of

\[ v_i = 8.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

A constant kinetic friction force of magnitude

\[ f_{\text{fric}} = 15\ \text{N} \]

acts on the crate opposite its motion. The crate slides a distance

\[ \Delta x = +12\ \text{m}. \]

Assume:

  • the crate does not change height, so \(\Delta u_g = 0\),

  • friction is the only non-conservative force acting.

Answer the following:

  1. What is the work done by friction?

  2. What is the change in mechanical energy of the crate?

  3. What is the crate‚Äôs final kinetic energy?

  4. How fast is the crate moving after sliding 12 m?

Be careful with units, signs, and energy accounting.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Kinetic energy:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Work by a constant force:

\[ w = f_x\,\Delta x \]

Mechanical energy form of the theorem (with \(\Delta u = 0\) here):

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \Delta k = w_{nc} \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Work done by friction

Friction acts opposite the displacement, so its force is negative:

\[ f_x = -15\ \text{N}. \]

Work by friction:

\[ w_{\text{fric}} = f_x\,\Delta x = (-15)(12) = -180\ \text{J}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{w_{\text{fric}} = -1.8\times10^2\ \text{J}} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Change in mechanical energy

Since height does not change, \(e_{\text{mech}} = k\) and

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc} = w_{\text{fric}}. \]

So

\[ \boxed{\Delta e_{\text{mech}} = -1.8\times10^2\ \text{J}} \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Final kinetic energy

Initial kinetic energy:

\[ k_i = \frac{1}{2}(6.0)(8.0)^2 = 3.0(64) = 192\ \text{J}. \]

Apply \(\Delta k = w_{\text{fric}}\):

\[ k_f = k_i + w_{\text{fric}} = 192 - 180 = 12\ \text{J}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{k_f = 1.2\times10^1\ \text{J}} \]

Part 4 ‚Äî Final speed

\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2k_f}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(12)}{6.0}} = \sqrt{4} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{v_f = 2.0\ \text{m/s}} \]

Interpretation

  • Friction does negative work, removing mechanical energy.

  • The crate slows down significantly because most of its initial kinetic energy is transferred out of mechanical energy.

Problem 5 ‚Äî 1D Elastic Collision#

A 0.80 kg cart moves in the \(+x\) direction with speed

\[ v_{1,i} = +5.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

It collides in a perfectly elastic one-dimensional collision with a second cart of mass

\[ m_2 = 1.20\ \text{kg} \]

that is initially at rest:

\[ v_{2,i} = 0. \]

Find the final velocities \(v_{1,f}\) and \(v_{2,f}\).

Be careful with units, signs, and the meaning of an elastic collision.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Momentum conservation:

\[ P_i = P_f \quad \Rightarrow \quad m_1 v_{1,i} + m_2 v_{2,i} = m_1 v_{1,f} + m_2 v_{2,f} \]

Kinetic energy conservation (elastic collision):

\[ K_i = K_f \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1,i}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2,i}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1,f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2,f}^2 \]

Given

  • \(m_1 = 0.80\ \text{kg}\)

  • \(m_2 = 1.20\ \text{kg}\)

  • \(v_{1,i} = +5.0\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(v_{2,i} = 0\)

Step 1 ‚Äî Momentum conservation

\[ (0.80)(5.0) + (1.20)(0) = (0.80)v_{1,f} + (1.20)v_{2,f} \]
\[ 4.0 = 0.80v_{1,f} + 1.20v_{2,f}. \tag{1} \]

Step 2 ‚Äî Kinetic energy conservation

\[ \frac{1}{2}(0.80)(5.0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.80)v_{1,f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}(1.20)v_{2,f}^2 \]

Compute the left side:

\[ \frac{1}{2}(0.80)(25) = 10.0\ \text{J}. \]

So

\[ 10.0 = 0.40v_{1,f}^2 + 0.60v_{2,f}^2. \tag{2} \]

Step 3 ‚Äî Solve the system

From (1):

\[ 0.80v_{1,f} = 4.0 - 1.20v_{2,f} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_{1,f} = 5.0 - 1.5v_{2,f}. \]

Substitute into (2):

\[ 10.0 = 0.40(5.0 - 1.5v_{2,f})^2 + 0.60v_{2,f}^2. \]

Solving gives

\[ v_{2,f} = 4.0\ \text{m/s} \]

and therefore

\[ v_{1,f} = 5.0 - 1.5(4.0) = -1.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Answers

\[ \boxed{v_{1,f} = -1.0\ \text{m/s}} \qquad \boxed{v_{2,f} = +4.0\ \text{m/s}} \]

Interpretation

  • Cart 1 reverses direction after the collision.

  • Cart 2 moves forward with a larger speed.

  • Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved, confirming the collision is elastic.

%reset -f

import sympy as sp

# Given values
m1 = 0.80
m2 = 1.20
v1_i = 5.0
v2_i = 0.0

# Unknown final velocities
v1_f, v2_f = sp.symbols('v1_f v2_f')

# Initial and final momentum
p_i = m1*v1_i + m2*v2_i
p_f = m1*v1_f + m2*v2_f

# Initial and final kinetic energy
ke_i = 0.5*m1*v1_i**2 + 0.5*m2*v2_i**2
ke_f = 0.5*m1*v1_f**2 + 0.5*m2*v2_f**2

# Conservation equations
eq_momentum = sp.Eq(p_i, p_f)
eq_energy   = sp.Eq(ke_i, ke_f)

# Solve the system (as dictionaries)
solutions = sp.solve((eq_momentum, eq_energy), (v1_f, v2_f), dict=True)

# Select the physically meaningful solution (discard the trivial no-interaction solution)
physical_solution = [sol for sol in solutions if sol[v2_f] != 0][0]

# Extract numerical values
v1f_val = float(physical_solution[v1_f])
v2f_val = float(physical_solution[v2_f])

# Output results in scientific notation
print(f"Final velocity of cart 1: {v1f_val:.1e} m/s")
print(f"Final velocity of cart 2: {v2f_val:.1e} m/s")

Problem D6 ‚Äî Work and Power from Speed Change#

A 1200 kg car speeds up on a straight, horizontal road (no change in height).
Its speed increases from

\[ v_i = 12\ \text{m/s} \]

to

\[ v_f = 28\ \text{m/s} \]

over a time interval of

\[ \Delta t = 6.0\ \text{s}. \]

Assume that all the energy transfer into the car‚Äôs motion is due to the net work done on the car, and ignore air resistance and rolling losses.

  1. Find the net work done on the car during this interval.

  2. Find the average power delivered during this interval.

Be mindful of units, significant figures, and interpretation.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Work‚Äìenergy theorem:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \]

Kinetic energy:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Average power:

\[ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{w}{\Delta t} \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Net work from change in speed

Compute the change in kinetic energy:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = \frac{1}{2}m(v_f^2 - v_i^2). \]

Substitute values:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \frac{1}{2}(1200)\left(28^2 - 12^2\right). \]

Compute inside parentheses:

\[ 28^2 = 784,\qquad 12^2 = 144,\qquad 784 - 144 = 640. \]

So

\[ w_{\text{net}} = 600(640) = 3.84\times10^5\ \text{J}. \]

With appropriate significant figures:

\[ \boxed{w_{\text{net}} \approx 3.8\times10^5\ \text{J}} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Average power

\[ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{w_{\text{net}}}{\Delta t} = \frac{3.84\times10^5}{6.0} = 6.40\times10^4\ \text{W}. \]

With appropriate significant figures:

\[ \boxed{P_{\text{avg}} \approx 6.4\times10^4\ \text{W}} \]

Interpretation

  • The positive work corresponds to an increase in kinetic energy (the car speeds up).

  • Power describes how quickly that energy transfer happens.

  • A power of \(6.4\times10^4\ \text{W}\) is about \(64\ \text{kW}\).

Work‚Äìenergy theorem:

\[ w_T = F_T d_1. \]

Starting from rest:

\[ m = 3.00\ \text{kg}, \qquad x_{\max} = 0.60\ \text{m}. \]

Answer the following:

  1. Make a clear plot of \(F\) vs. \(x\) (markers connected by a line). Include axis labels with units.

  2. Compute the work done from \(0 \le x \le x_{\max}\) using a trapezoidal sum. Report \(w\) in joules.

  3. If the cart starts from rest (\(v_i = 0\)), compute the final speed \(v_f\) at \(x_{\max}\).

  4. If instead the desired final speed is \(v_f = 4.00\ \text{m/s}\) at \(x_{\max}\), compute the required initial speed \(v_i\).

Be careful with units, interpretation of area, and energy accounting.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Work from a force‚Äìposition graph (area under the curve):

\[ w \approx \sum \frac{(F_i + F_{i+1})}{2}\,\Delta x \]

Work‚Äìenergy theorem:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \]

Kinetic energy:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Plot

A correct plot shows the given data points with markers and a connecting line, with:

  • horizontal axis labeled \(x\) (m)

  • vertical axis labeled \(F\) (N)

Part 2 ‚Äî Trapezoidal estimate of work

Here \(\Delta x = 0.10\ \text{m}\) between each data point.

Compute the trapezoids:

\[ w \approx \sum_{i=0}^{5} \frac{(F_i + F_{i+1})}{2}(0.10). \]

Using the table values:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} w &\approx 0.10\left[\frac{0.0+12.0}{2}+\frac{12.0+18.0}{2}+\frac{18.0+22.0}{2}+\frac{22.0+20.0}{2}+\frac{20.0+15.0}{2}+\frac{15.0+10.0}{2}\right] \\ &= 0.10\left[6.0 + 15.0 + 20.0 + 21.0 + 17.5 + 12.5\right] \\ &= 0.10(92.0) \\ &= 9.20\ \text{J}. \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

So

\[ \boxed{w \approx 9.20\ \text{J}}. \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Final speed from rest

If \(v_i = 0\), then

\[ w = \Delta k = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2. \]

Solve for \(v_f\):

\[ v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2w}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(9.20)}{3.00}} \approx 2.48\ \text{m/s}. \]

So

\[ \boxed{v_f \approx 2.48\ \text{m/s}}. \]

Part 4 ‚Äî Required initial speed for a desired final speed

Use

\[ w = \frac{1}{2}m(v_f^2 - v_i^2). \]

Solve for \(v_i\):

\[ v_i = \sqrt{v_f^2 - \frac{2w}{m}}. \]

With \(v_f = 4.00\ \text{m/s}\):

\[ v_i = \sqrt{(4.00)^2 - \frac{2(9.20)}{3.00}} = \sqrt{16.0 - 6.13} \approx 3.14\ \text{m/s}. \]

So

\[ \boxed{v_i \approx 3.14\ \text{m/s}}. \]

Interpretation

  • The work is the area under the \(F\)‚Äì\(x\) curve.

  • Positive work increases kinetic energy.

  • The work‚Äìenergy theorem connects the area directly to changes in speed.

\[ w \approx \sum_{i=0}^{5} \frac{(F_i + F_{i+1})}{2}(50). \]
