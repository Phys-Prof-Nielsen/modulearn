D4.1 Kinetic Energy#

D4.1.1 Concept of Energy#

Before introducing kinetic energy, we first need a clear conceptual understanding of what physicists mean by energy.

At its most basic level, energy is not something we see directly—it is a bookkeeping quantity that helps us track how physical systems change through interactions.

Concept

Energy is the ability to do work.

This definition may sound abstract at first, but it becomes meaningful when we look at how energy appears, moves, and changes form in real situations.

Some simple examples include:

  • Sliding across a hardwood gym floor
    The energy of motion (kinetic energy) of a person is gradually converted into thermal energy due to friction. This energy transfer increases the temperature of both the floor and the skin, sometimes enough to cause a burn.

  • Wind turning a turbine blade
    The energy of motion (kinetic energy) of the wind is transferred to the blade, giving it kinetic energy as well. The turbine then converts this energy into electrical energy.

  • Water stored in a reservoir above a turbine
    The water has energy of position (gravitational potential energy). As the water flows downward under gravity, this stored energy is converted into energy of motion, which is then transferred into electrical energy by the turbine.

In this module, we will see that kinetic energy is by far the simplest form of energy to calculate directly.
In contrast, potential energy depends on the details of the interaction and can take many different forms—some simple, some quite complex.

D4.1.2 Kinetic Energy: Energy of Motion#

For a point particle moving in one dimension, we define the linear kinetic energy as:

Kinetic Energy of a Point Particle

\[ k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \tag{1} \]

Here, \(v\) represents the speed of the particle. Because speed is always non-negative, kinetic energy does not depend on direction.
Later in the course (Phase C), we will revisit this expression and write kinetic energy in terms of the velocity of the particle in a more general framework.

Several important observations follow directly from this definition:

  1. Kinetic energy is always non-negative.
    Since it depends on \(v^2\), reversing direction does not change the kinetic energy.

  2. Kinetic energy depends on the observer.
    Two observers moving relative to each other (in inertial reference frames) will generally measure different speeds for the same particle and therefore assign different kinetic energies.
    This suggests that, as with momentum, the absolute value of kinetic energy is less important than how it changes during an interaction.

  3. Kinetic energy scales differently with mass and speed.

    • Doubling the mass \(\rightarrow\) doubles the kinetic energy

    • Doubling the speed \(\rightarrow\) quadruples the kinetic energy

    This strong dependence on speed explains why stopping distance for a car increases by roughly a factor of four when the speed doubles.

Unit of Kinetic Energy#

From the definition,

\[ [k] = \text{kg}\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2, \]

which is defined as the joule (J).

Historical Context#

Historical Perspective: Momentum vs. Kinetic Energy

Earlier in this course, we defined motion using momentum,
$\( p = m v, \)$ which emphasizes how difficult it is to change an object’s state of motion through interactions.

In this module, we introduce kinetic energy,
$\( k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2, \)$ as a different—but equally valid—way of quantifying motion.

This distinction has deep historical roots. In the late 1600s, Newton formulated the laws of motion using momentum as the central quantity. Momentum fit naturally with forces and interactions and became the foundation of classical mechanics.

At the same time, other scientists—notably Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz—argued that motion should instead be measured using a quantity proportional to \(m v^2\), which he called vis viva (“living force”). This quantity is what we now recognize as kinetic energy.

For many decades, there was debate over which description was more “fundamental.” Modern physics shows that both viewpoints are correct, but they answer different questions:

  • Momentum tracks how motion is transferred between objects during interactions.

  • Kinetic energy tracks how motion changes through work and energy transfer.

In short, motion can be described consistently using either momentum or kinetic energy.
The choice depends not on which is “right,” but on which perspective makes the physics clearer for the problem at hand.

Example 1 – Kinetic Energy of a Race Car

Problem

A 798 kg Haas F1 race car is capable of reaching a top speed of about 220 mph on straight segments of a race track.
How much kinetic energy does the car have at that speed?



Solution

First, convert the speed to SI units.

\[ 220~\text{mph} = 98.35~\text{m/s} \]

The speed is given to two significant figures, but we will carry extra digits through the calculation and round only at the end.

Using the definition of kinetic energy,

\[ k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2, \]

we find

\[ k = (0.5)(798)(98.35)^2 = 3.859 \times 10^6~\text{J}. \]

Answer

Rounded to the appropriate number of significant figures, the kinetic energy of the car is

\[ \boxed{3.9 \times 10^6~\text{J}}. \]

Box Activity 1 – Kinetic Energy and Speed for a Point Particle

In this activity, you will explore kinetic energy as a scalar measure of motion for a point particle.
The focus is on how kinetic energy depends on speed, not direction.

Recall the definition of linear kinetic energy for a point particle:

\[ k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \]

Task: Complete the following steps.

  1. Choose a particle mass
    Let the particle have a mass of

    \[ m = 2.0\ \text{kg}. \]

  2. Compute the kinetic energy at an initial speed
    Suppose the particle moves with a speed

    \[ v = 3.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

    Calculate the kinetic energy \(k\).

  3. Vary the speed
    Now repeat the calculation for the following speeds while keeping the mass fixed:

    • \(v = 6.0\ \text{m/s}\)

    • \(v = 9.0\ \text{m/s}\)

  4. Compare results

    • How does the kinetic energy change when the speed doubles?

    • How does it change when the speed triples?

  5. Reflect

    • Does reversing the direction of motion change the kinetic energy?

    • Why does kinetic energy depend on \(v^2\) rather than \(v\)?

A Possible Solution Guide

Step 2: Initial kinetic energy

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(3.0)^2 = 9.0\ \text{J} \]

Step 3: Increased speeds

  • For \(v = 6.0\ \text{m/s}\):

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(6.0)^2 = 36\ \text{J} \]

  • For \(v = 9.0\ \text{m/s}\):

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(9.0)^2 = 81\ \text{J} \]

Observations

  • Doubling the speed increases the kinetic energy by a factor of four.

  • Tripling the speed increases the kinetic energy by a factor of nine.

  • Reversing direction does not change \(k\) because it depends on \(v^2\), not the sign of velocity.

Physical interpretation

Kinetic energy measures the amount of motion in terms of energy transfer.
It does not encode direction—only how much work would be required to bring the particle to rest.
