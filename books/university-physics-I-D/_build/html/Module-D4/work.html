D4.2 Work#

D4.2.1 Comparative Concept: Impulse and Momentum Transfer#

In an earlier module, we introduced impulse as a way to describe how interactions change motion.

Rather than starting with force, we defined net linear impulse directly as a change in linear momentum:

\[ i_{\text{net},x} = \Delta p_x \]

This definition emphasized a key idea:

Impulse records how motion changes due to an interaction.

Only later did we connect impulse to a particular mechanism—force acting through time.
The important concept was not force itself, but the transfer of momentum.

This perspective allowed us to reason about interactions, collisions, and conservation without relying on acceleration-based arguments.

D4.2.2 Work as Change in Kinetic Energy#

In this section, we introduce an analogous idea for energy. In some situations, an interaction is not most naturally described by how long it acts, but by how far it acts. For example, we may know that a box is pushed with a constant force of 50 N across a floor through a distance of 15 m, without knowing how long the push lasted.

This motivates a second, complementary way of describing interactions.

Key Concept: Work as Energy Transfer

Work is the transfer of energy caused by a force acting through a displacement.

While impulse records interactions through time, work records interactions through traveled distance.

Just as impulse was defined as change in momentum, we define work as change in kinetic energy.

Key Concept: Work

Work is the net change in kinetic energy of a particle.

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \]

This definition emphasizes that work is not a new physical substance, but a way of accounting for energy transfer during an interaction.

Several important observations follow immediately:

  • Work depends only on the initial and final states of motion through \(k\).

  • If the kinetic energy of a particle increases, positive work has been done on it (or energy was transferred to the particle).

  • If the kinetic energy decreases, negative work has been done on it (or energy was transferred from the particle).

  • If the kinetic energy does not change, the net work is zero, even if interactions occurred.

At this stage, we do not need to know how the interaction took place—only that it resulted in a change in kinetic energy.

In the next sub-module, we will connect this definition of work to a specific physical mechanism:
forces acting through distance, in direct analogy with how impulse was later connected to forces acting through time.

Example – Work as Change in Kinetic Energy (No Force Needed)

Problem

A 0.50 kg cart speeds up from 2.0 m/s to 6.0 m/s on a horizontal track.
Find the net work done on the cart during this change in motion.



Solution

We use the definition of work introduced in this section:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k. \]

First compute the initial and final kinetic energies:

\[ k_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)(2.0)^2 = 1.0\ \text{J}, \]
\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)(6.0)^2 = 9.0\ \text{J}. \]

Then the change in kinetic energy is

\[ \Delta k = k_f - k_i = 9.0 - 1.0 = 8.0\ \text{J}. \]

Therefore,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = 8.0\ \text{J}. \]

Answer

\[ \boxed{w_{\text{net}} = +8.0\ \text{J}} \]

The positive sign means the particle gained kinetic energy: energy was transferred to the cart.

Box Activity 1 – Determining Work from a Change in Speed

In this activity, you will compute work using the idea

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k, \]

without using any force information.

Task: Complete the following steps.

  1. A point particle has mass

    \[ m = 1.2\ \text{kg}. \]

  2. The particle speeds up from

    \[ v_i = 1.5\ \text{m/s} \quad \text{to} \quad v_f = 4.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

  3. Compute the initial kinetic energy \(k_i\), the final kinetic energy \(k_f\), and the net work:

    \[ w_{\text{net}} = k_f - k_i. \]

  4. Reflect

    • Is the work positive or negative?

    • What does the sign tell you physically?

A Possible Solution Guide

Compute kinetic energies:

\[ k_i = \frac{1}{2}(1.2)(1.5)^2 = 1.35\ \text{J}, \]
\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}(1.2)(4.5)^2 = 12.15\ \text{J}. \]

So the net work is:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = 12.15 - 1.35 = 10.8\ \text{J}. \]

Interpretation

  • The work is positive, meaning kinetic energy increased.

  • Energy was transferred to the particle during the interaction.

Box Activity 2 – Exploring How Work Depends on Speed

In this activity, you will use Python to explore how the net work depends on speed changes, using

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = \frac{1}{2}m\left(v_f^2 - v_i^2\right). \]

Task: Complete the following steps.

  1. Use a particle mass of

    \[ m = 2.0\ \text{kg}. \]

  2. Choose an initial speed \(v_i = 0\) m/s (starting from rest).

  3. Compute the kinetic energy \(k\) for speeds from 0 to 10 m/s in steps of 1 m/s.

  4. Make a plot of kinetic energy \(k\) versus speed \(v\).

  5. Compute the work required to go from rest to each speed:

    \[ w_{\text{net}}(v) = \Delta k = k(v) - k(0). \]

  6. Reflect

    • Does \(k\) grow linearly with \(v\) or nonlinearly?

    • If you double the speed, by what factor does the required work change?

    • How does this connect to real-world stopping distance at higher speeds?

A Possible Solution Guide

What you should observe

See code and output below.

  • The graph of \(k\) vs. \(v\) curves upward because

    \[ k \propto v^2. \]

  • Doubling the speed requires four times the work (starting from rest), since

    \[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \propto v^2. \]

Example check

From 0 to 5 m/s:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(5^2) = 25\ \text{J}. \]

From 0 to 10 m/s:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(10^2) = 100\ \text{J}. \]

Doubling the speed from 5 m/s to 10 m/s increases the required work by a factor of four, not two.

%reset -f

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

m = 2.0  # kg

v = np.arange(0, 11, 1)          # speeds from 0 to 10 m/s
k = 0.5 * m * v**2               # kinetic energy
w_net = k - k[0]                 # work from rest to speed v

# Print a small table
print(" v (m/s)    k (J)     w_net (J)")
for vi, ki, wi in zip(v, k, w_net):
    print(f"{vi:6.1f}   {ki:7.2f}   {wi:9.2f}")

# Plot k vs v
plt.figure()
plt.plot(v, k, marker='o')
plt.xlabel("Speed v (m/s)")
plt.ylabel("Kinetic energy k (J)")
plt.title("Kinetic Energy vs Speed for a Point Particle")
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
