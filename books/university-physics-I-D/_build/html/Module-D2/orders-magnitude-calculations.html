D2.4 Orders of Magnitude Calculations#

D2.4.1 Introduction#

In physics, we often want quick estimates rather than exact answers.
An order-of-magnitude calculation estimates a quantity within a factor of about 10,
focusing on scale rather than precision.

These calculations:

  • Help check plausibility of results.

  • Allow us to compare quantities with vastly different sizes.

  • Provide a fast way to make predictions without a calculator.

D2.4.2 Definition#

If a quantity \(Q\) is approximately \(10^n\) in size,
we say it is of order of magnitude \(10^n\).

Mathematically:

\[ \text{Order of magnitude of } Q \approx 10^n \]

where \(n\) is an integer chosen so that \(Q\) is within a factor of 10 of \(10^n\).

Example:

  • \(2.6 \times 10^7\) → order of magnitude \(10^7\)

  • \(8.6 \times 10^{-3}\) → order of magnitude \(10^{-2}\) (because it’s closer to \(10^{-2}\) than \(10^{-3}\))

D2.4.3 Rounding Rules for Orders of Magnitude#

When determining \(n\):

  1. Express \(Q\) in scientific notation:
    \(Q = a \times 10^n\) with \(1 \le a < 10\).

  2. Compare \(a\) to \(\sqrt{10} \approx 3.16\).

  3. If \(a < 3.16\), round down to \(10^n\).

  4. If \(a > 3.16\), round up to \(10^{n+1}\).

Why \(\sqrt{10}\) and not 5?#

The cutoff point comes from equal relative error reasoning:

We have:

\[ x = a \times 10^n \]

and we want to know when \(x\) is equally close to \(10^n\) and \(10^{n+1}\) in a multiplicative sense.

Step 1 — Relative distance to the lower bound:

\[ \text{Factor to go down} = \frac{x}{10^n} = \frac{a \times 10^n}{10^n} = a \]

Step 2 — Relative distance to the upper bound:

\[ \text{Factor to go up} = \frac{10^{n+1}}{x} = \frac{10^{n+1}}{a \times 10^n} = \frac{10}{a} \]

Step 3 — Equal relative error condition:

\[ a = \frac{10}{a} \]

Step 4 — Solve:

\[ a^2 = 10 \]
\[ a = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.1623 \]

Interpretation:

  • If \(a < 3.16\) → closer to \(10^n\) (round down)

  • If \(a > 3.16\) → closer to \(10^{n+1}\) (round up)

  • If \(a = 3.16\) → exactly halfway on a logarithmic scale

This is why 5 is not the midpoint:
Orders of magnitude work on a logarithmic scale, so “halfway” means equal ratios, not equal differences.

D2.4.4 Using Orders of Magnitude in Estimations#

Orders of magnitude are useful when:

  • Input values are uncertain.

  • You just need a ballpark figure.

  • The system has many unknowns.

Example — Mass of the Earth (Order-of-Magnitude Estimate)

Known:

  • \(R_{\oplus} \approx 6.4 \times 10^6\ \text{m}\)

  • \(\rho_{\oplus} \approx 5.5 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3\)

Estimate:

\[M \approx \rho \cdot \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3\]

Ignoring constants (\(\frac{4}{3}\pi \approx 4\)),

\[M \approx (5 \times 10^3)(6 \times 10^6)^3\]

Compute powers of ten:

\[M \approx 5 \times 10^3 \cdot 2 \times 10^{20} \approx 10^{24}\ \text{kg}\]

Order of magnitude: \(10^{24}\) kg.

Example — Energy from a Chocolate Bar (Order-of-Magnitude Estimate)

Known:

  • Energy content \(\approx 200\ \text{kcal}\)

  • \(1\ \text{kcal} \approx 4.2 \times 10^3\ \text{J}\)

Estimate:

\[ E \approx 200 \times 4 \times 10^3 = 8 \times 10^5\ \text{J} \]

Order of magnitude: \(10^6\ \text{J}\).

D2.4.5 Quick Multiplication & Division in Orders of Magnitude#

  • Approximate each number to 1 significant digit.

  • Keep powers of ten separate from mantissas.

  • Multiply mantissas first, adjust power of ten.

Example:

\[ (3 \times 10^4) \cdot (7 \times 10^{-2}) \approx 21 \times 10^{2} \approx 2 \times 10^3 \]

Order of magnitude: \(10^3\).

D2.4.6 Common Pitfalls#

  • Overestimating precision — orders of magnitude are not for exact answers.

  • Forgetting units — even estimates must track units.

  • Mis-rounding near \(\sqrt{10}\) — \(a \approx 3.16\) is the break point.

D2.4.7 Fermi Estimates — Thinking Like a Physicist#

A Fermi estimate is a quick, order-of-magnitude approximation of a quantity that is difficult or impossible to measure directly.
Instead of exact data, we rely on:

  • Simple physical reasoning

  • Reasonable assumptions

  • Breaking a complex problem into simpler pieces

  • Order-of-magnitude arithmetic (powers of ten)

Fermi estimation is named after physicist Enrico Fermi, famous for obtaining surprisingly accurate results with very limited information. It is one of the most powerful mental tools in physics because it teaches you to approximate, validate your intuition, and notice when answers are way off.

Why We Use Fermi Estimates#

Fermi problems help us:

  • Build intuition for the scale of physical quantities.

  • Check whether a more detailed calculation is reasonable.

  • Avoid “nonsense answers” (e.g., densities larger than neutron stars in an intro physics problem).

  • Understand which assumptions matter and which details you can ignore.

The Basic Approach#

  1. Break the problem into parts

    For example, to estimate the number of piano tuners in Chicago (a famous Fermi problem), you might estimate
    population → households → pianos per household → tunings per year → tunings a tuner can do.

  2. Use round, convenient numbers

    Replace exact values with powers of ten for speed and clarity:

    • Instead of 312,000,000 people → use \(3\times10^8\).

  3. Multiply your estimates

    You will often get a final result accurate within a factor of 2–10, which is excellent for an order-of-magnitude estimate.

  4. Report the answer as an order of magnitude

    Example: \(7\times10^{24}~\text{kg}\) is the same order of magnitude as \(10^{25}~\text{kg}\).

Example — Fermi Estimate of the Radius of the Earth

One of the classic “Fermi thinking” exercises is estimating the size of the Earth without using Google, formulas, or scientific constants.
Surprisingly — it’s possible with nothing more than everyday facts.

Step 1 — Use what we know about time zones Most people know:

  • There are 24 time zones around the Earth.

  • One time zone corresponds to 1 hour difference in local time.

  • In 1 hour the Earth rotates through one-twenty-fourth of a full circle.

Thus:

\[ 1\ \text{time zone} \approx \frac{360^\circ}{24} = 15^\circ. \]

Step 2 — Use a familiar distance between two cities Pick two cities you know the approximate distance between, and know that they are roughly one time zone apart.
For example:

  • New York to Chicago is about 1 time zone apart.

  • The distance is roughly 1,000 km (common-knowledge round number: “it’s about a 2-hour flight”).

So:
15° of Earth’s circumference ≈ 1,000 km.

Step 3 — Scale up to the full circumference If 15° corresponds to 1,000 km, then 360° corresponds to:

\[ \frac{360}{15} \times 1000 \approx 24\times 1000 = 2.4\times10^4\ \text{km}. \]

So the Earth’s circumference is roughly:

\[ C \approx 2.4\times 10^4\ \text{km}. \]

Step 4 — Convert circumference to radius We know:

\[ C = 2\pi R. \]

Using \(\pi \approx 3\) for a Fermi estimate:

\[ R \approx \frac{C}{6} \approx \frac{2.4\times10^4}{6} \approx 4\times10^3\ \text{km}. \]

Final Estimate

\[ R_{\oplus} \approx 4\times10^3\ \text{km}. \]

Actual Value

\[ R_{\oplus,\text{actual}} \approx 6.4\times10^3\ \text{km}. \]

This is off by less than a factor of 2 — excellent for a pure reasoning-only estimate based entirely on common knowledge.


Box Activity — How Many Guitar Strings Are Being Played Right Now in Utah?

Using the Fermi-estimation approach:

  1. Estimate the population of Utah.

  2. Estimate what fraction plays guitar.

  3. Estimate how many practice or play per day, and for how long.

  4. Convert this into the rate at a random moment.

  5. Give your final answer as an order of magnitude, using powers of ten.

A Possible Solution Strategy

  • Utah population: \(3\times10^6\)

  • Fraction who play: maybe \(5\% = 0.05\)

  • Players: \(1.5\times10^5\)

  • Fraction playing at a random moment: maybe \(1\% = 0.01\)

  • Strings per guitar: \(6\)

Multiply:

\[ N \approx (1.5\times10^5)(0.01)(6) \approx 10^4 \text{ strings} \]

Order of magnitude:
$\(10^4 \text{ guitar strings being played right now.}\)$

D2.4.8 Summary#

  • Purpose: Focus on scale, not exactness.

  • Approach: Round to 1 sig. digit, compare to \(\sqrt{10}\), adjust powers of ten.

  • Key skill: Recognize when an estimate is “good enough” for the question.
