D7.5 The First Law of Thermodynamics#

D7.5.1 Why the First Law matters#

In earlier modules, we introduced energy in a mechanical context. We learned to track changes in kinetic energy and relate them to work done by forces.

In thermodynamics, we broaden this view. Systems can exchange energy not only through mechanical work, but also through heat transfer, and they can store energy internally in microscopic forms that are not directly visible as motion.

The First Law of Thermodynamics provides a single, unified statement of energy conservation that applies to all physical systems—mechanical, thermal, and beyond.

D7.5.2 Statement of the First Law#

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that the change in a system’s internal energy is equal to the energy transferred into the system as heat minus the energy transferred out of the system as work:

\[ \Delta U = Q - W. \]

This equation is not a special thermodynamic rule—it is simply energy conservation, written in a form that explicitly accounts for how energy enters and leaves a system.

Internal energy \(U\) is a state variable, meaning that \(\Delta U\) depends only on the initial and final states of the system, not on the path taken between them.

D7.5.3 Sign conventions for heat and work#

Concept — Sign Conventions for Heat and Work

In thermodynamics, signs indicate the direction of energy transfer relative to the system.


Heat (same convention in physics in most disciplines)

  • \(Q > 0\): heat flows into the system

  • \(Q < 0\): heat flows out of the system

Heat represents energy transferred due to a temperature difference and is always treated consistently across disciplines.


Work (physics convention used in this course)

In physics, work is defined as work done by the system on its surroundings.
The sign of the work follows directly from the direction of the force exerted by the system and the displacement of the boundary.

With this definition:

  • \(W > 0\) corresponds to the system doing work on its surroundings (e.g., expansion).

  • \(W < 0\) corresponds to the surroundings doing work on the system (e.g., compression).

With this convention, the First Law is written as

\[ \Delta U = Q - W. \]

Note on chemistry conventions

In chemistry, work is often defined with the opposite sign, by taking work done on the system as positive.
With that bookkeeping choice, the First Law is written as

\[ \Delta U = Q + W. \]

The underlying physics is the same; only the sign convention for work differs.

In this course, we will consistently use the physics definition: work done by the system.

D7.5.4 The First Law as the true energy equation#

In mechanics, we often write an “energy equation” such as

\[ \Delta K = W, \]

or, when potential energy is included,

\[ \Delta K + \Delta U_{\text{mech}} = 0, \]

for isolated systems.

These equations are special cases of energy conservation that apply when:

  • energy transfer occurs only through mechanical work,

  • and internal thermal effects are negligible.

The First Law makes no such restrictions.

It states that all changes in a system’s energy—mechanical or thermal—must be accounted for through heat and work:

\[ \boxed{\Delta U = Q - W} \]

Here, the internal energy \(U\) includes:

  • kinetic energy of microscopic motion,

  • potential energy of microscopic interactions,

  • and, when relevant, macroscopic mechanical energy.

From this perspective:

  • The mechanical energy equations are limited forms of the First Law.

  • The First Law is the general energy accounting principle.

D7.5.5 Interpreting the First Law qualitatively#

The First Law allows us to reason about processes without detailed calculations:

  • If a system absorbs heat and does no work, its internal energy increases.

  • If a system does work without absorbing heat, its internal energy decreases.

  • If heat enters and work is done simultaneously, the net change depends on the balance between the two.

The power of the First Law lies not in computation, but in consistent bookkeeping of energy flow.

Example — Internal Energy Is a State Variable

A closed system starts in an initial state A and ends in a final state B.
The system undergoes the same net change in state in two different ways.


Process 1

  • Heat absorbed by the system:

    \[ Q_1 = +800\,\text{J} \]

  • Work done by the system:

    \[ W_1 = +300\,\text{J} \]

Using the First Law,

\[ \Delta U_1 = Q_1 - W_1 = 800 - 300 = 500\,\text{J}. \]

Process 2

  • Heat absorbed by the system:

    \[ Q_2 = +1200\,\text{J} \]

  • Work done by the system:

    \[ W_2 = +700\,\text{J} \]

Again applying the First Law,

\[ \Delta U_2 = Q_2 - W_2 = 1200 - 700 = 500\,\text{J}. \]

Interpretation

Even though the amount of heat absorbed and the amount of work done are different for the two processes, the change in internal energy is the same:

\[ \Delta U_1 = \Delta U_2 = 500\,\text{J}. \]

This shows that internal energy depends only on the initial and final states, not on the path taken between them.


Key takeaway

Internal energy \(U\) is a state variable.
Heat \(Q\) and work \(W\) are process variables: they depend on how the change occurs, but their combination \(Q - W\) does not.

Example — Friction, Newton’s Second Law, and Internal Energy

A block of mass

\[ m = 2.0\,\text{kg} \]

slides across a rough horizontal floor with an initial speed

\[ v_0 = 6.0\,\text{m/s}. \]

The kinetic friction force acting on the block has magnitude

\[ f_k = 4.0\,\text{N}. \]

The block eventually comes to rest after sliding some distance.


Mechanical description (Newton’s laws)

The friction force acts opposite the direction of motion, producing a constant acceleration

\[ a = -\frac{f_k}{m} = -\frac{4.0}{2.0} = -2.0\,\text{m/s}^2. \]

Using kinematics, the stopping distance is

\[ 0 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta x = \frac{v_0^2}{2|a|} = \frac{36}{4} = 9.0\,\text{m}. \]

Work done by friction

The work done by the friction force is

\[ W_{\text{fric}} = -f_k \Delta x = -(4.0)(9.0) = -36\,\text{J}. \]

This equals the change in the block’s kinetic energy:

\[ \Delta K = K_f - K_i = 0 - \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = -36\,\text{J}. \]

From a purely mechanical viewpoint, the kinetic energy is “lost.”


Thermodynamic interpretation

The energy is not destroyed.
The friction force converts mechanical energy into internal energy of the block and the floor (microscopic motion of atoms).

Applying the First Law to the block–floor system:

  • No heat enters or leaves the system:

    \[ Q = 0 \]

  • The system does no macroscopic work on the surroundings:

    \[ W = 0 \]

So the First Law gives

\[ \Delta U = Q - W = 0. \]

However, the internal energy of the system increases by

\[ \Delta U_{\text{thermal}} = +36\,\text{J}, \]

exactly equal to the kinetic energy lost.


Key takeaway

Friction does not violate energy conservation.
It transfers energy from macroscopic motion into internal energy.

Newton’s laws describe the motion, but the First Law of Thermodynamics completes the energy accounting.

Box Activity 1 — Friction, Work, and Temperature Increase

In this activity, you will connect Newton’s Second Law, mechanical work, and the First Law of Thermodynamics to determine how mechanical energy becomes internal energy.

Task:

A solid metal block slides across a rough horizontal surface.

The block has:

  • mass

    \[ m = 3.0\,\text{kg} \]

  • initial speed

    \[ v_0 = 5.0\,\text{m/s} \]

A constant frictional force of magnitude

\[ f = 6.0\,\text{N} \]

acts on the block opposite the direction of motion.

Assume:

  • specific heat capacity of the block \( c = 450 \) J/(kg\(\cdot\) K)

Assume all mechanical energy lost goes into the internal energy of the block.

Answer the following questions.

  1. Use Newton’s Second Law to determine the acceleration of the block.

  2. Determine the distance the block slides before coming to rest.

  3. Calculate the work done by friction.

  4. Use the First Law of Thermodynamics to determine the change in internal energy of the block.

  5. Determine the increase in temperature of the block.

A Possible Solution Guide

Step 1: Acceleration (Newton’s Second Law)

The friction force is the only horizontal force, so

\[ a = -\frac{f}{m} = -\frac{6.0}{3.0} = -2.0\,\text{m/s}^2. \]

Step 2: Stopping distance

Using kinematics,

\[ 0 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta x = -\frac{v_0^2}{2a} = \frac{25}{4} = 6.25\,\text{m}. \]

Step 3: Work done by friction

\[ W_{\text{fric}} = f\,\Delta x = (-6.0)(6.25) = -37.5\,\text{J}. \]

Step 4: First Law of Thermodynamics

For the block:

  • No heat enters or leaves the “system” during the slide:

    \[ Q = 0 \]

  • The work done by the block:

    \[ W = -37.5\,\text{J} \]

Applying the First Law,

\[ \Delta U = Q - W = 0 - (-37.5) = +37.5\,\text{J}. \]

The internal energy of the block increases by 37.5 J.


Step 5: Temperature increase

Using

\[ \Delta U = mc\Delta T, \]
\[ \Delta T = \frac{\Delta U}{mc} = \frac{37.5}{(3.0)(450)} \approx 0.028\,\text{K}. \]

Interpretation

The temperature increase is small, but the energy accounting is exact:
every joule of mechanical energy lost becomes internal energy.

This demonstrates how Newton’s laws, work, and the First Law of Thermodynamics describe the same physical process from different perspectives.

D7.5.6 Key takeaway#

The First Law of Thermodynamics is not a new kind of physics.
It is energy conservation, written in its most complete and general form.

All other energy equations—mechanical or thermal—are special cases of this law.
