Problem 1: Linear Momentum with Direction and Units#

Calculate the linear momentum for each of the following cases.
Be careful with units, signs, and scientific notation.

  1. A proton moving in the +x-direction with a speed equal to 99.9% of the speed of light.

  2. A 2000.0 kg sedan traveling with a velocity of –70.0 mph.

Problem solution

Relevant equation

For 1D motion, linear momentum is

\[ p_x = m v_x. \]

Part 1 — Proton at relativistic speed (Newtonian treatment)

Given

  • Proton mass:

    \[m_p = 1.67\times10^{-27}\ \text{kg}\]

  • Speed:

    \[v_x = +0.999\,c = +0.999(3.00\times10^8)\ \text{m/s}\]

Compute velocity

\[ v_x = +2.997\times10^8\ \text{m/s}. \]

Momentum

\[ p_x = (1.67\times10^{-27})(2.997\times10^8) = 5.00\times10^{-19}\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{p_x \approx +5.00\times10^{-19}\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

Note: At this speed, a fully correct treatment requires relativistic momentum.
Here we are intentionally using the Newtonian definition for comparison and scale.

Part 2 — Sedan traveling at –70.0 mph

Given

  • Mass:

    \[m = 2000.0\ \text{kg}\]

  • Velocity:

    \[v_x = -70.0\ \text{mph}\]

Convert mph to m/s

\[ 1\ \text{mph} = 0.44704\ \text{m/s} \]
\[ v_x = -70.0(0.44704) = -31.3\ \text{m/s}. \]

Momentum

\[ p_x = (2000.0)(-31.3) = -6.26\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{p_x = -6.26\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

Final Summary

  • Proton: very small momentum due to extremely small mass

  • Sedan: much larger momentum due to large mass, despite much lower speed

The sign of each momentum reflects the direction of motion along the x-axis.

Problem 2: Linear Momentum of Two Vehicles (Opposite Directions)#

A semi-truck with mass

\[ m_{\text{truck}} = 1.20\times10^4\ \text{kg} \]

is traveling northbound on I-15 with a speed of

\[ 65\ \text{mph}. \]

At the same time, a sedan with mass

\[ m_{\text{sedan}} = 2.40\times10^3\ \text{kg} \]

is traveling southbound on I-15 with a speed of

\[ 85\ \text{mph}. \]

Take the northbound direction as positive.

  1. Determine the linear momentum of each vehicle (including sign).

  2. Determine the magnitude of the momentum for each vehicle.

Problem solution

Relevant equation

For 1D motion, linear momentum is $\( p_x = m v_x. \)$

Step 1 — Convert speeds to m/s

Using

\[ 1\ \text{mph} = 0.44704\ \text{m/s}, \]

Semi-truck (northbound, positive):

\[ v_{\text{truck},x} = +65(0.44704) = +29.1\ \text{m/s}. \]

Sedan (southbound, negative):

\[ v_{\text{sedan},x} = -85(0.44704) = -38.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 2 — Compute individual momenta

Semi-truck

\[ p_{\text{truck},x} = (1.20\times10^4)(+29.1) = +3.49\times10^5\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Sedan

\[ p_{\text{sedan},x} = (2.40\times10^3)(-38.0) = -9.12\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3 — Magnitudes of the momenta

The magnitude of momentum is the absolute value:

\[ |p_{\text{truck}}| = 3.49\times10^5\ \text{kg·m/s}, \]
\[ |p_{\text{sedan}}| = 9.12\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final Answers

  • Semi-truck momentum:

\[ \boxed{p_{\text{truck},x} = +3.49\times10^5\ \text{kg·m/s}}, \qquad \boxed{|p_{\text{truck}}| = 3.49\times10^5\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

  • Sedan momentum:

\[ \boxed{p_{\text{sedan},x} = -9.12\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}}, \qquad \boxed{|p_{\text{sedan}}| = 9.12\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

The signs indicate direction relative to northbound (+), while the magnitudes quantify “how much motion” each vehicle has regardless of direction.

Problem 3: Momentum, Direction, and Sign Conventions#

A point particle of mass

\[ m = 1.2\ \text{kg} \]

moves along the \(x\)-axis with velocity

\[ v_x = -3.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. Compute the particle’s momentum \(p_x\).

  2. State clearly what the sign of the momentum means physically.

  3. Would two observers moving at different constant velocities necessarily agree on the value of \(p_x\)?

Problem solution

Step 1: Relevant equation

For a point particle in 1D,

\[ p_x = m v_x. \]

Step 2: Calculation

\[ p_x = (1.2)(-3.5) = -4.2\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3: Physical interpretation

The negative sign indicates that the particle’s momentum is directed in the negative \(x\)-direction, according to the chosen coordinate system.

Step 4: Observer dependence

Different inertial observers may measure different values of momentum because they measure different velocities.
However, all observers agree on the change in momentum caused by an interaction.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{p_x = -4.2\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

Problem 4: Impulse as Momentum Transfer#

A point particle has mass

\[ m = 0.40\ \text{kg}. \]

It moves initially with velocity

\[ v_{x,i} = +6.0\ \text{m/s}, \]

and after an interaction its velocity becomes

\[ v_{x,f} = +2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. Compute the impulse \(j_x\) delivered to the particle.

  2. State whether the impulse is positive or negative.

  3. Describe what the sign tells you about the direction of the interaction.

Problem solution

Step 1: Relevant equation

Impulse is momentum transfer:

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

For a point particle,

\[ p_x = m v_x, \]

so

\[ j_x = m(v_{x,f} - v_{x,i}). \]

Step 2: Calculate the impulse

\[ j_x = (0.40)\left(2.0 - 6.0\right) = (0.40)(-4.0) = -1.6\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3: Sign and interpretation

The impulse is negative, which means the net momentum transfer was in the negative \(x\)-direction.

Even though the particle continued moving in the positive direction, the interaction transferred momentum in the opposite direction, reducing the particle’s momentum.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{j_x = -1.6\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

Problem 5: Net Impulse in a Crash-Test Collision#

Before a crash-test collision, a dummy of mass

\[ m = 75\ \text{kg} \]

is traveling toward a wall with velocity

\[ v_{x,i} = +25.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

After the collision, the dummy comes to rest.

  1. Determine the net impulse delivered to the dummy during the collision.

  2. State the direction of the impulse and explain its physical meaning.

Problem solution

Step 1 — Identify the relevant principle

The net impulse is equal to the change in momentum:

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = \Delta p_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

Step 2 — Compute initial and final momentum

Initial momentum:

\[ p_{x,i} = m v_{x,i} = (75)(+25.0) = +1875\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final velocity is zero, so final momentum:

\[ p_{x,f} = 0. \]

Step 3 — Compute the net impulse

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = 0 - 1875 = -1875\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 4 — Interpret the sign

The negative sign indicates that the impulse acts in the negative \(x\)-direction, opposite the dummy’s initial motion.
This impulse is provided by the wall and is responsible for bringing the dummy to rest.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{j_{\text{net},x} = -1.88\times10^3\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

Problem 6: Momentum Change and Impulse (1D Signed Motion)#

A particle of mass

\[ m = 5.0\times10^{-3}\ \text{kg} \]

moves in 1D along the \(z\)-axis.

  • Initially, it has a speed of \(335\ \text{m/s}\) in the \(-z\) direction.

  • After some elapsed time, its velocity is

    \[ v_{z,f} = -875\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. What is the initial velocity of the particle, \(v_{z,i}\)?

  2. What are the initial and final momenta, \(p_{z,i}\) and \(p_{z,f}\)?

  3. Before calculating the net impulse, predict the direction of the impulse. Explain briefly.

  4. Calculate the momentum transfer (net impulse) \(j_{\text{net},z} = \Delta p_z\).

Problem solution

Step 1 — Initial velocity from speed and direction

A speed of \(335\ \text{m/s}\) in the \(-z\) direction means the signed velocity is

\[ v_{z,i} = -335\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 2 — Initial and final momentum

For 1D motion,

\[ p_z = m v_z. \]

Initial momentum

\[ p_{z,i} = (5.0\times10^{-3})(-335) = -1.675\ \text{kg·m/s} \approx -1.7\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final momentum

\[ p_{z,f} = (5.0\times10^{-3})(-875) = -4.375\ \text{kg·m/s} \approx -4.4\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3 — Predict the direction of impulse (before calculating)

The velocity changes from \(-335\) to \(-875\ \text{m/s}\), meaning the particle becomes more negative (it speeds up in the \(-z\) direction).

So the momentum becomes more negative, which implies

\[ \Delta p_z = p_{z,f} - p_{z,i} < 0. \]

Therefore, the net impulse should be in the negative \(z\) direction.

Step 4 — Net impulse (momentum transfer)

Net impulse is momentum transfer:

\[ j_{\text{net},z} = \Delta p_z = p_{z,f} - p_{z,i}. \]

Compute:

\[ j_{\text{net},z} = (-4.375) - (-1.675) = -2.700\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

With appropriate significant figures,

\[ j_{\text{net},z} \approx -2.7\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final Answers

  1. \[\boxed{v_{z,i} = -335\ \text{m/s}}\]
  2. \[\boxed{p_{z,i} \approx -1.7\ \text{kg·m/s}},\qquad \boxed{p_{z,f} \approx -4.4\ \text{kg·m/s}}\]

  3. Impulse is predicted to be in the \(-z\) direction because the momentum becomes more negative.

  4. \[\boxed{j_{\text{net},z} \approx -2.7\ \text{kg·m/s}}\]

Problem 7: Impulse from a Constant Force (Units + Interpretation)#

A constant force acts on a point particle along the \(x\)-axis:

\[ f_x = -5.0\ \text{N} \]

for a time interval

\[ \Delta t = 0.30\ \text{s}. \]

  1. Compute the impulse \(j_x\) delivered by this force.

  2. Show that the unit of impulse can be written as kg·m/s.

  3. Explain what the negative sign of your answer means physically.

Problem solution

Step 1: Relevant equation

For a constant (or average) force,

\[ j_x = f_x\,\Delta t. \]

Step 2: Compute the impulse

\[ j_x = (-5.0)(0.30) = -1.5\ \text{N·s}. \]

Step 3: Show impulse units as kg·m/s

Using

\[ 1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg·m/s}^2, \]

we get

\[ \text{N·s} = \left(\text{kg·m/s}^2\right)\text{s} = \text{kg·m/s}. \]

So the impulse can be written as

\[ j_x = -1.5\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 4: Interpret the sign

The negative sign means the momentum transfer (impulse) is in the negative \(x\)-direction.
In other words, the interaction pushes/pulls in the negative direction during the time interval.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{j_x = -1.5\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]

Problem 8: Frictional Force from Momentum Change#

You observe a point particle–like object of mass

\[ m = 2.0\ \text{kg} \]

moving in 1D with an initial velocity

\[ v_{x,i} = -45\ \text{m/s}. \]

The object is subjected only to a frictional force and comes to rest after

\[ \Delta t = 8.0\ \text{s}. \]

  1. Determine the direction of the frictional force before doing any calculations.

  2. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the frictional force.

Problem solution

Step 1 — Predict the direction of the force

The object is initially moving in the negative \(x\)-direction and comes to rest.
Friction always acts opposite the direction of motion, so the frictional force must act in the positive \(x\)-direction.

Step 2 — Use impulse–momentum theorem

The net impulse equals the change in momentum:

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = \Delta p_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

Step 3 — Compute initial and final momentum

Initial momentum:

\[ p_{x,i} = m v_{x,i} = (2.0)(-45) = -90\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final velocity is zero, so final momentum:

\[ p_{x,f} = 0. \]

Change in momentum:

\[ \Delta p_x = 0 - (-90) = +90\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 4 — Relate impulse to force

Since friction is the only force acting and it acts over the entire time interval,

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = f_x\,\Delta t. \]

Solve for the frictional force:

\[ f_x = \frac{\Delta p_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{90}{8.0} = 11.25\ \text{N}. \]

Step 5 — Interpret the result

The force is positive, meaning it acts in the positive \(x\)-direction, opposite the object’s initial motion—as expected for friction.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{f_x \approx +11\ \text{N}} \]

The frictional force acts in the positive \(x\)-direction.

Problem 9: Impact Force in Two Head-On Collisions#

A \(2.0\ \text{kg}\) particle travels at

\[ v_{x,i} = +25\ \text{m/s} \]

toward a fixed wall. Two experiments are performed, each with the same impact time

\[ \Delta t = 0.020\ \text{s}. \]

  • Experiment A: The particle sticks to the wall after the collision.

  • Experiment B: The particle bounces off the wall with the same speed.

Assume the impact force is much larger than any other forces during the collision.

  1. Predict which experiment produces the larger impact force, and explain why.

  2. Quantify your prediction by calculating the average impact force in both cases.

Problem solution

Step 1 — Conceptual prediction

Impact force is tied to impulse, which equals the change in momentum:

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = \Delta p_x. \]

  • Sticking: momentum goes from \(+mv\) to \(0\).

  • Bouncing back: momentum goes from \(+mv\) to \(-mv\) (a larger change).

Therefore, the bounce-back collision produces a larger momentum change and thus a larger impact force (for the same \(\Delta t\)).

Step 2 — Compute impulse in each experiment

Given \(m=2.0\ \text{kg}\) and \(v=25\ \text{m/s}\):

Experiment A (sticks):

\[ p_{x,i} = (2.0)(25)=50\ \text{kg·m/s}, \qquad p_{x,f}=0 \]
\[ \Delta p_x = 0-50 = -50\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Experiment B (bounces with same speed):

\[ p_{x,f} = (2.0)(-25)= -50\ \text{kg·m/s} \]
\[ \Delta p_x = -50-50 = -100\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3 — Average impact force

Using

\[ \bar f_x = \frac{\Delta p_x}{\Delta t}, \]

Experiment A (sticks):

\[ \bar f_x = \frac{-50}{0.020} = -2.5\times10^3\ \text{N}. \]

Experiment B (bounces):

\[ \bar f_x = \frac{-100}{0.020} = -5.0\times10^3\ \text{N}. \]

(The negative sign indicates the force acts opposite the incoming direction.)

Final Answers

  • Larger impact force: Bounce-back collision

  • Average impact forces:

\[ \boxed{\bar f_x = -2.5\times10^3\ \text{N}\ \text{(sticks)}},\qquad \boxed{\bar f_x = -5.0\times10^3\ \text{N}\ \text{(bounces)}} \]

For the same impact time, doubling the momentum change doubles the impact force.

Problem 10: Impulse from a Force–Time Graph (Area Interpretation)#

A point particle experiences a force along the \(x\)-axis that varies with time as shown conceptually by a triangular force–time pulse:

  • The force increases linearly from \(0\) to \(8.0\ \text{N}\) over a time interval of \(1.2\ \text{s}\).

  • After reaching \(8.0\ \text{N}\), the force immediately drops back to zero.

  1. Compute the impulse delivered to the particle.

  2. If the particle has mass \(m = 0.60\ \text{kg}\) and initial velocity \(v_{x,i} = +1.5\ \text{m/s}\), find the final velocity.

  3. Explain why the detailed shape of the force–time curve is less important than its area.

Problem solution

Step 1: Impulse from the force–time graph

Impulse is given by the area under the force–time curve.

For a triangular pulse,

\[ j_x = \frac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{height}). \]

Here,

\[ j_x = \frac{1}{2}(1.2)(8.0) = 4.8\ \text{N·s}. \]

Using \(1\ \text{N·s} = 1\ \text{kg·m/s}\),

\[ j_x = 4.8\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 2: Apply impulse–momentum theorem

Initial momentum:

\[ p_{x,i} = m v_{x,i} = (0.60)(1.5) = 0.90\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final momentum:

\[ p_{x,f} = p_{x,i} + j_x = 0.90 + 4.8 = 5.7\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final velocity:

\[ v_{x,f} = \frac{p_{x,f}}{m} = \frac{5.7}{0.60} = 9.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 3: Conceptual interpretation

Although the force changes with time, the total momentum transfer depends only on the area under the force–time curve, not on the exact shape of the curve.

Different force–time profiles with the same area produce the same impulse and therefore the same change in momentum.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{j_x = 4.8\ \text{kg·m/s}}, \qquad \boxed{v_{x,f} = 9.5\ \text{m/s}} \]

Problem 11: Two-Particle Momentum Conservation (1D)#

Two carts move along a straight, frictionless track and interact briefly.

  • Cart 1 has mass

    \[ m_1 = 1.0\ \text{kg} \]

    and initial velocity

    \[ v_{1,x,i} = +3.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

  • Cart 2 has mass

\[ m_2 = 2.0\ \text{kg} \]

and is initially at rest.

After the interaction, cart 1 is observed to move with velocity

\[ v_{1,x,f} = -1.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. Compute the initial total momentum of the system.

  2. Determine the final velocity of cart 2.

  3. Explain why cart 1 reverses direction even though no external impulse acts on the system.

Problem solution

Step 1: Conservation of momentum

Because the net external impulse on the two-cart system is zero, total momentum is conserved:

\[ P_{x,i} = P_{x,f}. \]

For a two-particle system in 1D,

\[ m_1 v_{1,x,i} + m_2 v_{2,x,i} = m_1 v_{1,x,f} + m_2 v_{2,x,f}. \]

Step 2: Compute the initial momentum

Initial total momentum:

\[ P_{x,i} = (1.0)(+3.0) + (2.0)(0) = 3.0\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3: Solve for the final velocity of cart 2

Substitute known values:

\[ 3.0 = (1.0)(-1.0) + (2.0)v_{2,x,f}. \]

Rearranging,

\[ 3.0 + 1.0 = 2.0\,v_{2,x,f}, \]
\[ v_{2,x,f} = \frac{4.0}{2.0} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 4: Physical interpretation

Cart 1 reverses direction because it transfers a significant amount of momentum to cart 2 during the interaction.
Even though the total momentum of the system remains constant, individual particles are free to change direction as momentum is redistributed internally.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{v_{2,x,f} = +2.0\ \text{m/s}} \]

Problem 12: Net Impulse from Multiple Forces (Same Time Interval)#

A point particle experiences three forces along the \(x\)-axis for the same time interval

\[ \Delta t = 0.40\ \text{s}. \]

The forces are:

  • \(f_{1,x} = +6.0\ \text{N}\)

  • \(f_{2,x} = -2.0\ \text{N}\)

  • \(f_{3,x} = -1.0\ \text{N}\)

The particle has mass

\[ m = 2.0\ \text{kg} \]

and starts from rest.

  1. Compute the net force \(f_{\text{net},x}\).

  2. Compute the net impulse \(j_{\text{net},x}\).

  3. Determine the final velocity \(v_{x,f}\).

  4. State what the sign of \(v_{x,f}\) tells you physically.

Problem solution

Step 1: Net force

Since all forces act over the same time interval, we first add them:

\[ f_{\text{net},x} = f_{1,x} + f_{2,x} + f_{3,x} = 6.0 - 2.0 - 1.0 = +3.0\ \text{N}. \]

Step 2: Net impulse

For forces acting over the same \(\Delta t\),

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = f_{\text{net},x}\,\Delta t = (3.0)(0.40) = 1.2\ \text{N·s}. \]

Using \(1\ \text{N·s} = 1\ \text{kg·m/s}\),

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = 1.2\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3: Use impulse–momentum theorem to find final velocity

Impulse–momentum theorem:

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = \Delta p_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

Since the particle starts from rest, \(p_{x,i} = 0\).

So,

\[ p_{x,f} = j_{\text{net},x} = 1.2\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Then

\[ v_{x,f} = \frac{p_{x,f}}{m} = \frac{1.2}{2.0} = 0.60\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 4: Interpret the sign

The positive final velocity means the particle ends up moving in the positive \(x\)-direction.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{f_{\text{net},x} = +3.0\ \text{N}},\quad \boxed{j_{\text{net},x} = 1.2\ \text{kg·m/s}},\quad \boxed{v_{x,f} = +0.60\ \text{m/s}} \]

Problem 13: Net Impulse from Multiple Forces (Different Time Intervals)#

A point particle experiences three forces along the \(x\)-axis, but each force acts for a different elapsed time:

  • \(f_{1,x} = +8.0\ \text{N} \quad \text{acts for} \quad \Delta t_1 = 0.10\ \text{s}\)

  • \(f_{2,x} = -3.0\ \text{N} \quad \text{acts for} \quad \Delta t_2 = 0.40\ \text{s}\)

  • \(f_{3,x} = +2.0\ \text{N} \quad \text{acts for} \quad \Delta t_3 = 0.50\ \text{s}\)

The particle has mass

\[ m = 1.6\ \text{kg} \]

and initial velocity

\[ v_{x,i} = +1.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. Compute the impulse from each force: \(j_{1,x}\), \(j_{2,x}\), \(j_{3,x}\).

  2. Compute the net impulse \(j_{\text{net},x}\).

  3. Determine the final velocity \(v_{x,f}\).

  4. State whether the particle speeds up or slows down (and why).

Problem solution

Step 1: Compute each impulse separately

Because the forces act over different time intervals, we must add impulses:

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = j_{1,x} + j_{2,x} + j_{3,x}. \]

For each force,

\[ j_{k,x} = f_{k,x}\,\Delta t_k. \]

So,

\[ j_{1,x} = (8.0)(0.10) = 0.80\ \text{N·s}, \]
\[ j_{2,x} = (-3.0)(0.40) = -1.2\ \text{N·s}, \]
\[ j_{3,x} = (2.0)(0.50) = 1.0\ \text{N·s}. \]

Step 2: Net impulse

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = 0.80 - 1.2 + 1.0 = 0.60\ \text{N·s}. \]

Using \(1\ \text{N·s} = 1\ \text{kg·m/s}\),

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = 0.60\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3: Use impulse–momentum theorem

Impulse–momentum theorem:

\[ j_{\text{net},x} = \Delta p_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

Initial momentum:

\[ p_{x,i} = m v_{x,i} = (1.6)(1.0) = 1.6\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final momentum:

\[ p_{x,f} = p_{x,i} + j_{\text{net},x} = 1.6 + 0.60 = 2.2\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final velocity:

\[ v_{x,f} = \frac{p_{x,f}}{m} = \frac{2.2}{1.6} = 1.375\ \text{m/s}. \]

To a reasonable level of rounding,

\[ v_{x,f} \approx 1.38\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 4: Speed-up or slow-down?

The net impulse is positive, so it increases the particle’s momentum in the +x direction.
Since the particle was already moving in +x, this means it speeds up.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{j_{1,x}=0.80\ \text{kg·m/s}},\quad \boxed{j_{2,x}=-1.2\ \text{kg·m/s}},\quad \boxed{j_{3,x}=1.0\ \text{kg·m/s}} \]
\[ \boxed{j_{\text{net},x}=0.60\ \text{kg·m/s}},\qquad \boxed{v_{x,f}\approx 1.38\ \text{m/s}} \]

Problem 14: Use Python to Solve (Two-Particle Momentum Conservation)#

Two carts interact on a frictionless track (1D). Assume the net external impulse on the two-cart system is zero.

  • \(m_1 = 0.75\ \text{kg}, \qquad v_{1,x,i} = +3.20\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(m_2 = 1.50\ \text{kg}, \qquad v_{2,x,i} = -0.80\ \text{m/s}\)

  • After the interaction: \(v_{1,x,f} = +0.50\ \text{m/s}\)

Task: Use Python to compute the final velocity \(v_{2,x,f}\).

  • Show two solution methods in Python:

    1. a direct calculator approach (algebra solved by you),

    2. a SymPy equation-solver approach.

Format your final numerical answer using scientific notation with three significant figures.

Problem solution

Method 1 — Direct calculator approach

m1 = 0.75
m2 = 1.50
v1_i = 3.20
v2_i = -0.80
v1_f = 0.50

P_i = m1*v1_i + m2*v2_i
v2_f = (P_i - m1*v1_f)/m2

print(f"{v2_f:.2e}")

Method 2 — SymPy solver approach

import sympy as sp

m1 = 0.75
m2 = 1.50
v1_i = 3.20
v2_i = -0.80
v1_f = 0.50

v2_f = sp.symbols('v2_f')

eq = sp.Eq(m1*v1_i + m2*v2_i, m1*v1_f + m2*v2_f)
sol = sp.solve(eq, v2_f)[0]

print(f"{float(sol):.2e}")

Check

Both methods should print the same value for \(v_{2,x,f}\), because they are solving the same momentum-conservation equation.

Problem 15: Electron Acceleration and Momentum (Python + SymPy)#

In a particle accelerator, an electron of mass

\[ m_e = 9.11\times10^{-31}\ \text{kg} \]

is accelerated along a straight line.

Its velocity increases linearly with time according to

\[ v_x(t) = \frac{v_f}{t_f}\,t, \]

where

\[ t_f = 2.0\times10^{-9}\ \text{s} \]

is the total acceleration time, and \(v_f\) is the final velocity at \(t=t_f\).

At the end of the acceleration period, the electron’s momentum is measured to be

\[ p_x = 1.82\times10^{-22}\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

  1. Use SymPy’s solve function to determine the final velocity \(v_f\).

  2. Plot the velocity–time curve \(v_x(t)\) from \(t=0\) to \(t=t_f\).

Problem solution

Step 1 — Momentum relation at final time

At \(t=t_f\), the velocity equals \(v_f\), so the electron’s momentum is

\[ p_x = m_e v_f. \]

We solve this equation symbolically for \(v_f\) using SymPy.

Step 2 — Solve for the final velocity

import sympy as sp

m_e = 9.11e-31
p_x = 1.82e-22

v_f = sp.symbols('v_f')

eq = sp.Eq(m_e*v_f, p_x)
sol = sp.solve(eq, v_f)[0]

print(f"{float(sol):.3e}")

This gives

\[ v_f \approx 2.00\times10^8\ \text{m/s}. \]

Step 3 — Define and plot the velocity–time function

The velocity increases linearly:

\[ v_x(t) = \frac{v_f}{t_f}\,t. \]

The following Python script plots \(v_x(t)\) from \(t=0\) to \(t=t_f\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t_f = 2.0e-9
v_f_val = float(sol)

t = np.linspace(0, t_f, 200)
v = (v_f_val/t_f)*t

plt.figure()
plt.plot(t, v)
plt.xlabel("time (s)")
plt.ylabel("velocity v_x (m/s)")
plt.title("Electron Velocity vs. Time")
plt.show()

Interpretation

  • The straight-line graph confirms constant acceleration.

  • The final velocity is a significant fraction of the speed of light, signaling that relativistic effects would be important in a more accurate treatment.

  • Here, we intentionally use the Newtonian momentum definition to focus on impulse, momentum, and computational tools.

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{v_f \approx 2.00\times10^8\ \text{m/s}} \]

Problem 16: Force–Time Plot, Impulse, and Final Velocity#

A point particle of mass

\[ m = 1.20\ \text{kg} \]

moves along the \(x\)-axis with an initial velocity

\[ v_{x,i} = -1.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

During a short interaction, the particle experiences a time-varying force over the interval

\[ 0 \le t \le 0.50\ \text{s}. \]

The force behaves as follows:

  • From \(t=0\) to \(t=0.20\ \text{s}\), the force is constant at \(+4.0\ \text{N}\).

  • From \(t=0.20\ \text{s}\) to \(t=0.50\ \text{s}\), the force decreases linearly from \(+4.0\ \text{N}\) to \(0\).

  1. Use Python to plot the force–time curve.

  2. Use the graph to calculate the impulse delivered to the particle.

  3. Determine the final velocity of the particle.

Problem solution

Step 1 — Force–time plot

The force consists of a rectangle followed by a triangle.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 0.50, 500)

f = np.where(t <= 0.20, 4.0, 4.0*(0.50 - t)/(0.30))

plt.figure()
plt.plot(t, f)
plt.xlabel("time (s)")
plt.ylabel("force $f_x$ (N)")
plt.title("Force vs. Time")
plt.show()

Step 2 — Impulse from the graph

Impulse equals the area under the force–time curve.

Rectangle area:

\[ j_{\text{rect}} = (4.0)(0.20) = 0.80\ \text{N·s}. \]

Triangle area:

\[ j_{\text{tri}} = \frac{1}{2}(0.30)(4.0) = 0.60\ \text{N·s}. \]

Total impulse:

\[ j_x = 0.80 + 0.60 = 1.40\ \text{N·s} = 1.40\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3 — Apply impulse–momentum theorem

Impulse–momentum theorem:

\[ j_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

Initial momentum:

\[ p_{x,i} = m v_{x,i} = (1.20)(-1.5) = -1.80\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final momentum:

\[ p_{x,f} = p_{x,i} + j_x = -1.80 + 1.40 = -0.40\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Final velocity:

\[ v_{x,f} = \frac{p_{x,f}}{m} = \frac{-0.40}{1.20} = -0.33\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • The positive impulse reduces the magnitude of the negative momentum.

  • The particle slows down but does not reverse direction.

  • The force–time graph provides a clear visual path from force to impulse to velocity.

Final Answers

\[ \boxed{j_x = 1.40\ \text{kg·m/s}},\qquad \boxed{v_{x,f} \approx -0.33\ \text{m/s}} \]

Problem 17: Force–Time Plot, Impulse, and Initial Velocity#

A point particle of mass

\[ m = 1.50\ \text{kg} \]

moves along the \(x\)-axis and experiences a time-varying force during a short interaction.

The force acts over the time interval \(0 \le t \le 0.40\ \text{s}\) and is described by:

  • The force increases linearly from \(0\) to \(+6.0\ \text{N}\) during the first \(0.20\ \text{s}\).

  • The force then decreases linearly back to \(0\) by \(t = 0.40\ \text{s}\).

After the interaction, the particle is observed to have a velocity

\[ v_{x,f} = +3.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

  1. Use Python to plot the force–time curve.

  2. Use the graph to calculate the impulse delivered to the particle.

  3. Determine the initial velocity of the particle.

Problem solution

Step 1 — Force–time model and plot

The force–time curve forms a triangle with:

  • base = \(0.40\ \text{s}\)

  • height = \(6.0\ \text{N}\)

Python script to plot the force:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 0.40, 400)
f = np.where(t <= 0.20, 30*t, 30*(0.40 - t))

plt.figure()
plt.plot(t, f)
plt.xlabel("time (s)")
plt.ylabel("force $f_x$ (N)")
plt.title("Force vs. Time")
plt.show()

Step 2 — Impulse from the graph

Impulse equals the area under the force–time curve:

\[ j_x = \frac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{height}) = \frac{1}{2}(0.40)(6.0) = 1.20\ \text{N·s}. \]

Using \(1\ \text{N·s} = 1\ \text{kg·m/s}\):

\[ j_x = 1.20\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Step 3 — Use impulse–momentum theorem

Impulse–momentum theorem:

\[ j_x = p_{x,f} - p_{x,i}. \]

Final momentum:

\[ p_{x,f} = m v_{x,f} = (1.50)(3.0) = 4.50\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Initial momentum:

\[ p_{x,i} = p_{x,f} - j_x = 4.50 - 1.20 = 3.30\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Initial velocity:

\[ v_{x,i} = \frac{p_{x,i}}{m} = \frac{3.30}{1.50} = 2.2\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • The area under the force–time curve determines the momentum transfer.

  • Knowing the final velocity allows us to work backward in time to find the initial velocity.

  • Different force shapes with the same area would produce the same impulse.

Final Answers

\[ \boxed{j_x = 1.20\ \text{kg·m/s}},\qquad \boxed{v_{x,i} = +2.2\ \text{m/s}} \]
