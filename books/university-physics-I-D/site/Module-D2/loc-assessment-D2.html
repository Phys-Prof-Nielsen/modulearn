Assessment Problem A1 — Scientific Notation#

A physicist measures the intensity of a faint light source and records a value of

\[ I = 0.000\,004\,52~\text{W/m}^2. \]

Task:
Write the intensity \(I\) in proper scientific notation with three significant figures.

Solution to Problem A1

We begin by identifying the first nonzero digit in
\(0.00000452\) → the 4 in the millionths place.

Move the decimal so the coefficient is between 1 and 10:

\[ 0.00000452 = 4.52 \times 10^{-6}. \]

The exponent is \(-6\) because we moved the decimal six places to the right.

  • The coefficient \(4.52\) has three significant figures.

  • The unit remains \(\text{W/m}^2\).

Final answer:

\[ I = 4.52 \times 10^{-6}~\text{W/m}^2. \]

Assessment Problem A2 — Orders of Magnitude#

A small drone has a battery that stores

\[ E = 72\,000~\text{J}. \]

A typical lightning strike releases approximately

\[ E_{\text{lightning}} \approx 1\times10^{9}~\text{J}. \]

Task:
Determine the order of magnitude of the ratio

\[ \dfrac{E}{E_{\text{lightning}}}, \]

and briefly state what this comparison means physically.

Solution to Problem A2

First convert the drone battery energy into scientific notation:

\[ E = 7.2\times10^{4}~\text{J}. \]

Compute the ratio:

\[ \frac{E}{E_{\text{lightning}}} = \frac{7.2\times10^{4}}{1\times10^{9}} = 7.2\times10^{-5}. \]

Order-of-magnitude rounding Order-of-magnitude rounding uses a logarithmic midpoint at

\[3.16 \approx \sqrt{10}.\]

  • If the coefficient is below 3.16, we round down.

  • If the coefficient is above 3.16, we round up.

Because:

\[ 7.2 > 3.16, \]

the ratio is closer to

\[ 10^{-4} \quad\text{than}\quad 10^{-5}. \]

Final order of magnitude

\[ \boxed{10^{-4}} \]

Interpretation A lightning strike releases roughly

\[10^{4} = 10{,}000\]

times more energy than the drone battery.
Equivalently, the drone battery holds only about one ten-thousandth of the energy in a single lightning strike.

Assessment Problem A3 — Significant Figures (Multiplication & Division)#

A metal rod has a measured diameter

\[ d = 0.86~\text{cm}. \]

You want to compute the cross-sectional area assuming the rod is cylindrical. Task:
Compute the area \(A\) and report the final answer with the correct number of significant figures.

Use \(\pi = 3.14159\) (exact for sig-fig purposes).

Solution to Problem A3

We begin by identifying significant figures:

  • Diameter \(d = 0.86\) has 2 significant figures, and it will determine the sig figs of the final result.

  • \(\pi\) is treated as an exact mathematical constant (infinite sig figs).

Step 1: Compute the radius

\[ r = \frac{d}{2} = \frac{0.86}{2} = 0.43~\text{cm}. \]

The intermediate value 0.43 has 2 significant figures, consistent with the input.

Step 2: Compute the area

\[ A = \pi r^2 = 3.14159 \times (0.43)^2. \]

and we get $\( A = 0.5808~\text{cm}^2. \)$

Step 3: Apply significant-figure rules

Since the diameter had 2 significant figures, the final answer must also have 2 significant figures.

Round \(0.5808\) to 2 sig figs:

  • First two sig figs: \(0.58\)

  • Next digit: \(0\) (so we do not round up)

Final rounded area:

\[ A = 0.58~\text{cm}^2. \]

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{A = 0.58~\text{cm}^2} \]

Assessment Problem A4 — Significant Figures (Addition & Subtraction)#

In a timing experiment, a student measures how long a cart takes to travel three segments of a track:

  • Segment 1: \(t_1 = 1.204~\text{s}\)

  • Segment 2: \(t_2 = 0.83~\text{s}\)

  • Segment 3: \(t_3 = 2.1150~\text{s}\)

The total travel time is

\[ T = t_1 + t_2 + t_3. \]

Task:
Compute \(T\) and report the final answer with the correct number of digits after the decimal point (addition/subtraction rule).

Solution to Problem A4

1. Identify decimal precision (not just sig figs)

For addition and subtraction, the rule is:

The final answer must be rounded to the least precise decimal place among the terms.

Check each time:

  • \(t_1 = 1.204~\text{s}\) → thousandths place (3 digits after decimal)

  • \(t_2 = 0.83~\text{s}\) → hundredths place (2 digits after decimal)

  • \(t_3 = 2.1150~\text{s}\) → ten-thousandths place (4 digits after decimal)

The least precise is \(t_2\) (hundredths place),
so the final answer must be rounded to the hundredths place.

2. Add the times

First, add them normally (keep extra digits during the calculation):

\[ T = t_1 + t_2 + t_3 = 1.204 + 0.83 + 2.1150. \]

Compute step by step:

\[ 1.204 + 0.83 = 2.034 \]
\[ 2.034 + 2.1150 = 4.1490 \]

So the raw sum is:

\[ T = 4.1490~\text{s}. \]

3. Apply the decimal-place rule

We must report \(T\) to the hundredths place, i.e., two digits after the decimal.

  • \(4.1490 \rightarrow 4.15\) (because the third decimal digit is 9, so we round up)

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{T = 4.15~\text{s}} \]

This follows the addition/subtraction rule: match the least precise decimal place (here, the hundredths from \(t_2\)).

Assessment Problem A5 — Basic Statistics (Mean, Standard Deviation, SEM)#

A student times one full oscillation of a pendulum five times and records:

  • \(T_1 = 2.01~\text{s}\)

  • \(T_2 = 1.98~\text{s}\)

  • \(T_3 = 2.05~\text{s}\)

  • \(T_4 = 2.00~\text{s}\)

  • \(T_5 = 1.99~\text{s}\)

Tasks:

  1. Compute the mean period \(\bar{T}\).

  2. Compute the sample standard deviation \(s\) of the measurements.

  3. Compute the standard error of the mean (SEM), \(s_{\bar{T}} = s / \sqrt{N}\), where \(N\) is the number of measurements.

  4. Report the period in the two common formats:

    • As a single measurement with spread: \(\bar{T} \pm s\)

    • As a mean with precision: \(\bar{T} \pm s_{\bar{T}}\)
      Use a sensible number of significant figures in each.

Solution to Problem A5

1. Mean period \(\bar{T}\)

Add the times:

\[ 2.01 + 1.98 + 2.05 + 2.00 + 1.99 = 10.03~\text{s}. \]

Number of measurements: \(N = 5\).

Mean:

\[ \bar{T} = \frac{10.03}{5} = 2.006~\text{s}. \]

The raw mean is \(2.006~\text{s}\). Since the original data are given to the hundredths place, it is reasonable to report the mean to the hundredths place as well:

\[ \bar{T} \approx 2.01~\text{s}. \]

2. Sample standard deviation \(s\)

Formula for the sample standard deviation:

\[ s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N} (T_i - \bar{T})^2}. \]

Using the raw mean \(2.006~\text{s}\), one finds (by calculator or Python):

\[ s \approx 0.0270~\text{s}. \]

We typically keep 1–2 significant figures in the standard deviation. Here we’ll report:

\[ s \approx 0.03~\text{s}. \]

3. Standard error of the mean (SEM)

The standard error of the mean is

\[ s_{\bar{T}} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}. \]

With \(s \approx 0.0270~\text{s}\) and \(N = 5\):

\[ s_{\bar{T}} = \frac{0.0270}{\sqrt{5}} \approx \frac{0.0270}{2.24} \approx 0.012~\text{s}. \]

Again, 1–2 significant figures is typical. Since we report the period the hundredths:

\[ s_{\bar{T}} \approx 0.01~\text{s}. \]

4. Reporting the result

As a single measurement with spread (shows scatter of the data):

\[ \bar{T} \pm s \approx (2.01 \pm 0.03)~\text{s}. \]

This says: individual measurements typically vary by about \(0.03~\text{s}\) around the mean.

As a mean with precision (shows how well the mean is known):

\[ \bar{T} \pm s_{\bar{T}} \approx (2.01 \pm 0.01)~\text{s}. \]

This says: based on 5 trials, the best estimate of the true period is \(2.01~\text{s}\) with an uncertainty of about \(0.01~\text{s}\) in the mean.

(Optional) Python check

You can verify your calculations with a short Python snippet:

import numpy as np

T = np.array([2.01, 1.98, 2.05, 2.00, 1.99])

T_mean = np.mean(T)
s = np.std(T, ddof=1)          # sample standard deviation
sem = s / np.sqrt(len(T))

print(f"Mean T: {T_mean:.3f} s")
print(f"Sample std dev s: {s:.3f} s")
print(f"SEM: {sem:.3f} s")

Expected output:

Mean T: 2.006 s
Sample std dev s: 0.027 s
SEM: 0.012 s

Assessment Problem A6 — Unit Conversions (Factor–Label Method)#

A student weighs 150. lb on a bathroom scale (in pounds-force, lb).
We want to find their weight in newtons (SI unit of force).

Use the following information:

  • \(1~\text{lb} = 4.448~\text{N}\)

  • \(1~\text{N} = 1~\text{kg·m/s}^2\)

Task:
Convert the student’s weight from pounds to newtons using the factor–label method.
Show the intermediate step with the conversion factor written as a “number 1,” and report the final answer with the correct number of significant figures.

Optionally, verify your result with a short Python calculation.

Solution to Problem A6

1. Identify given and needed quantities

Given:

  • Measured weight: \(W = 150.~\text{lb}\)

We want:

  • \(W\) in newtons (N).

Conversion factor (our “number 1”):

\[ 1 = \frac{4.448~\text{N}}{1~\text{lb}} \]

This equals 1 because \(4.448~\text{N}\) and \(1~\text{lb}\) represent the same physical force.

2. Set up the factor–label conversion

Start with the given value and multiply by the conversion factor:

\[ W = 150.~\text{lb} \times \frac{4.448~\text{N}}{1~\text{lb}}. \]

Units:

  • \(\text{lb}\) in numerator and denominator cancel, leaving newtons.

Numerically:

\[ W = 150. \times 4.448~\text{N}. \]

Compute the product:

\[ W = 667.2~\text{N}. \]

3. Significant figures

The given weight is \(150.\) with a decimal point, which we interpret as 3 significant figures.

  • Conversion factor \(4.448\) has 4 significant figures.

  • The result is limited by the fewest significant figures: 3 sig figs.

So we round \(667.2\) to 3 significant figures:

  • \(667.2 \rightarrow 667~\text{N}\) (since the next digit is 2, we do not round up).

Final Answer

\[ \boxed{W \approx 667~\text{N}} \]

The student’s weight is about \(667~\text{N}\) in SI units.

4. Optional Python check

You can verify with a simple Python snippet:

W_lb = 150.0
conv = 4.448      # N per lb

W_N = W_lb * conv

print(f"Weight in newtons (raw): {W_N:.4f} N")
print(f"Weight in newtons (3 sig figs): {W_N:.3g} N")

Expected output:

Weight in newtons (raw): 667.2000 N
Weight in newtons (3 sig figs): 667 N

Assessment Problem A7 — Basic Graphing & Trend Interpretation#

A student measures the cooling of a cup of hot tea as it sits on a desk.
The measured temperatures are:

Time \(t\) (min)

Temperature \(T\) (°C)

0

92

5

78

10

67

15

58

20

51

25

46

Tasks

  1. Using Python, create a clear plot of temperature \(T\) vs. time \(t\).

    • Label axes with units.

    • Use a marker such as 'o' for each data point.

    • Add a descriptive title.

  2. Describe, in one or two sentences, the overall trend you observe in the data.

  3. Estimate from the graph the temperature at 30 minutes (no calculation required — just read from the trend).

Solution to Problem A6

1. Python code (all in one Jupyter cell)

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# data
t = np.array([0, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25])
T = np.array([92, 78, 67, 58, 51, 46])

# plot
plt.figure(figsize=(6,4))
plt.plot(t, T, 'o-', label='Cooling Data')
plt.xlabel('Time t (min)')
plt.ylabel('Temperature T (°C)')
plt.title('Cooling Curve of Hot Tea')
plt.grid(True)
plt.legend()
plt.show()

2. Trend description

The temperature decreases steadily over time, dropping quickly at first and then more slowly.
This is consistent with typical cooling behavior governed by Newton’s law of cooling.

3. Estimate at \(t = 30\) minutes

From the plotted trend, the temperature at 30 minutes is approximately 42–44°C.
