D4.0 Learning Outcomes — Work–Energy Framework#

Why this module matters#

In everyday language, work is often described in terms of effort, fatigue, or how hard something feels to do.
In physics, these intuitive ideas are formalized through the concepts of energy, work, and power.

This module develops the work–energy framework step by step. We begin by introducing kinetic energy as a scalar property associated with motion. We then define work as the mechanism by which energy is transferred through interactions that act over a distance. These ideas come together naturally in the work–energy theorem, which connects interactions to changes in motion without relying on acceleration-based reasoning.

After establishing this foundation, we introduce gravitational potential energy as a stored form of energy associated with position. This allows us to distinguish between conservative interactions, which store energy, and non-conservative interactions, which transfer energy into or out of mechanical energy. These ideas are unified through the concept of mechanical energy, which provides a powerful bookkeeping tool for understanding motion.

We conclude the module with power, which describes how quickly energy is transferred.

Throughout this module, all motion and interactions are treated in one dimension, with direction handled using signed quantities. Energy itself remains a scalar, allowing us to focus on physical interpretation, energy transfer, and conservation rather than mathematical complexity.

Learning Outcomes#

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

A. Kinetic Energy as a Property of Motion#

Establishes kinetic energy as the foundational energy quantity.

  • A1. Describe kinetic energy as a scalar physical property associated with the motion of an object.

  • A2. Define kinetic energy in one dimension as

    \[ k = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \]

  • A3. Explain why kinetic energy depends on the magnitude of velocity, not its direction.

  • A4. Interpret changes in kinetic energy as indicators of energy transfer.

B. Work as Energy Transfer#

Introduces work as the mechanism that changes kinetic energy.

  • B1. Define work as energy transferred by a force acting through a displacement.

  • B2. Calculate work done by a constant force in one dimension using

    \[ w = f_x\,\Delta x \]

  • B3. Use the sign of work to distinguish between energy added to or removed from a particle.

  • B4. Interpret work physically as a process that changes an object’s kinetic energy.

C. Work–Energy Theorem#

Connects interactions to changes in motion.

  • C1. State and apply the work–energy theorem,

    \[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \]

  • C2. Interpret net work as the combined effect of multiple interactions acting over a distance.

  • C3. Use the work–energy theorem to predict changes in speed without using acceleration or kinematic equations.

D. Gravitational Potential Energy#

Introduces stored energy associated with position.

  • D1. Describe gravitational potential energy as energy associated with vertical position near Earth’s surface.

  • D2. Use the near-Earth expression

    \[ u_g = m g y \]

    without formal derivation.

  • D3. Relate the work done by gravity to changes in gravitational potential energy using

    \[ w_g = -\Delta u_g. \]

E. Mechanical Energy and Energy Accounting#

Unifies kinetic and potential energy.

  • E1. Define mechanical energy as

    \[ e_{\text{mech}} = k + u. \]

  • E2. Distinguish between conservative and non-conservative forces based on how they affect mechanical energy.

  • E3. Apply the mechanical-energy form of the work–energy theorem,

    \[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc}, \]

    to analyze motion.

  • E4. Identify conditions under which mechanical energy is conserved.

F. Power as the Rate of Energy Transfer#

Connects energy ideas to performance and real systems.

  • F1. Define power as the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.

  • F2. Calculate average power using

    \[ p_{\text{avg}} = \frac{w}{\Delta t}. \]

  • F3. Interpret power physically as how quickly energy is transferred, not how much energy is involved.

Performance Criteria#

  • Energy quantities are treated consistently as scalars with clear physical interpretation.

  • Work, energy, and power are used in a coherent conceptual hierarchy.

  • Directional reasoning is handled through signed forces and displacements, not vector formalism.

  • Conservative and non-conservative interactions are clearly distinguished.

  • Explanations emphasize energy transfer, storage, and transformation, not memorized formulas.
