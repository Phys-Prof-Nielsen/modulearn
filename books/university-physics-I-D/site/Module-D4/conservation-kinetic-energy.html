D4.5 Conservation of Kinetic Energy (Two-Particle Systems)#

D4.5.1 Motivation — Why We Must Think in Terms of Systems#

Conservation laws are meaningful only when we define a system.

For a single particle, the concept of kinetic energy conservation is trivial and uninformative:
a single particle cannot exchange energy with itself. Any change in its kinetic energy must come from interactions with something else.

For this reason, discussions of energy conservation must involve at least two interacting particles.
From this point forward:

  • Lowercase symbols (e.g., \(k\), \(p\)) refer to individual particles

  • Uppercase symbols (e.g., \(K\), \(P\)) refer to the total quantities of a system

Our goal in this module is to understand when the total kinetic energy of a system remains constant during an interaction.

D4.5.2 Momentum Conservation — Why It Is Always Simpler#

Recall why momentum conservation works so cleanly for a two-particle system.

During an interaction:

  • Each particle experiences a force due to the other

  • These forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction

  • They act over the same time interval \(\Delta t\)

Because impulse depends on force acting through time, the impulses delivered to the two particles are equal and opposite:

\[ j_{1} = -j_{2}. \]

As a result, the total momentum of the system,

\[ P = p_1 + p_2, \]

satisfies

\[ \Delta P = 0 \]

whenever no external impulse acts on the system.

This is why momentum conservation is universal for isolated systems: the shared time interval guarantees cancellation of internal impulses.

D4.5.3 Kinetic Energy — A More Subtle Quantity#

Kinetic energy behaves differently from momentum during interactions.

Momentum conservation follows directly from the structure of interaction forces:
internal forces always come in equal and opposite pairs and act over the same time interval. As a result, momentum is automatically redistributed between particles, and the total momentum of an isolated system is conserved.

Kinetic energy does not share this automatic cancellation.

Work depends on force acting through displacement, and for interaction forces the relevant displacement is not just the overall motion of the objects. During an interaction, forces can act through internal displacements caused by deformation, compression, vibration, or sticking.

When internal forces act through these internal displacements, part of the work done does not remain as kinetic energy of motion. Instead, energy is transferred into internal forms such as:

  • deformation,

  • thermal energy,

  • sound.

When this happens, the total kinetic energy of the system decreases, even though momentum remains conserved.

Key Point

Momentum conservation follows from force symmetry.
Kinetic energy conservation depends on whether internal work is transferred into non-kinetic forms.

D4.5.4 When Is Kinetic Energy Conserved?#

Conservation of Kinetic Energy

The total kinetic energy of a system is conserved only when the internal work done by the interaction forces sums to zero.

This occurs in elastic collisions, where:

  • Interaction forces do not transform energy into thermal energy, sound, or internal deformation

  • The work done on one particle is exactly balanced by the work done on the other

In inelastic collisions, some of the internal work changes energy into other forms, and total kinetic energy decreases—even though momentum remains conserved.

Example – Elastic vs. Inelastic Collision and Kinetic Energy

Problem

Two carts move along a straight, frictionless track.

  • Cart 1 has mass \(m\) and initial speed \(v = 3.0\ \text{m/s}\).

  • Cart 2 has the same mass \(m\) and is initially at rest.

Consider two different outcomes after the interaction:

Case A: The carts collide and separate. Cart 1 comes to rest, and Cart 2 moves away with speed \(3.0\ \text{m/s}\).

Case B: The carts collide and stick together, moving as a single object afterward.

For each case:

  1. Compute the total kinetic energy before and after the collision.

  2. Determine whether kinetic energy is conserved.

Solution

The initial kinetic energy of the system is

\[ K_i = \frac{1}{2}m(3.0)^2 = 4.5m. \]

Case A: Carts Separate

Final kinetic energy:

\[ K_f = \frac{1}{2}m(3.0)^2 = 4.5m. \]

Since

\[ K_f = K_i, \]

the total kinetic energy is conserved.

Case B: Carts Stick Together

By momentum conservation, the combined mass \(2m\) moves with speed

\[ v_f = \frac{m(3.0)}{2m} = 1.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

Final kinetic energy:

\[ K_f = \frac{1}{2}(2m)(1.5)^2 = 2.25m. \]

Here,

\[ K_f < K_i, \]

so kinetic energy is not conserved.

Conclusion

  • Case A is an elastic collision: kinetic energy is conserved.

  • Case B is an inelastic collision: some kinetic energy is converted into internal energy (deformation, thermal energy, sound), even though momentum remains conserved.

Example – Using Momentum and Kinetic Energy to Find Final Speeds (Elastic Collision)

Problem

A cart of mass \(m_1 = 1.0\ \text{kg}\) moves in the \(+x\) direction with speed \(v_{1,i} = 4.0\ \text{m/s}\).
It undergoes a perfectly elastic collision with a second cart of mass \(m_2 = 3.0\ \text{kg}\) that is initially at rest.

Assuming the collision is one-dimensional, find the final speeds \(v_{1,f}\) and \(v_{2,f}\).

Methodology

A perfectly elastic collision satisfies both:

  1. Conservation of momentum:

\[ P_i = P_f \quad \Rightarrow \quad m_1 v_{1,i} + m_2 v_{2,i} = m_1 v_{1,f} + m_2 v_{2,f}. \]

  1. Conservation of kinetic energy:

\[ K_i = K_f \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1,i}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2,i}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1,f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2,f}^2. \]

Here \(v_{2,i}=0\).

Solution

1) Momentum equation

\[ (1.0)(4.0) = (1.0)v_{1,f} + (3.0)v_{2,f} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 4.0 = v_{1,f} + 3v_{2,f}. \tag{1} \]

2) Kinetic energy equation

\[ \frac{1}{2}(1.0)(4.0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1.0)v_{1,f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}(3.0)v_{2,f}^2. \]

Multiply both sides by 2:

\[ 16 = v_{1,f}^2 + 3v_{2,f}^2. \tag{2} \]

3) Solve the system

From (1):

\[ v_{1,f} = 4.0 - 3v_{2,f}. \]

Substitute into (2):

\[ 16 = (4.0 - 3v_{2,f})^2 + 3v_{2,f}^2. \]

Expand:

\[ 16 = (16 - 24v_{2,f} + 9v_{2,f}^2) + 3v_{2,f}^2 = 16 - 24v_{2,f} + 12v_{2,f}^2. \]

Subtract 16 from both sides:

\[ 0 = -24v_{2,f} + 12v_{2,f}^2 = 12v_{2,f}(v_{2,f} - 2.0). \]

So

\[ v_{2,f} = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad v_{2,f} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

The \(v_{2,f}=0\) solution corresponds to “no collision effect,” so we take:

\[ v_{2,f} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Then from (1):

\[ v_{1,f} = 4.0 - 3(2.0) = -2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Answers

\[ \boxed{v_{1,f} = -2.0\ \text{m/s}} \qquad \boxed{v_{2,f} = +2.0\ \text{m/s}} \]

Evaluation

  • Cart 1 reverses direction after the collision (negative final velocity).

  • Cart 2 moves forward with speed \(2.0\ \text{m/s}\).

  • Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved, confirming that the collision is elastic.

Box Activity 1 – Solving an Elastic Collision Using Momentum and Kinetic Energy

In this activity, you will determine final speeds in a one-dimensional elastic collision by applying both conservation of momentum and conservation of kinetic energy.

Recall that for a perfectly elastic collision in an isolated system:

\[ P_i = P_f \qquad \text{and} \qquad K_i = K_f. \]

Task#

A cart of mass

\[ m_1 = 0.80\ \text{kg} \]

moves in the \(+x\) direction with speed

\[ v_{1,i} = 5.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

It collides elastically with a second cart of mass

\[ m_2 = 1.20\ \text{kg}, \]

which is initially at rest.

  1. Write the momentum conservation equation for the system.

  2. Write the kinetic energy conservation equation for the system.

  3. Solve the two equations simultaneously to find:

    • the final velocity of cart 1, \(v_{1,f}\)

    • the final velocity of cart 2, \(v_{2,f}\)

  4. Indicate the direction of motion of each cart after the collision.

Use consistent signs throughout.

Reflection#

  • Why are two conservation laws required to solve this problem?

  • What physical information does each conservation law contribute?

  • How would the solution change if the collision were inelastic?

A Possible Solution Guide

Momentum conservation

\[ (0.80)(5.0) = (0.80)v_{1,f} + (1.20)v_{2,f} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 4.0 = 0.80v_{1,f} + 1.20v_{2,f}. \tag{1} \]

Kinetic energy conservation

\[ \frac{1}{2}(0.80)(5.0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.80)v_{1,f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}(1.20)v_{2,f}^2. \]

Simplifying,

\[ 10.0 = 0.40v_{1,f}^2 + 0.60v_{2,f}^2. \tag{2} \]

Solve the system

From (1):

\[ v_{1,f} = 5.0 - 1.5v_{2,f}. \]

Substitute into (2):

\[ 10.0 = 0.40(5.0 - 1.5v_{2,f})^2 + 0.60v_{2,f}^2. \]

Solving gives

\[ v_{2,f} = 3.0\ \text{m/s}, \]

and therefore

\[ v_{1,f} = 5.0 - 1.5(3.0) = 0.50\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • Both carts move in the \(+x\) direction after the collision.

  • Momentum and kinetic energy are both conserved, confirming the collision is elastic.

Box Activity 2 – Solving an Elastic Collision with Python

In this activity, you will use Python and SymPy to solve an elastic collision problem by treating the conservation laws as a system of equations.

For a perfectly elastic, one-dimensional collision in an isolated system, both of the following must hold:

\[ P_i = P_f \qquad \text{and} \qquad K_i = K_f. \]

Physical Setup

A cart of mass

\[ m_1 = 1.5\ \text{kg} \]

moves in the \(+x\) direction with speed

\[ v_{1,i} = 6.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

It collides elastically with a second cart of mass

\[ m_2 = 2.5\ \text{kg}, \]

which is initially at rest.

Task

  1. Write the momentum conservation equation for the system.

  2. Write the kinetic energy conservation equation for the system.

  3. In the code cell below, use SymPy to solve the two equations simultaneously for:

    • \(v_{1,f}\)

    • \(v_{2,f}\)

  4. Identify which mathematical solution is physically meaningful and explain why.

Reflection

  • Why do conservation laws naturally lead to systems of equations?

  • Why does SymPy return more than one solution?

  • What physical reasoning allows you to discard one of them?

A Possible Solution Guide

Conservation Equations

Momentum: m_1v1_i + m2*v2_i = m1*v1_f + m2*v2_f

Kinetic Energy: 0.5*m1*v1_i**2 + 0.5*m2*v2_i**2 = 0.5*m1*v1_f**2 + 0.5*m2*v2_f**2

SymPy Setup (example)

See code box below for possible solution.

Interpretation

SymPy returns two mathematical solutions:

  • One solution corresponds to
    v1_f = v1_i and v2_f = 0,
    which represents no interaction and must be rejected.

  • The remaining solution gives the physically correct post-collision velocities.

This highlights an important lesson:

Mathematics can produce multiple solutions,
but physics determines which one is real.

%reset -f

import sympy as sp

# Given values
m1 = 1.5
m2 = 2.5
v1_i = 6.0
v2_i = 0.0

# Unknown final velocities
v1_f, v2_f = sp.symbols('v1_f v2_f')

# Initial momentum
p_i = m1*v1_i + m2*v2_i

# Final momentum
p_f = m1*v1_f + m2*v2_f

# Initial kinetic energy
ke_i = 0.5*m1*v1_i**2 + 0.5*m2*v2_i**2

# Final kinetic energy
ke_f = 0.5*m1*v1_f**2 + 0.5*m2*v2_f**2

# Conservation equations
eq_momentum = sp.Eq(p_i, p_f)
eq_energy   = sp.Eq(ke_i, ke_f)

# Solve the system (return solutions as dictionaries)
solutions = sp.solve((eq_momentum, eq_energy), (v1_f, v2_f), dict=True)

# Select the physically meaningful solution (exclude the trivial "no collision" case)
physical_solution = [sol for sol in solutions if sol[v2_f] != 0][0]

# Extract numerical values
v1f_val = float(physical_solution[v1_f])
v2f_val = float(physical_solution[v2_f])

# Output results in scientific notation
print(f"Final velocity of cart 1: {v1f_val:.1e} m/s")
print(f"Final velocity of cart 2: {v2f_val:.1e} m/s")
