D4.3 Work–Energy: Force Acting Through Displacement#

Motivation — Newton’s First Law Revisited (Energy View)#

Newton’s First Law

An object continues in its state of motion unless acted upon by an unbalanced interaction.

From an energy perspective, this statement can be interpreted as follows:

  • If the motion of an object changes, then its kinetic energy must have changed.

  • If the motion does not change, then the net change in kinetic energy must be zero.

Seen this way, Newton’s First Law is not fundamentally a statement about force. It is a statement about the absence of energy transfer.

This perspective will guide how we now connect force to work.

D4.3.1 Work Due to a Single Force#

In earlier section, we defined work purely in terms of its effect on motion:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k. \]

This definition emphasized that work is fundamentally about energy transfer, not force.

We now ask the natural next question:

What physical mechanism causes a change in kinetic energy?

From Energy Change to Physical Cause#

In some situations, an interaction is not most naturally described by how long it acts, but by how far it acts.
For example, we may know that a box is pushed with a constant force across a floor through a known distance, without knowing how long the push lasted.

This motivates a complementary way of describing energy transfer.

Key Concept: Work as Energy Transfer

Work is the transfer of energy caused by a force acting through a displacement.

This statement plays the same role for energy that force–time played for momentum:

  • Impulse records interactions through time

  • Work records interactions through distance

Definition of Linear Work (Single Force)#

When a single force acts on a point particle and causes it to move through a displacement, the energy transfer can be quantified directly.

For a constant force, or when a force can be reasonably replaced by its average value, the work done by that force is

Linear Work on a Point Particle

\[ w = f_x\,\Delta x \tag{1} \]

Here,

  • \(f_x\) is the force acting along the \(x\)-direction, and

  • \(\Delta x\) is the directed displacement of the particle during the interaction.

The displacement \(\Delta x\) represents a distance traveled with direction.
Using our current notation, it could equivalently be written as \(d_x\), emphasizing that displacement carries a sign just like velocity and force.
The use of \(\Delta x\) highlights the parallel with the impulse expression \(f_x\,\Delta t\).

This expression makes an important physical point:

A large force acting over a short distance can transfer the same amount of energy as a smaller force acting over a longer distance.

Sign of Work#

Because both force and displacement are directed quantities in one dimension, the sign of the work has clear physical meaning:

  • If the force and displacement are in the same direction, the work is positive.

  • If the force and displacement are in opposite directions, the work is negative.

The sign tells us how energy is transferred:

  • Positive work: energy is transferred to the particle.

  • Negative work: energy is transferred from the particle.

Units of Work#

From the definition,

\[ [w] = \text{N·m}, \]

since work is the product of force and displacement.

Because work represents energy transfer, this unit is defined as the joule (J):

\[ 1\ \text{N·m} = 1\ \text{J}. \]

This confirms the consistency of our definitions:
work and energy share the same unit because they describe the same physical quantity from different perspectives. We must emphasize though, that work is not a form of energy, it is a transfer of energy.

D4.3.2 Net Work from Multiple Forces#

In many situations, a point particle experiences more than one force while moving through a displacement.
Each force may transfer energy to or from the particle.

As with impulse, the most general and physically correct approach is to start with work itself, not force.

Net Work — General Statement#

The net work done on a point particle is the sum of the work done by each individual force:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \sum w. \]

Each individual contribution depends on:

  • the force involved, and

  • the displacement over which that force acts.

For a single force acting over a displacement \(\Delta x\),

\[ w = f_x\,\Delta x \quad \text{(constant or average force)}. \]

Putting this together,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \sum (f_x\,\Delta x)_i. \]

Important Caution

The displacement over which each force acts does not have to be the same.

Some forces may act only over part of the motion (such as friction or contact forces),
while others may act over the entire displacement (such as gravity).

You must therefore never assume that forces can be added first and multiplied by a single displacement unless you have verified that they act over the same distance.

Special Case — Forces Acting Over the Same Displacement#

In simplified situations, all forces act over the same displacement \(\Delta x\).
When this is true, the net work simplifies to

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \left( \sum f_x \right)\Delta x. \]

Defining the net force as

\[ f_{\text{net},x} = \sum f_x, \]

we obtain

\[ w_{\text{net}} = f_{\text{net},x}\,\Delta x. \]

This form is convenient, but it is valid only when all contributing forces act over the same displacement.

Work–Energy Theorem (1D)

The net work done on a point particle equals the change in its kinetic energy:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k. \]

This relation connects forces acting through displacement to changes in motion.

Example – Verifying the Work–Energy Theorem

Problem

A \(0.50\ \text{kg}\) cart initially moves at \(2.0\ \text{m/s}\) along a straight track.
A constant force of \(f_x = +2.0\ \text{N}\) acts on the cart over a displacement of \(4.0\ \text{m}\) in the direction of motion.

The cart’s speed at the end of this displacement is \(6.0\ \text{m/s}\).

Verify that the work done by the force equals the change in kinetic energy.

Solution

1. Work done by the force

Since the force is constant and acts in the direction of motion, the work done is

\[ w = f_x\,\Delta x = (2.0)(4.0) = 8.0\ \text{J}. \]

2. Change in kinetic energy

Initial kinetic energy:

\[ k_i = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)(2.0)^2 = 1.0\ \text{J}. \]

Final kinetic energy:

\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)(6.0)^2 = 9.0\ \text{J}. \]

Change in kinetic energy:

\[ \Delta k = k_f - k_i = 9.0 - 1.0 = 8.0\ \text{J}. \]

Conclusion

\[ w = \Delta k = 8.0\ \text{J}. \]

The work done by the force exactly equals the change in kinetic energy, verifying the work–energy theorem for this situation.

Example – Net Work from Multiple Forces and Resulting Speed

Problem

A box is pushed across a horizontal floor by a constant pushing force of \(25\ \text{N}\). A frictional force of \(5.0\ \text{N}\) acts on the box in the opposite direction.

The box moves a distance of \(12\ \text{m}\) and starts from rest.

  1. How much net work is done on the box by the net force?

  2. How fast is the box moving after it has traveled this distance?

Assume the mass of the box is \(m = 10\ \text{kg}\).

Methodology

First determine the net force acting on the box and compute the net work using

\[ w_{\text{net}} = f_{\text{net},x}\,\Delta x. \]

Then use the work–energy theorem

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \]

to determine the final speed of the box.

Solution

Let the direction of motion be the positive \(x\)-direction.

  • Pushing force: \(+25\ \text{N}\)

  • Frictional force: \(-5.0\ \text{N}\)

The net force is

\[ f_{\text{net},x} = (+25) + (-5.0) = +20\ \text{N}. \]

1. Net work

The net work done over the displacement \(\Delta x = 12\ \text{m}\) is

\[ w_{\text{net}} = f_{\text{net},x}\,\Delta x = (20)(12) = 240\ \text{J}. \]

2. Final speed

Since the box starts from rest, its initial kinetic energy is

\[ k_i = 0. \]

From the work–energy theorem,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k = k_f - k_i = k_f. \]

Thus,

\[ k_f = 240\ \text{J}. \]

Solving for the final speed:

\[ k_f = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \sqrt{\frac{2k_f}{m}}. \]

Substituting values,

\[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2(240)}{10}} = \sqrt{48} \approx 6.9\ \text{m/s}. \]

Answers

\[ \boxed{w_{\text{net}} = 2.4 \times 10^{2}\ \text{J}} \]
\[ \boxed{v \approx 6.9\ \text{m/s}} \]

Evaluation

The net force acts in the same direction as the displacement, so the work is positive.
As a result, energy is transferred to the box, increasing its kinetic energy and causing it to speed up from rest.

Box Activity 1 – Work from Force Acting Through Displacement

In this activity, you will connect force acting through displacement to energy transfer, using the work–energy framework.

Recall the two equivalent descriptions of work:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k \qquad \text{and} \qquad w = f_x\,\Delta x. \]

Important Setup Condition

In this activity, all forces act on the particle over the same displacement \(\Delta x\).
This allows us to compute net work using the net force:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = f_{\text{net},x}\,\Delta x. \]

Task: Complete the following steps.

  1. Given quantities

    A point particle has mass

    \[ m = 2.0\ \text{kg}. \]

    The particle starts from rest and moves along the \(+x\) direction.

  2. Forces acting on the particle

    All of the following forces act throughout the entire displacement:

    • Applied force: \(f_{\text{app}} = +6.0\ \text{N}\)

    • Resistive force: \(f_{\text{res}} = -2.0\ \text{N}\)

  3. Displacement

    The particle moves a distance

    \[ \Delta x = 5.0\ \text{m}. \]

  4. Compute the net force

    Determine the net force \(f_{\text{net},x}\) acting on the particle.

  5. Compute the net work

    Use

    \[ w_{\text{net}} = f_{\text{net},x}\,\Delta x \]

    to find the net work done on the particle.

  6. Connect to kinetic energy

    Using the work–energy theorem,

    \[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k, \]

    determine the final speed of the particle.

  7. Reflect

    • Why is it valid here to combine the forces before computing work?

    • What would you need to do differently if the forces acted over different distances?

    • Does the result depend on how long the forces acted?

A Possible Solution Guide

Step 4: Net force

\[ f_{\text{net},x} = (+6.0) + (-2.0) = +4.0\ \text{N}. \]

Step 5: Net work

Because both forces act over the same displacement,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = (4.0)(5.0) = 20\ \text{J}. \]

Step 6: Final speed

Since the particle starts from rest, \(k_i = 0\), so

\[ k_f = w_{\text{net}} = 20\ \text{J}. \]

Using \(k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\),

\[ 20 = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)v^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v^2 = 20 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v \approx 4.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • The net work is positive, so energy is transferred to the particle.

  • Combining forces is valid here only because they act over the same displacement.

  • The calculation depends on displacement, not time.

Box Activity 2 – Work When Forces Act Over Different Displacements

In many real situations, different forces do not act over the same displacement.
In such cases, it is not valid to combine forces first and multiply by a single distance.

Instead, work must be computed force by force, and then summed.

Recall the general definition:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \sum w_i = \sum (f_{x,i}\,\Delta x_i). \]

Important Setup Condition

In this activity, each force acts over a different displacement.
You must therefore compute the work done by each force separately.

Task: Complete the following steps.

  1. Given quantities

    A box of mass

    \[ m = 4.0\ \text{kg} \]

    starts from rest and moves along the \(+x\) direction.

  2. Forces acting on the box

    • A pushing force

      \[ f_{\text{push}} = +10\ \text{N} \]

      acts over the first

      \[ \Delta x_1 = 6.0\ \text{m}. \]

    • A frictional force

      \[ f_{\text{fric}} = -4.0\ \text{N} \]

      acts over the entire motion, for a total displacement

      \[ \Delta x_2 = 10.0\ \text{m}. \]

  3. Compute individual work contributions

    • Compute the work done by the pushing force.

    • Compute the work done by friction.

  4. Compute the net work

    Add the individual work contributions to find \(w_{\text{net}}\).

  5. Connect to kinetic energy

    Using

    \[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k, \]

    determine the final speed of the box.

  6. Reflect

    • Why would combining the forces and using a single displacement give an incorrect result?

    • Which force removes energy from the system, and how can you tell?

A Possible Solution Guide

Step 3: Individual work contributions

Work by the pushing force:

\[ w_{\text{push}} = (+10)(6.0) = +60\ \text{J}. \]

Work by friction:

\[ w_{\text{fric}} = (-4.0)(10.0) = -40\ \text{J}. \]

Step 4: Net work

\[ w_{\text{net}} = 60 - 40 = 20\ \text{J}. \]

Step 5: Final speed

Since the box starts from rest,

\[ k_i = 0, \quad k_f = w_{\text{net}} = 20\ \text{J}. \]

Using \(k_f = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\):

\[ 20 = \frac{1}{2}(4.0)v^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v^2 = 10 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v \approx 3.2\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • The pushing force transfers energy to the box.

  • Friction transfers energy from the box.

  • Because the forces act over different displacements, work must be computed term by term.
