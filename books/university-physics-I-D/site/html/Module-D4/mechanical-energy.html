D4.7 Mechanical Energy and the Work–Energy Theorem#

D4.7.1 Motivation — Combining Motion and Configuration#

So far, we have introduced two distinct ways that energy can appear in a system:

  • Kinetic energy \(k\), associated with motion

  • Potential energy \(u\), associated with configuration (position)

In many physical situations, energy moves back and forth between these two forms without being lost.
To describe this exchange efficiently, we introduce a new quantity: mechanical energy.

D4.7.2 Mechanical Energy#

Key Concept: Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energy:

\[ e_{\text{mech}} = k + u \]

Mechanical energy accounts for both:

  • how fast objects are moving, and

  • how they are positioned within a system.

D4.7.3 Rewriting the Work–Energy Theorem#

We begin with the work–energy theorem in its original form:

\[ w_{\text{net}} = \Delta k. \]

The net work can be separated into two contributions:

  • work done by conservative forces, \(w_c\)

  • work done by non-conservative forces, \(w_{nc}\)

Thus,

\[ w_{\text{net}} = w_c + w_{nc}. \]

Substituting into the work–energy theorem gives

\[ w_c + w_{nc} = \Delta k. \]

For conservative forces, we define potential energy such that

\[ w_c = -\Delta u. \]

This relationship reflects the fact that work done by a conservative force is stored as potential energy.

Substituting this into the previous equation yields

\[ -\Delta u + w_{nc} = \Delta k. \]

Rearranging,

\[ \Delta k + \Delta u = w_{nc}. \]

Mechanical Energy Form of the Theorem#

Recognizing that \(\Delta(k + u) = \Delta e_{\text{mech}}\), we arrive at the mechanical energy form of the work–energy theorem:

Work–Energy Theorem (Mechanical Energy Form)

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc} \]

Key Insight: Only non-conservative forces can change the mechanical energy of a particle. Conservative forces merely transfer energy between kinetic and potential forms.

Special Case — Mechanical Energy Conservation#

If no non-conservative work is done on the system (\(w_{nc} = 0\)), then

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = 0, \]

which means

\[ e_{\text{mech}} = k + u = \text{constant}. \]

This is the principle of conservation of mechanical energy.

Working Equation (Near Earth’s Surface)#

For motion near Earth’s surface involving gravity and possibly friction:

  • \(k = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2\)

  • \(u = mgy\)

The mechanical energy equation becomes

\[ \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mg y_i + w_{nc} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mg y_f. \]

If friction is the only non-conservative force acting along the direction of motion,

\[ w_{nc} = f_x\,\Delta x, \]

and the full working equation is

\[ \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mg y_i + f_x\,\Delta x = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mg y_f. \]

Interpretation#

  • Conservative forces move energy between \(k\) and \(u\).

  • Non-conservative forces transfer energy into or out of mechanical energy.

  • Mechanical energy conservation is not a new law—it is a rearranged form of the work–energy theorem.

Example – Mechanical Energy with Air Drag (Non-Conservative Force)

Problem

A \(0.50\ \text{kg}\) ball is thrown straight upward with an initial speed of

\[ v_i = 10.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

As the ball rises, it experiences:

  • the force of gravity, and

  • a constant air-drag force of magnitude

    \[ f_{\text{drag}} = 1.5\ \text{N}, \]

    directed downward.

The ball rises a vertical distance of

\[ \Delta y = +3.0\ \text{m}. \]

Using mechanical energy, determine the speed of the ball after it has risen \(3.0\ \text{m}\).

Methodology

We use the mechanical-energy form of the work–energy theorem:

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc}. \]

Gravity is a conservative force and is accounted for through gravitational potential energy.
Air drag is non-conservative and contributes through its work.

Solution

Initial mechanical energy:

\[ e_{\text{mech},i} = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mg y_i. \]

Choose \(y_i = 0\), so

\[ e_{\text{mech},i} = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)(10.0)^2 = 25\ \text{J}. \]

Final mechanical energy:

\[ e_{\text{mech},f} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mg y_f = \frac{1}{2}(0.50)v_f^2 + (0.50)(9.8)(3.0). \]

The work done by air drag is

\[ w_{\text{drag}} = f_{\text{drag}}\Delta y = (-1.5)(3.0) = -4.5\ \text{J}. \]

Applying the work–energy theorem:

\[ e_{\text{mech},f} - e_{\text{mech},i} = w_{\text{drag}}. \]

Substitute values:

\[ \left[\frac{1}{2}(0.50)v_f^2 + 14.7\right] - 25 = -4.5. \]

Simplifying:

\[ \frac{1}{4}v_f^2 = 5.8 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_f^2 = 23.2. \]

Answer

\[ \boxed{v_f \approx 4.8\ \text{m/s}} \]

Interpretation

  • Gravity transfers energy between kinetic and potential forms but does not change the total mechanical energy.

  • Air drag removes mechanical energy from the system.

  • Because of air drag, the ball has less kinetic energy than it would in the absence of non-conservative forces.

This example illustrates that only non-conservative forces, such as air drag, can change mechanical energy.

Box Activity 1 – Mechanical Energy with No Non-Conservative Forces

In this activity, you will analyze a situation in which only conservative forces act on a particle.
You will verify that the mechanical energy remains constant.

Physical Setup

A \(0.60\ \text{kg}\) ball is released from rest at a height of

\[ y_i = 4.0\ \text{m} \]

above the ground.

Assume:

  • air resistance is negligible,

  • gravity is the only force acting after release.

Task

  1. Write an expression for the initial mechanical energy of the system.

  2. Write an expression for the final mechanical energy just before the ball reaches the ground.

  3. Use conservation of mechanical energy to determine the speed of the ball just before impact.

  4. Compare this result to what you would expect if you solved the problem using forces and acceleration.

Reflection

  • Why does mechanical energy remain constant in this situation?

  • What role does gravity play in redistributing energy between kinetic and potential forms?

  • Would your answer change if the zero level of gravitational potential energy were chosen differently?

A Possible Solution Guide

Initial mechanical energy

The ball is released from rest, so

\[ e_{\text{mech},i} = k_i + u_i = 0 + mg y_i. \]

Substituting values:

\[ e_{\text{mech},i} = (0.60)(9.8)(4.0) = 23.5\ \text{J}. \]

Final mechanical energy

Just before impact, \(y_f = 0\), so

\[ e_{\text{mech},f} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2. \]

Apply conservation of mechanical energy

Since no non-conservative forces act,

\[ e_{\text{mech},f} = e_{\text{mech},i}. \]

Thus,

\[ \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 = 23.5. \]

Solving:

\[ v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2(23.5)}{0.60}} \approx 8.9\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • Mechanical energy is conserved because no non-conservative forces act.

  • Gravitational potential energy is fully converted into kinetic energy.

  • The result is independent of the chosen reference height.

Box Activity 2 – Mechanical Energy with a Non-Conservative Push

In this activity, you will analyze a situation in which a non-conservative force does work on a particle and changes its mechanical energy.

Physical Setup

A box of mass

\[ m = 4.0\ \text{kg} \]

slides along a horizontal floor.

A constant horizontal pushing force of magnitude

\[ f_{\text{push}} = +12\ \text{N} \]

acts on the box over a distance

\[ \Delta x = +5.0\ \text{m}. \]

Assume:

  • the box starts from rest,

  • friction is negligible,

  • gravity acts vertically and does no work during the motion.

Task

  1. Compute the work done by the pushing force.

  2. Write expressions for the initial and final mechanical energy of the box.

  3. Use the mechanical-energy form of the work–energy theorem,

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = w_{nc}, \]

to determine the final speed of the box.
4. State explicitly why mechanical energy is not conserved in this situation.

Reflection

  • Why does the pushing force count as a non-conservative force?

  • How would the result change if the pushing force acted over a longer distance?

  • In what way is this situation different from motion under gravity alone?

A Possible Solution Guide

Work done by the push

\[ w_{\text{push}} = f_{\text{push}}\Delta x = (12)(5.0) = 60\ \text{J}. \]

Initial mechanical energy

The box starts from rest and does not change height:

\[ e_{\text{mech},i} = k_i + u_i = 0. \]

Final mechanical energy

\[ e_{\text{mech},f} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2. \]

Apply the work–energy theorem

\[ \Delta e_{\text{mech}} = e_{\text{mech},f} - e_{\text{mech},i} = w_{nc}. \]

Thus,

\[ \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 = 60. \]

Solving,

\[ v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2(60)}{4.0}} \approx 5.5\ \text{m/s}. \]

Interpretation

  • The pushing force adds energy to the system.

  • Mechanical energy increases because non-conservative work is done.

  • Mechanical energy is therefore not conserved, even though momentum may change smoothly.
