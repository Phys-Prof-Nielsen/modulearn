D5.0 Learning Outcomes — Kinematics and the Emergence of Newton’s Second Law#

Why this module matters#

In Module D4, motion was analyzed without reference to acceleration or Newton’s laws. Instead, changes in motion were explained using impulse–momentum and energy, which apply broadly—even in situations where forces or accelerations vary in complicated ways.

In this module, we introduce kinematics, the mathematical description of motion itself. Rather than explaining why motion changes, kinematics focuses on how position, velocity, and acceleration are related in time.

We begin by defining the core kinematic quantities—position, displacement, speed, velocity, and acceleration—all in one dimension, using signed quantities to represent direction. We then derive the familiar kinematic equations for constant acceleration, emphasizing both their usefulness and their limitations.

Only after this foundation is established do we introduce Newton’s second law,

\[ f_x = ma_x, \]

not as a starting axiom, but as a derived relationship that emerges naturally from the impulse–momentum theorem under specific assumptions. In this way, acceleration becomes a measurable consequence of interaction, not a core physics concept.

Together, kinematics and Newton’s second law allow us to make quantitative predictions about motion in the past, present, and future—highlighting the deterministic structure of Newtonian mechanics within its domain of validity.

Throughout the module, all motion is treated in one dimension, without vectors or calculus, preparing the ground for more general formulations introduced later in Phase B.

Learning Outcomes#

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

A. Kinematic Quantities in One Dimension#

Defines the language used to describe motion.

  • A1. Define position as a signed coordinate along a one-dimensional axis.

  • A2. Distinguish between displacement and distance traveled, interpreting displacement as a directed change in position.

  • A3. Define average speed and average velocity, and explain how they differ conceptually.

  • A4. Define average acceleration as the rate of change of velocity over a time interval.

  • A5. Use sign conventions consistently to describe direction in one-dimensional motion.

  • A6. Interpret position–time, velocity–time, and acceleration–time graphs in one dimension by relating

    • slopes to rates of change (velocity and acceleration), and

    • areas to accumulated quantities (displacement and change in velocity),
      and explain which graphical areas and slopes have physical meaning in kinematics.

B. Constant-Acceleration Kinematics#

Develops predictive equations under a specific physical assumption.

  • B1. State the assumption of constant acceleration and explain when it is physically reasonable.

  • B2. Use the definition of acceleration to relate velocity and time,

    \[ v_x = v_{0_x} + a_{x}t. \]

  • B3. Use the definition of average velocity under constant acceleration to relate position and time,

    \[ x = x_0 + v_{0_x} t + \tfrac{1}{2} a_x t^2. \]

  • B4. Use the time-independent kinematic equation (Torricelli’s equation),

    \[ v_x^2 = v_{0_x}^2 + 2a_x(x - x_0), \]

    to relate speed and position without explicit reference to time.

C. Scope and Limitations of Kinematic Equations#

Clarifies what these equations do—and do not—describe.

  • C1. Identify the physical assumptions underlying constant-acceleration kinematics.

  • C2. Recognize situations where the kinematic equations do not apply, such as variable forces or accelerations.

  • C3. Explain why more general descriptions of motion require new mathematical tools introduced in later phases.

D. Newton’s Second Law from Impulse–Momentum#

Derives \( f_x = ma_x \) rather than postulating it.

  • D1. State the impulse–momentum theorem,

    \[ j_x = \Delta p_x. \]

  • D2. Recall that, for a constant force acting over a time interval, impulse can be written as

    \[ j_x = f_x\,\Delta t. \]

  • D3. Combine impulse–momentum with constant mass to obtain

    \[ f_x = m a_x, \]

    without the use of calculus.

  • D4. Interpret Newton’s second law as a special case of a more general momentum-based description of motion.

E. Determinism and Prediction in Newtonian Motion#

Connects equations to physical meaning.

  • E1. Explain how knowing initial conditions and interactions allows prediction of motion forward and backward in time.

  • E2. Describe Newtonian dynamics as deterministic within its domain of validity.

  • E3. Use \( f_x = ma_x \) together with kinematics to make quantitative predictions about one-dimensional motion.

Performance Criteria#

  • Motion is described using clear physical definitions, not memorized formulas.

  • Direction is handled consistently using signed quantities, not vector notation.

  • Kinematic equations are applied only when their assumptions are valid.

  • Newton’s second law is understood as a derived relationship, not a starting axiom.

  • Explanations emphasize causal structure, assumptions, and limitations of Newtonian mechanics.
