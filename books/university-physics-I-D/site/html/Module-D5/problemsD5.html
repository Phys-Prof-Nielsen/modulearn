Problem 1 ‚Äî Constant Acceleration from Rest#

A car starts from rest and accelerates at a constant rate of

\[ a_x = +3.0\ \text{m/s}^2 \]

for a time interval of

\[ t = 10.0\ \text{s}. \]

Answer the following:

  1. What is the final velocity of the car?

  2. How much distance does the car travel during this time?

Be careful with units, signs, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Velocity‚Äìtime relation (constant acceleration):

\[ v_x = v_{x,0} + a_x t \]

Position‚Äìtime relation (constant acceleration):

\[ \Delta x = v_{x,0} t + \frac{1}{2} a_x t^2 \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Final velocity

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = 0\) (starts from rest)

  • \(a_x = +3.0\ \text{m/s}^2\)

  • \(t = 10.0\ \text{s}\)

Compute final velocity

\[ v_x = 0 + (3.0)(10.0) = 30.0\ \text{m/s} \]

With proper significant figures:

\[ \boxed{v_x = +30\ \text{m/s}} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Distance traveled

Since the car starts from rest, \(v_{x,0} = 0\), so the displacement becomes

\[ \Delta x = \frac{1}{2} a_x t^2. \]

Substitute the values:

\[ \Delta x = \frac{1}{2}(3.0)(10.0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(3.0)(100) = 150\ \text{m}. \]

With proper significant figures:

\[ \boxed{\Delta x = 1.5\times10^2\ \text{m}} \]

Final Summary

  • A constant positive acceleration increases the velocity linearly in time.

  • The distance traveled grows with \(t^2\), reflecting the increasing speed.

  • Because the motion is entirely in the positive direction, both velocity and displacement are positive.

Problem 2 ‚Äî Vertical Motion After Engine Burn-Out (Torricelli‚Äôs Equation)#

A UVU rocket is launched vertically upward.
After the engine burns out, the rocket‚Äôs speed is

\[ v_{x,0} = +740.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

After burnout, the rocket is in free flight under the influence of gravity only.

At what height above the burn-out altitude is the rocket‚Äôs speed

\[ v_x = 120.0\ \text{m/s} \ ? \]

Take the acceleration due to gravity to be

\[ a_x = -9.81\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Be careful with signs, units, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equation

For constant acceleration in one dimension, Torricelli‚Äôs equation relates velocity and position without time:

\[ v_x^2 = v_{x,0}^2 + 2a_x(x - x_0). \]

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = +740.0\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(v_x = +120.0\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(a_x = -9.81\ \text{m/s}^2\)

Let the burn-out altitude be the reference point, so

\[ x_0 = 0. \]

Solve for height above burn-out

Rearrange Torricelli‚Äôs equation:

\[ x - x_0 = \frac{v_x^2 - v_{x,0}^2}{2a_x}. \]

Substitute the given values:

\[ x = \frac{(120.0)^2 - (740.0)^2}{2(-9.81)}. \]

Evaluate the numerator:

\[ (120.0)^2 = 1.44\times10^4, \qquad (740.0)^2 = 5.476\times10^5. \]
\[ v_x^2 - v_{x,0}^2 = -5.332\times10^5. \]

Now compute the height:

\[ x = \frac{-5.332\times10^5}{-19.62} \approx 2.72\times10^4\ \text{m}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{x \approx 2.7\times10^4\ \text{m}} \]

Final Summary

  • After burn-out, gravity provides a constant negative acceleration.

  • The rocket continues upward while slowing down.

  • Torricelli‚Äôs equation allows us to find the height change without using time.

  • The positive result indicates the rocket is still above the burn-out altitude when its speed is \(120.0\ \text{m/s}\).

Problem 3 ‚Äî Japan Metro Train: Acceleration, Service Braking, and Emergency Braking#

A Tokyo metro train (Japan) operates on a section of line where the operating speed limit is

\[ v_x = 80.0\ \text{km/h}. \]

The train‚Äôs typical performance specifications are approximately:

  • acceleration (speeding up):

    \[ a_x = +0.92\ \text{m/s}^2 \]

  • service braking (slowing down normally):

    \[ a_x = -1.03\ \text{m/s}^2 \]

  • emergency braking (maximum): the train can come to rest from \(80.0\ \text{km/h}\) in

    \[ t = 18.0\ \text{s}. \]

Answer the following:

  1. Starting from rest, how long does it take the train to reach \(80.0\ \text{km/h}\)?

  2. From \(80.0\ \text{km/h}\), how long does it take to come to rest under service braking?

  3. What is the emergency acceleration \(a_x\) (in \(\text{m/s}^2\)) if the train comes to rest from \(80.0\ \text{km/h}\) in \(18.0\ \text{s}\)?

Be careful with unit conversions, signs, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equation

For constant acceleration in one dimension:

\[ v_x = v_{x,0} + a_x t. \]

Step 0 ‚Äî Convert the top speed to \(\text{m/s}\)

\[ 80.0\ \text{km/h} = 80.0\left(\frac{1000\ \text{m}}{1\ \text{km}}\right)\left(\frac{1\ \text{h}}{3600\ \text{s}}\right) = 22.2\ \text{m/s}. \]

So we will use

\[ v_x = 22.2\ \text{m/s}. \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Time to reach top speed (start from rest)

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = 0\)

  • \(v_x = 22.2\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(a_x = +0.92\ \text{m/s}^2\)

Solve \(v_x = v_{x,0} + a_x t\) for \(t\):

\[ t = \frac{v_x - v_{x,0}}{a_x} = \frac{22.2}{0.92} = 24.1\ \text{s}. \]

With appropriate significant figures:

\[ \boxed{t \approx 24\ \text{s}} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Time to stop under service braking

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = 22.2\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(v_x = 0\)

  • \(a_x = -1.03\ \text{m/s}^2\)

\[ 0 = 22.2 + (-1.03)t \quad\Rightarrow\quad t = \frac{22.2}{1.03} = 21.6\ \text{s}. \]

So,

\[ \boxed{t \approx 22\ \text{s}} \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Emergency acceleration from stopping time

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = 22.2\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(v_x = 0\)

  • \(t = 18.0\ \text{s}\)

Solve for \(a_x\):

\[ a_x = \frac{v_x - v_{x,0}}{t} = \frac{0 - 22.2}{18.0} = -1.23\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

So,

\[ \boxed{a_x \approx -1.23\ \text{m/s}^2} \]

Final Summary

  • Convert speeds to \(\text{m/s}\) before using kinematics.

  • The sign of \(a_x\) indicates whether the train speeds up (\(a_x>0\)) or slows down (\(a_x<0\)).

  • Emergency braking corresponds to a larger-magnitude negative acceleration than service braking.

Problem 4 ‚Äî Vertical Motion from an Observation Platform (Upward vs. Downward Launch)#

An engineer on a high observation platform releases a small test capsule that moves only under the influence of gravity.
Air resistance is negligible.

Take upward as the positive \(x\)-direction and use

\[ a_x = -9.81\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Scenario A ‚Äî Launched Upward#

The test capsule is launched straight upward from the edge of the platform with initial velocity

\[ v_{x,0} = +8.00\ \text{m/s}. \]

The capsule reaches the ground 2.35 s after launch.

Scenario B ‚Äî Launched Downward#

The same capsule is instead launched straight downward from the same platform with the same speed,

\[ v_{x,0} = -8.00\ \text{m/s}. \]

Answer the following:

  1. What is the height of the platform above the ground?

  2. How long does it take the capsule to reach the ground in Scenario B?

Be careful with sign conventions, units, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equation

For constant acceleration in one dimension:

\[ x = x_0 + v_{x,0} t + \frac{1}{2} a_x t^2 \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Height of the platform (Scenario A)

Let the launch point be \(x_0 = 0\).
When the capsule reaches the ground, its position is

\[ x = -h, \]

where \(h\) is the platform height.

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = +8.00\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(t = 2.35\ \text{s}\)

  • \(a_x = -9.81\ \text{m/s}^2\)

Substitute into the position equation:

\[ -h = (8.00)(2.35) + \frac{1}{2}(-9.81)(2.35)^2. \]

Evaluate each term:

\[ (8.00)(2.35) = 18.8\ \text{m}, \]
\[ \frac{1}{2}(9.81)(2.35)^2 = 27.1\ \text{m}. \]

So,

\[ -h = 18.8 - 27.1 = -8.3\ \text{m}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \boxed{h = 8.3\ \text{m}}. \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Time to reach the ground (Scenario B)

The platform height is unchanged, so the final position is again

\[ x = -8.3\ \text{m}. \]

Given

  • \(v_{x,0} = -8.00\ \text{m/s}\)

  • \(a_x = -9.81\ \text{m/s}^2\)

Use the position equation:

\[ -8.3 = (-8.00)t + \frac{1}{2}(-9.81)t^2. \]

Rewriting:

\[ \frac{1}{2}(9.81)t^2 + 8.00 t - 8.3 = 0. \]

Solving this quadratic equation gives the physically meaningful root:

\[ \boxed{t = 0.71\ \text{s}}. \]

Final Summary

  • The observation platform is approximately 8.3 m above the ground.

  • When launched downward, the capsule reaches the ground much faster because gravity and the initial velocity act in the same direction.

  • Even with the same initial speed, the direction of the velocity strongly affects the time of flight.

Problem 5 üêçüíª ‚Äî Autonomous Drone Intercept: Algebraic and Graphical Analysis#

An autonomous tracking drone hovers motionless above a straight test corridor.
A ground robot moves along the corridor at a constant speed of

\[ v_{\text{robot}} = 40.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

At the instant the robot passes directly beneath the hovering drone, the drone begins to move along the corridor, accelerating from rest with a constant acceleration of

\[ a_x = +4.00\ \text{m/s}^2, \]

in the same direction as the robot.

Assume:

  • motion is one-dimensional along the corridor,

  • the robot maintains constant speed,

  • the drone maintains constant acceleration.

Tasks#

  1. Using kinematic equations, determine how long it takes for the drone to intercept the robot.

  2. Using a position‚Äìtime plot, determine the same intercept time graphically.

  3. Compare the two results and explain why they agree.

Be careful with units, sign conventions, and physical interpretation.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Ground robot (constant speed):

\[ x_{\text{robot}}(t) = v_{\text{robot}}\,t \]

Tracking drone (constant acceleration, starts from rest):

\[ x_{\text{drone}}(t) = \frac{1}{2}a_x t^2 \]

The interception occurs when both objects have the same position at the same time.

Part 1 ‚Äî Algebraic solution

Set the two position equations equal:

\[ \frac{1}{2}a_x t^2 = v_{\text{robot}} t. \]

Factor out \(t\):

\[ t\left(\frac{1}{2}a_x t - v_{\text{robot}}\right) = 0. \]

The solution \(t=0\) corresponds to the instant the robot passes beneath the drone and is not physically meaningful.

Solve the remaining equation:

\[ \frac{1}{2}a_x t = v_{\text{robot}}. \]

Substitute the given values:

\[ \frac{1}{2}(4.00)t = 40.0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2.00t = 40.0. \]

Thus,

\[ \boxed{t = 20.0\ \text{s}}. \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Graphical interpretation

  • The robot‚Äôs position increases linearly with time.

  • The drone‚Äôs position increases quadratically with time.

  • The interception time corresponds to the intersection point of the two curves.

From the position‚Äìtime plot, the intersection occurs at

\[ t \approx 20\ \text{s}, \]

confirming the algebraic result.

Final Summary

  • The drone intercepts the robot after 20.0 s.

  • The agreement between algebraic and graphical solutions reflects that both describe the same physical motion.

  • Position‚Äìtime plots provide an important visual validation of kinematic predictions.

Problem 6 ‚Äî Crash Safety and Impulse: Seat Belts and Airbags#

Before a crash-test collision, a car (with a crash-test dummy) is traveling toward a rigid wall with speed

\[ v_{0,x} = +25.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

The crash-test dummy has mass

\[ m = 75\ \text{kg}. \]

During the collision, assume the impact force is much larger than any other forces, so the net force is well-approximated by the impact force. The dummy comes to rest, so

\[ v_{f,x} = 0. \]

Consider three different impact times:

  • Scenario A (no restraint / rigid stop): \(\Delta t = 10.00\ \text{ms}\)

  • Scenario B (seat belt): \(\Delta t = 20.0\ \text{ms}\)

  • Scenario C (seat belt + airbag): \(\Delta t = 0.100\ \text{s}\)

Answer the following:

  1. If the dummy is fastened rigidly to the car (Scenario A), what impact force is experienced by the dummy?

  2. With seat belts (Scenario B), what is the impact force?

  3. With seat belts plus airbags (Scenario C), what is the impact force?

  4. The acceleration of gravity is \(9.81\ \text{m/s}^2\). How many g‚Äôs did the dummy experience in each scenario?
    Hint: compute \(|a_x|/9.81\).

Be careful with units, signs, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Impulse‚Äìmomentum theorem:

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x \]

With constant average impact force over the collision interval:

\[ j_x = \left(\sum f_x\right)\Delta t \]

Momentum:

\[ p_x = mv_x \]

Acceleration from force (constant mass case):

\[ \sum f_x = m a_x \]

Also, from the definition of acceleration over a time interval:

\[ a_x = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t}. \]

Given (all scenarios)

  • \(m = 75\ \text{kg}\)

  • \(v_{0,x} = +25.0\ \text{m/s}\)

\[ \Delta p_x = m\Delta v_x = (75)(-25.0) = -1875\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

So the impulse is

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x = -1875\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

The negative sign indicates the impulse (and force) points in the negative \(x\)-direction (opposite the motion).

Part 1 ‚Äî Scenario A: \(\Delta t = 10.00\ \text{ms}\)

Convert time:

\[ 10.00\ \text{ms} = 10.00\times10^{-3}\ \text{s} = 0.01000\ \text{s}. \]

Use \(j_x = (\sum f_x)\Delta t\):

\[ \sum f_x = \frac{j_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{-1875}{0.01000} = -1.875\times10^5\ \text{N}. \]

So,

\[ \boxed{\sum f_x \approx -1.88\times10^5\ \text{N}} \]

Acceleration:

\[ a_x = \frac{\sum f_x}{m} = \frac{-1.875\times10^5}{75} = -2500\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

\[ \frac{|a_x|}{9.81} = \frac{2500}{9.81} \approx 2.55\times10^2. \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Scenario B: \(\Delta t = 20.0\ \text{ms}\)

Convert time:

\[ 20.0\ \text{ms} = 0.0200\ \text{s}. \]

Impact force:

\[ \sum f_x = \frac{-1875}{0.0200} = -9.375\times10^4\ \text{N}. \]

So,

\[ \boxed{\sum f_x \approx -9.38\times10^4\ \text{N}} \]

Acceleration:

\[ a_x = \frac{-9.375\times10^4}{75} = -1250\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

g‚Äôs experienced:

\[ \frac{|a_x|}{9.81} = \frac{1250}{9.81} \approx 1.27\times10^2. \]
\[ \boxed{\text{Scenario B: } \approx 127\ \text{g}} \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Scenario C: \(\Delta t = 0.100\ \text{s}\)

Impact force:

\[ \sum f_x = \frac{-1875}{0.100} = -1.875\times10^4\ \text{N}. \]

So,

\[ \boxed{\sum f_x \approx -1.88\times10^4\ \text{N}} \]

Acceleration:

\[ a_x = \frac{-1.875\times10^4}{75} = -250\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

    \[ \sum f_x = \frac{\Delta p_x}{\Delta t}. \]

  • Seat belts and airbags reduce injury risk largely by increasing the stopping time, which reduces both force and acceleration (g-load).

Problem 7 ‚Äî Impulse, Direction, and Collision Time (1D)#

A particle of mass

\[ m = 5.0\ \text{kg} \]

is traveling in one dimension with initial velocity

\[ v_{0,x} = -35\ \text{m/s}. \]

During an interaction (collision), its final velocity becomes

\[ v_{f,x} = -5.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Assume the interaction force is much larger than any other forces during the short collision interval, so the net force is well-approximated by the interaction force.

Answer the following:

  1. Make a prediction about the direction of the interaction (positive or negative) and explain your reasoning.

  2. If the net average force during the collision had magnitude

    \[ \left|\sum f_x\right| = 3.0\times10^3\ \text{N}, \]

    what was the impact time \(\Delta t\)?

  3. What was the acceleration \(a_x\) of the particle during the interaction?

Be careful with signs, units, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Impulse‚Äìmomentum theorem:

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x \]

Impulse from a constant average net force:

\[ j_x = \left(\sum f_x\right)\Delta t \]

Momentum in 1D:

\[ p_x = m v_x \]

Acceleration relationships (constant mass):

\[ \sum f_x = m a_x, \qquad a_x = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t}. \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Direction of the interaction

The velocity changes from \(-35\ \text{m/s}\) to \(-5.0\ \text{m/s}\). The particle is still moving in the negative direction, but its speed is much smaller, meaning it has gained velocity in the positive direction (it has become less negative).

Compute the change in velocity:

\[ \Delta v_x = v_{f,x} - v_{0,x} = (-5.0) - (-35) = +30\ \text{m/s}. \]

Because \(\Delta v_x\) is positive, the change in momentum is also positive (mass is positive), so the impulse must be positive:

\[ j_x = m\Delta v_x > 0. \]

Therefore, the interaction (net) force must have acted in the positive \(x\)-direction.

Result

\[ \boxed{\text{The interaction is in the } +x \text{ direction.}} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Impact time

First compute the change in momentum:

\[ \Delta p_x = m\Delta v_x = (5.0)(+30) = +150\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

Impulse equals change in momentum:

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x = +150\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

We are told the net average force has magnitude \(3.0\times10^3\ \text{N}\).
From Part 1 the direction is positive, so we take

\[ \sum f_x = +3.0\times10^3\ \text{N}. \]

Use \(j_x = (\sum f_x)\Delta t\):

\[ \Delta t = \frac{j_x}{\sum f_x} = \frac{150}{3.0\times10^3} = 5.0\times10^{-2}\ \text{s}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{\Delta t = 5.0\times10^{-2}\ \text{s}} \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Acceleration during the interaction

Use Newton‚Äôs second law (constant mass case):

\[ a_x = \frac{\sum f_x}{m} = \frac{3.0\times10^3}{5.0} = 6.0\times10^2\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

The acceleration is positive, consistent with Part 1.

Result

\[ \boxed{a_x = +6.0\times10^2\ \text{m/s}^2} \]

Final Summary

  • The particle slows down while moving in the negative direction, which requires a positive impulse and thus a positive interaction force.

  • Using \(j_x=\Delta p_x\) connects the observed velocity change directly to the interaction.

  • A larger force would produce the same momentum change in a shorter time interval.

Problem 10 ‚Äî Determining Acceleration and Net Force from Velocity Change#

A particle moves along the \(x\)-axis and undergoes a brief interaction during which its velocity changes.

The particle has mass

\[ m = 1.20\ \text{kg}. \]

Before the interaction, its velocity is

\[ v_{0,x} = -18.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

After an elapsed time of

\[ \Delta t = 0.400\ \text{s}, \]

the particle‚Äôs velocity is measured to be

\[ v_{f,x} = +6.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Assume:

  • the mass of the particle is constant,

  • all forces act over the same elapsed time interval \(\Delta t\),

  • the net force is approximately constant during the interaction.

Answer the following:

  1. What is the particle‚Äôs acceleration during the interaction?

  2. What is the net force acting on the particle?

Be careful with sign conventions, units, and significant figures.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Change in velocity over a time interval:

\[ a_x = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t} \]

Newton‚Äôs second law (constant mass):

\[ \sum f_x = m a_x \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Acceleration

Compute the change in velocity:

\[ \Delta v_x = v_{f,x} - v_{0,x} = (+6.0) - (-18.0) = +24.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Now divide by the elapsed time:

\[ a_x = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{24.0}{0.400} = 60.0\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{a_x = +60.0\ \text{m/s}^2} \]

The positive sign indicates that the acceleration points in the positive \(x\)-direction.

Part 2 ‚Äî Net force

Use Newton‚Äôs second law:

\[ \sum f_x = m a_x. \]

Substitute values:

\[ \sum f_x = (1.20)(60.0) = 72.0\ \text{N}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{\sum f_x = +72.0\ \text{N}} \]

Final Summary

  • The particle‚Äôs velocity changes from negative to positive, so the acceleration must point in the positive \(x\)-direction.

  • The acceleration is determined directly from the velocity change and elapsed time.

  • Once \(a_x\) is known, the net force follows immediately from \(\sum f_x = m a_x\).

Problem 11 üêçüíª ‚Äî Impulse‚ÄìMomentum for a Muon in a Beamline#

In a beamline, a muon of mass

\[ m_\mu = 1.884\times10^{-28}\ \text{kg} \]

moves along the \(x\)-axis with initial speed

\[ v_{0,x} = +5.00\times10^{5}\ \text{m/s}. \]

A fast kicker applies a time-varying force \(f_x(t)\) along \(+x\) for a total of \(200\ \text{ns}\) with the shape:

  • Ramp up linearly from \(0\) to \(f_0\) during \(0\text{‚Äì}40\ \text{ns}\)

  • Hold constant at \(f_0\) during \(40\text{‚Äì}140\ \text{ns}\)

  • Ramp down linearly from \(f_0\) to \(0\) during \(140\text{‚Äì}200\ \text{ns}\)

After the kicker, the muon‚Äôs momentum is measured to be

\[ p_{f,x} = 2.50\times10^{-22}\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

Use the impulse‚Äìmomentum theorem for a single particle:

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x = p_{f,x} - p_{0,x}. \]

Tasks

  1. Using geometry only, express the total impulse \(j_x\) (the area under \(f_x(t)\)) in terms of \(f_0\) and the given time segments.

  2. Set \(j_x = \Delta p_x\) and use SymPy‚Äôs solve to find the unknown peak force \(f_0\).

  3. Create a plot of \(f_x(t)\) vs. time (in ns) showing the ramp-up, flat, and ramp-down segments.

  4. Using Python (no calculus), compute the numerical area under your plotted curve (trapezoid-sum) and verify it matches the impulse used to solve for \(f_0\).

  5. Compute the final speed from \(p_{f,x} = m_\mu v_{f,x}\) and report \(v_{f,x}\) (assume non-relativistic).

Hints: Triangle area: \(\tfrac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{height})\), rectangle area: \((\text{base})(\text{height})\). Keep times in seconds in code; label the plot in ns.

Problem solution

Relevant equations

Momentum:

\[ p_x = m v_x \]

Impulse‚Äìmomentum theorem:

\[ j_x = \Delta p_x = p_{f,x}-p_{0,x} \]

Impulse from area under a force‚Äìtime curve:

\[ j_x = \int f_x(t)\,dt \quad \text{(interpreted here as ‚Äúarea under the curve‚Äù)} \]

Part 1 ‚Äî Geometry for the impulse in terms of \(f_0\)

The area under \(f_x(t)\) is the sum of two triangles and one rectangle.

Convert the time widths:

  • Ramp up: \(40\ \text{ns}\)

  • Flat: \(140-40 = 100\ \text{ns}\)

  • Ramp down: \(200-140 = 60\ \text{ns}\)

Areas:

  • Ramp-up triangle: $\( A_1 = \frac{1}{2}(40\ \text{ns})\,f_0 \)$

  • Flat rectangle: $\( A_2 = (100\ \text{ns})\,f_0 \)$

  • Ramp-down triangle: $\( A_3 = \frac{1}{2}(60\ \text{ns})\,f_0 \)$

Total impulse:

\[ j_x = A_1 + A_2 + A_3 = \left[\frac{1}{2}(40) + 100 + \frac{1}{2}(60)\right]\text{ns}\,f_0 \]
\[ j_x = (20 + 100 + 30)\text{ns}\,f_0 = (150\ \text{ns})\,f_0. \]

In seconds:

\[ 150\ \text{ns} = 150\times10^{-9}\ \text{s}, \]

so

\[ \boxed{j_x = (150\times10^{-9})\,f_0.} \]

Part 2 ‚Äî Use \(j_x = \Delta p_x\) to solve for \(f_0\)

Compute the initial momentum:

\[ p_{0,x} = m_\mu v_{0,x} = (1.884\times10^{-28})(5.00\times10^{5}) = 9.42\times10^{-23}\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

Change in momentum:

\[ \Delta p_x = p_{f,x} - p_{0,x} = 2.50\times10^{-22} - 9.42\times10^{-23} = 1.56\times10^{-22}\ \text{kg¬∑m/s}. \]

Set \(j_x = \Delta p_x\):

\[ (150\times10^{-9})\,f_0 = 1.56\times10^{-22}. \]

Solve:

\[ f_0 = \frac{1.56\times10^{-22}}{150\times10^{-9}} \approx 1.04\times10^{-15}\ \text{N}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{f_0 \approx 1.04\times10^{-15}\ \text{N}} \]

Part 3‚Äì4 ‚Äî Plot and numerical area check

A plot of \(f_x(t)\) should show:

  • a linear rise from \(0\) to \(f_0\) over \(0\)‚Äì\(40\ \text{ns}\),

  • a flat region at \(f_0\) over \(40\)‚Äì\(140\ \text{ns}\),

  • a linear fall to \(0\) over \(140\)‚Äì\(200\ \text{ns}\).

A trapezoid-sum numerical area under that curve should match:

\[ j_x = (150\times10^{-9})\,f_0. \]

Part 5 ‚Äî Final speed (non-relativistic)

Use \(p_{f,x} = m_\mu v_{f,x}\):

\[ v_{f,x} = \frac{p_{f,x}}{m_\mu} = \frac{2.50\times10^{-22}}{1.884\times10^{-28}} \approx 1.33\times10^{6}\ \text{m/s}. \]

Result

\[ \boxed{v_{f,x} \approx 1.33\times10^{6}\ \text{m/s}} \]

Final Summary

  • The impulse is the geometric area under the force‚Äìtime curve: two triangles plus one rectangle.

  • Impulse equals the change in momentum, which allows solving for the unknown peak force \(f_0\).

  • A numerical trapezoid-sum of the plotted curve should match the geometric impulse.

  • The measured final momentum directly gives the final speed through \(p_{f,x}=m_\mu v_{f,x}\).

Problem 8 solution

Part 1 ‚Äî Graph

Plot the points and connect them with straight segments.

Part 2 ‚Äî Slope gives acceleration (0 to 3 s)

From the table:

  • \(v_x(0)=0.0\) m/s

  • \(v_x(3)=6.0\) m/s

The slope of the \(v_x\) vs. \(t\) graph over this interval is

\[ a_x = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{6.0-0.0}{3.0-0.0} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}^2. \]
\[ \boxed{a_x = +2.0\ \text{m/s}^2 \ \text{(for 0 to 3 s)}} \]

Part 3 ‚Äî Area gives displacement (0 to 5 s)

Displacement is the area under the \(v_x\) vs. \(t\) graph.

Break it into two pieces:

(i) 0 to 3 s: velocity increases linearly from 0 to 6 m/s, forming a triangle:

\[ \Delta x_{0\to3} = \tfrac{1}{2}(3.0)(6.0)=9.0\ \text{m}. \]

(ii) 3 to 5 s: velocity is constant at 6 m/s, forming a rectangle:

\[ \Delta x_{3\to5} = (2.0)(6.0)=12.0\ \text{m}. \]

Total displacement:

\[ \Delta x = 9.0 + 12.0 = 21.0\ \text{m}. \]
\[ \boxed{\Delta x_{0\to5} = +21.0\ \text{m}} \]

Part 4 ‚Äî Motion after 3 s

After \(t=3\) s, the velocity stays constant at \(+6.0\) m/s.
That means the acceleration is zero and the particle continues moving steadily in the positive direction.
