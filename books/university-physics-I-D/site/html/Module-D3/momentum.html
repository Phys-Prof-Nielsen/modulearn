D3.2 Linear Momentum#

Motivation and Concept of Motion#

Newton’s First Law (Law of Inertia)

An object continues in its state of motion unless acted upon by an unbalanced interaction.

Newton’s First Law is fundamentally a conceptual statement about motion.

It tells us that motion is not something that automatically changes on its own—something must interact with an object in order to change how it moves.

In everyday language, we often use the word motion without worrying about what it actually means. We typically associate motion with speed—how fast something is going. While this intuition feels natural, it turns out to be incomplete from a physics point of view.

To reveal this limitation, consider the following question:

Which of the following has more motion: a sedan traveling at 70 mph or a semi-truck traveling at 70 mph?

Many students initially answer that they have the same motion, since they are moving at the same speed. This response highlights a common assumption: that motion depends only on how fast an object moves.

Newton’s First Law invites us to think differently. If motion can only change when an unbalanced interaction occurs, then the amount of interaction required to stop an object tells us something meaningful about its motion.

Now reconsider the scenario from this perspective:

A sedan and a semi-truck are both traveling at 70 mph. Which one requires a greater interaction to come to a complete stop?

Without much hesitation, most people correctly answer that the semi-truck requires a greater interaction. This intuitive response points us toward a more precise physical understanding of motion.

Key Insight

A more massive object has more motion than a less massive object moving at the same speed.
This shows that motion depends not only on how fast an object is moving, but also on how much matter it contains.

In physics, motion is therefore linked to both speed and mass, not speed alone.

D3.2.1 Reference Frame#

Building on our first discussion of motion, we used the example of a sedan and a semi-truck to argue that motion is more than just speed—it also depends on the mass of the moving object:

A sedan and a semi-truck are both traveling at 70 mph. Which one requires a greater interaction to come to a complete stop?

The natural conclusion was that the semi-truck requires a greater interaction. This told us that motion depends on both how fast an object moves and how much matter it contains.

Let us now take this idea one step further.

Imagine a sailboat moving across Utah Lake with a certain motion. Suppose strong winds begin to act on the boat. Whether the boat speeds up or slows down depends not only on the strength of the interaction, but also on the relative direction of the wind and the boat’s motion. A tailwind increases the boat’s speed and motion, while a headwind reduces both.

This example reveals an important limitation in how we have described motion so far: to fully describe changes in motion, we must also account for direction, not just magnitude.

At this point, it is helpful to distinguish between two closely related ideas. Speed tells us how fast something is moving, but it says nothing about which way it is moving. To include directional information, we introduce a new quantity.

Velocity

Velocity describes how fast an object is moving and the direction of its motion.

In other words, velocity combines speed with direction.

Mass does not depend on direction, and speed alone does not either (a car’s speedometer, for example, does not tell you which way you are traveling). Directional information enters our description of motion through velocity.

To describe direction in a clear and consistent way, physics requires the use of a reference frame, also called a coordinate system. A reference frame provides:

  • a chosen direction (positive and negative), and

  • a reference point relative to which positions and motion are described.

That reference point is called the origin.

In this course, we will encounter several coordinate systems. In Phase D, we restrict ourselves to a one-dimensional Cartesian system, where direction is represented using positive and negative signs along a single line. Two- and three-dimensional Cartesian systems will be introduced in Phase C, and polar coordinates will appear later in Phase B.

While we may think of these reference frames being simple, they are indeed more complex than we may think and relates to the algebra they are formulated in. Fortunately, we do not have to worry about that in this course. I suggest you read the wikipedia page on Cartesian Coordinate System

The 1D cartesian system is represented just like a number line in elementary school. The difference here is that the positive and negative numbers now represents a sense of direction to us. For example, in the sketch below, positive numbers will represent objects that directed to the right, whereas negative numbers indicate objects directed to the left.

D3.2.3 Linear Momentum#

From our discussion of the sedan and the semi-truck, we were led to the idea that motion depends on both mass and speed. This motivates an initial definition of momentum—without yet accounting for direction—as

\[ p = mv \tag{1} \]

where \(m\) is the mass of the particle and \(v\) is its speed.

However, the sailboat example showed that motion—and therefore momentum—must also account for direction. In one dimension, we represent this directional information explicitly by attaching a subscript to the momentum and velocity.

Linear Momentum for a Point Particle (1D)

\[ p_x = m v_x \tag{2} \]

Here, the subscript \(x\) indicates that the momentum and velocity include directional information along a chosen one-dimensional axis. Positive and negative values correspond to motion in opposite directions.

The quantity defined in Equation (1), which does not include direction, is called the magnitude of the momentum.

We are now ready to explore momentum in one-dimensional motion.

Example 1 – Momentum of a Point Particle in 1D

A Subaru Outback (modeled as a point particle) has a mass of \(m = 1648\ \text{kg}\) and is traveling with a speed of \(v = 32\ \text{m/s}\) in the \(+x\) direction.

Determine the momentum of the Subaru.

Methodology

We are given the mass, speed, and direction of motion.
Since direction is specified, the speed can be written as a velocity with a sign.
Once the velocity is known, the momentum is found using the one-dimensional momentum definition,

\[ p_x = m v_x. \]

Solution

The magnitude of the momentum is

\[ p = mv = (1648)(32) = 5.3\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Because the Subaru is traveling in the positive \(x\)-direction, its momentum must also be positive:

\[ p_x = +5.3\times10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Example 2 – Momentum with Negative Direction

Problem
A hockey puck (modeled as a point particle) has a mass of
\(m = 0.17\ \text{kg}\) and is sliding with a speed of
\(v = 12\ \text{m/s}\) in the negative \(x\)-direction.

Determine the momentum of the puck.

Methodology
We are given the mass, speed, and direction of motion.
Because the motion is along the negative \(x\)-direction, the velocity must be assigned a negative sign.
The momentum is then computed using the one-dimensional definition

\[ p_x = m v_x. \]

Solution
The magnitude of the momentum is

\[ p = mv = (0.17)(12) = 2.0\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Since the puck is moving in the negative \(x\)-direction, its momentum is negative:

\[ p_x = -2.0\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

Box Activity 1 – Momentum and Direction in One Dimension

In this activity, you will work with momentum as a signed quantity in one dimension.
The focus is on understanding how direction enters through velocity.

Task: Complete the following steps.

  1. Choose a reference direction
    Decide which direction you will call the positive \(x\)-direction.

  2. Assign signed velocities

    Write the signed velocities \(v_x\) for both objects.

  3. Compute the momentum
    Use the definition

    \[ p_x = m v_x \]

    • Object A: \(m = 1.5\ \text{kg}\), \(v_x = +4.0\ \text{m/s}\)

    • Object B: \(m = 1.5\ \text{kg}\), \(v_x = -4.0\ \text{m/s}\)

  4. Reflect

    • How do their signs compare?

    • What does the sign of momentum tell you physically?

A Possible Solution Guide

Signed velocities

  • Car: \(v_x = +20\ \text{m/s}\)

  • Bicycle: \(v_x = -8\ \text{m/s}\)

Momentum calculations

  • Object A:

\[ p_x = (1.5)(+4.0) = +6.0\ \text{kg·m/s} \]

  • Object B:

\[ p_x = (1.5)(-4.0) = -6.0\ \text{kg·m/s} \]

Interpretation

  • The momenta have the same magnitude but opposite signs.

  • The sign of momentum indicates the direction of motion relative to the chosen reference direction.

  • Reversing direction reverses momentum, even when mass and speed remain the same.

Box Activity 2 – Solving for Velocity from Momentum Using Python

In this activity, you will use Python as a physics calculator to determine velocity from known values of momentum and mass.
The result must be displayed using scientific notation with an appropriate number of significant figures.

Task: In a Python code cell, complete the following steps.

  1. Define the known quantities
    A cart has mass

    \[ m = 2.50\ \text{kg} \]

    and momentum

    \[ p_x = -15.0\ \text{kg·m/s}. \]

  2. Solve for the velocity
    Use

    \[ v_x = \frac{p_x}{m}. \]

  3. Print the velocity using scientific notation
    Format the output using two significant figures.

  4. Reflect

    • Why does the velocity carry a negative sign?

    • Why is two significant figures appropriate here?

A Possible Solution Guide

Method 1 — Direct calculation with formatted output

m = 2.50
p_x = -15.0

v_x = p_x / m

print(f"{v_x:.2e}")

Method 2 — Solving symbolically with SymPy

import sympy as sp

m, p_x, v_x = sp.symbols('m p_x v_x')

equation = sp.Eq(p_x, m * v_x)

equation_sub = equation.subs({m: 2.50, p_x: -15.0})

solution = sp.solve(equation_sub, v_x)[0]

print(f"{solution:.2e}")

Interpretation

The negative sign indicates motion in the negative \(x\)-direction.
The scientific-notation format makes both the scale and precision of the velocity explicit.

Key takeaway:
In one-dimensional motion, momentum sets both the magnitude and direction of velocity, and numerical results should always be reported with appropriate precision and formatting.
